# Day 1 — Why You're Even Reading This

> **The one idea for today:** I was you 10 years ago — 21, fresh out of NS, skeptical, with every option on the table. I didn't take the "safe" path. Today I'm writing these 14 days because I wish someone had written them for me.

## What you'll walk away with

By the end of today you should be able to:

1. **Name** the specific reason *you're* exploring this career — not a generic one.
2. **Separate** two things people always conflate: *low-risk* versus *low-ceiling*.
3. **Decide** whether to spend 15 minutes a day for the next 13 days to get a real answer.

---

## 1. I've been where you are

Hey. I'm Leo.

Fresh out of NS, no direction. Just told — like you probably were — that good grades and a "stable first job" was the way. I had the grades. Perfect A-levels from RJC. Scholarship options abroad. NUS Engineering ahead of me. Every door was open.

![Leo post-NS](/first-14-days/images/leo-post-ns.png)

But a question kept nagging me: **why should I wait 8 to 10 years to earn $10K a month?** What if I could start building that income *now*, as a student, without the luxury of time?

So I looked at entrepreneurship. And because finance interested me, I looked at financial advisory.

I was skeptical. Obviously. Same as you reading this.

*Is this legit? Can I even do well? Isn't there a stigma? Isn't the market saturated?*

![The skeptical moment](/first-14-days/images/leo-skeptical.png)

What pulled me through wasn't some pitch. It was a simple observation: **there was no risk or harm in just learning more about this career. And if it didn't work out, I could back out anytime.**

So I took the plunge. No system back then. No scripts, no mentors, no community. I had to build everything from scratch — while juggling NUS Engineering on the Global Engineering Program (cramming 4 years into 3).

Ironically, the lack of time is what forced the system. I had maybe 2–3 hours on weekday evenings. I couldn't afford to waste a single one. That pressure compounded into a machine that earned me **$145K in my first year as a full-time uni student** — and scaled past $400K post-graduation.

![My uni schedule](/first-14-days/images/leo-uni-schedule.png)

Today I've closed 1,002 clients, hit MDRT 5×, COT 3×, and built four other companies on top — including Singapore's #1 ranked marketing agency (50 staff), a corporate cleaning business ($15K/month profit), an outsourcing firm, and a consulting firm. Combined: 7-figure revenue.

The financial-advisory business is *still* the easiest, most scalable, lowest-capital, "done-for-you" business I've ever run. That's why I'm writing these 14 days.

## 2. You're not here because things are bad

Most people think candidates like you are unhappy in your current situation. That's rarely the truth.

More often — you're doing *fine*. The salary pays bills. The course load is manageable. Nothing is on fire.

But something is off. And you're reading this page because:

- You've looked at your 10-year trajectory and the numbers don't match the life you want
- You've watched a peer do something entrepreneurial and wondered *why not me*
- You've run the math on retiring your parents, and your current ceiling doesn't get there
- You want *control* over your time — not more time off, control

Caution brought you here. Caution didn't stop you from looking. That's a specific kind of person. It's the kind I actually want to have a conversation with.

---

## 3. What this 14-day read is, and is not

This is **not** a recruitment deck. There's no form at the end. No one is going to call you.

This is a structured 14-day reading path that answers two honest questions:

1. **Is a financial-advisory career structurally right for how I want to live?**
2. **If yes — is my team the right home for it? (And if not, what should you look for elsewhere?)**

By Day 14 you'll land on one of four outcomes:

- **Commit** — you're ready, and we'll book an onboarding call
- **Delay** — right model, wrong timing, let's set a revisit date
- **Keep Exploring** — this model, different agency — Day 8 gives you the criteria
- **Honest No** — this career isn't for you, and you've saved yourself a year

I designed it so the course can produce any of those four. A course that can only produce *yes* is a sales funnel. A course that can produce all four is an evaluation.

## 4. The 9-to-5 isn't the enemy — the *ceiling* is

Nothing wrong with a 9-to-5. Reliable income. Clear expectations. Someone else handles the infrastructure.

The problem is the ceiling.

- **Income scales linearly with promotions** — which happen every 2–4 years
- **Your time is not yours** — the calendar is set by someone else
- **Your earning cap is someone else's decision** — not yours
- **Your learning plateau arrives fast** — after year 3 or 4, you're not learning much new

You can trade a ceiling for uncertainty. You can trade certainty for a ceiling. What you cannot do is have both.

Financial advisory is one of a small handful of careers where **the ceiling moves when you move.** It's also one where the first 12 months are harder than a 9-to-5 — and the next 10 years are materially easier.

Whether that trade fits you depends on who you are. That's what the next 13 days will help you figure out.

## 5. Why I built FINternship

Once I hit a decade in this career, I made a decision.

In 2025, I'm stepping back from personal client acquisition. My time now goes to something different: **building and mentoring the next generation of advisors.** I want to create something like an elite sports team — high-performance individuals who step up, contribute, and push each other to grow.

That's the mission.

Unlike other paid programs where the goal is to extract a quick buck, **I will not ask you to pay me a single cent.** I won't force you to do anything you don't want to do.

Think of it this way: if you wanted to be a footballer, you'd join a football academy. Most academies make you pay to join. **We pay you to test your skills here** and build conviction in the career.

All I ask is your patience over the next few months as we build trust, competency, confidence, and conviction — and figure out together whether this is actually suitable for you.

In a way, the next few months are a self-guided, self-motivated mentorship. My goal: cut your learning curve short and remove as many friction points as possible between you and being successful here.

## 6. Why caution is a strength here

Most agencies recruit aggressively. They need volume. They'll tell you the career is easy, income comes fast, and your friends and family will carry you through year one.

None of that is quite honest:

- It's not easy. It's a real business being built from zero.
- Income ramps over months, not weeks.
- Warm market matters and helps your first few cases — but on its own it dries up. You need a cold-market engine running in parallel (Day 10 goes deep on this).

A candidate who reads for 14 days before deciding is the kind of candidate who's still in the career five years in. Agencies that want quick headcount don't like candidates like you. Agencies that want top producers — like me — actively look for you.

**Your caution is signal, not friction. Keep it.**

---

## Worksheet — your real *why*

Write the answer to one question. Short. Honest. Only for your own eyes.

**What would need to be true in 10 years for you to say *that was the right move*?**

Not a number. A picture. Where do you live? What does your Tuesday morning look like? What's your parents' situation? What would you be able to say no to?

Save this somewhere you'll re-read on Day 14. The gap between *that picture* and *your current 10-year trajectory* is the real question this course answers.

## Quiz

**Q1. What actually drives a cautious-but-curious person to explore this career is:**
- A) Their current job has become unbearable
- B) A gap between their current trajectory and where they want to be in 10 years ✓
- C) A sudden desire to escape adult responsibilities
- D) A friend in the industry pushing them to join

**Why:** Unhappy people rarely take action — they just stay unhappy. What produces serious exploration is the gap between *trajectory* and *destination*, not day-to-day dissatisfaction. It's a planning instinct, not fragility. That's the exact profile this program is built for.

**Q2. In this framing, the real friction with a 9-to-5 is not the job itself but:**
- A) The boss
- B) The hours
- C) The ceiling ✓
- D) The commute

**Why:** A 9-to-5 is a legitimate choice for many people. It becomes a bad fit when ambition, skills, or desired lifestyle exceed what the role can ever grow into. That's structural, not a complaint. The ceiling — not the day-to-day — is what drives this decision.

**Q3. The intended output of this 14-day course is:**
- A) An enthusiastic yes, every time
- B) A signed recruitment form by Day 14
- C) An honest decision — yes, delay, keep exploring, or no ✓
- D) A booked meeting with a senior advisor

**Why:** A course that can only produce *yes* is a sales funnel. A real evaluation produces any of four legitimate outcomes, and an honest *no* is a success — it saves everyone a year. Measure the course by whether it helped you decide honestly, not by whether it pushed any specific answer.
