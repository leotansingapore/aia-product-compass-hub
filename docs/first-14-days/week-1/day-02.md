# Day 2 — The Franchise Without the $200K Fee

> **The one idea for today:** A Ya Kun franchise is $200K upfront plus 10% royalty forever. A GP clinic is $650K with a 6-year break-even. This career gives you the same franchise structure — brand, systems, products, infrastructure — without the entry fee, without the royalty, and cash-flow positive from month one.

## What you'll walk away with

By the end of today you should be able to:

1. **Explain** why most traditional businesses fail on capital, and why the "safe" small-business options often just buy you a stressful job.
2. **Compare** a traditional franchise (Ya Kun, Subway) to the FA career on fee, royalty, risk, and time-to-profit.
3. **Identify** what's actually being handled for you — and what you're still responsible for.

---

## 1. The entrepreneur's dilemma — three real examples

I've personally looked at three small businesses over the years. Here's what they actually cost:

### Ya Kun Kaya Toast franchise

A business partner of mine was excited about this. His friend owned several profitable outlets. Looked like a sure bet.

The reality:
- **$200,000+ upfront**
- **~10% royalty on revenue — forever**
- Rent, staff, opening hours, supply chain — all on you
- The dream of "owning a cool café" collapsed the moment we ran the numbers

### GP clinic

I explored this with a friend.

- **$650,000 initial investment**
- Even at $1M revenue/year, net profit after rent/salaries ≈ **$10K/month**
- Break-even: **5–6 years** *if everything went perfectly*

### Pain clinic in a prestige mall

I looked at buying this one over.

- Revenue: $500K/year
- Owner's take-home: $80K
- Asking price: $500K — **over 6 years just to break even**
- The owner was running front desk, ops, sales *and* service delivery himself

If I'd bought it, I wouldn't have been a business owner. I'd have bought myself a very expensive, high-stress job.

**This is the entrepreneur's dilemma:** the path to freedom is blocked by either huge capital + years of risk, or trading one grind for a harder one.

You're right to hesitate. A lot of people don't.

---

## 2. What a franchise actually does for you

Take a step back. Why do McDonald's, Subway, Ya Kun franchises exist at all? Because they bypass the hardest parts of entrepreneurship.

A franchise gives you:

- **Recognised brand** — customers already trust you
- **Proven systems** — ops manuals, supply chains, training
- **Marketing infrastructure** — paid by the parent
- **R&D done for you** — you don't design the menu
- **Product and service built** — you execute

In return, you pay an enormous upfront fee and a royalty on revenue forever. Expensive — but *that's why franchisees succeed at much higher rates than independent startups.*

---

## 3. The FA career is a franchise — without the fee

Here's what most people don't realise. A modern tied-agency financial-advisory career is structurally identical to a franchise:

| Franchise (Ya Kun, Subway) | Financial Advisory (AIA) |
|---|---|
| Brand recognition | Yes — AIA is a trusted brand across Asia |
| Proven systems | Yes — scripts, processes, CRMs, training |
| Marketing infrastructure | Yes — brand spend, digital assets, lead platforms |
| R&D done for you | Yes — product design, actuarial, underwriting |
| Product/service built | Yes — policies, funds, claims handled centrally |
| Upfront fee | **No — $0** |
| Royalty on revenue | **No — you keep your commissions** |

![Franchise vs FA comparison](/first-14-days/images/franchise-comparison.webp)

You get the upside structure of a franchise. You don't pay the downside.

And better: **the risk is reversed.** In a traditional franchise, *you* pay the company upfront. In this model, the company (AIA) invests in *you* first — sponsored exam fees, allowance through the FLEXI scheme or EPS, subsidised leads, training. That's a business-in-a-box where the box pays *you* to open it.

---

## 4. "But isn't every MLM saying the same thing?"

Fair objection. Here's the real difference:

- **Regulated industry.** Financial advisors must pass licensing exams (M5, M9, M9A, HI, CMFAS) overseen by MAS (Monetary Authority of Singapore). Not just anyone gets in.
- **Real product with real economic value.** Insurance and investment products solve problems people actually have. Nobody *needs* another skincare line. Everyone needs a claims cheque if something goes wrong.
- **Income from clients, not recruits.** Your pay comes from the company for work you did for clients. It does not depend on getting other people to join.
- **Long client relationships.** A policy I sold 10 years ago still pays renewal income today. MLM churn is brutal.

This is a regulated, institutional-grade business. It just happens to be one you can build without a capital outlay.

---

## 5. Your role — driver, not engineer

A franchise owner doesn't design the menu. They don't run corporate marketing. They don't negotiate with suppliers at scale. **Their job is to operate well, locally, and consistently.**

Same here. The company has already done the expensive work — decades of underwriting, product design, brand building, R&D. Your job is not to invent an insurance company. Your job is to:

- Find the right people to talk to
- Understand their situation properly
- Recommend what fits from products that already exist
- Serve them well over the years so they stay, refer, and buy more over time

That is a craft. It takes training. It also has a ceiling **orders of magnitude higher** than most corporate roles — because you're compensated for the relationship, not the hours.

---

## 6. Cash-flow positive from month one

Here's a detail most candidates miss. This career is cash-flow positive from month one because:

1. **No upfront capital required** — unlike a traditional franchise
2. **No monthly overhead** — no rent, no staff, no inventory
3. **Company bears the risk** — sponsored exams, FLEXI allowance, lead subsidies
4. **Commissions flow immediately** on closed cases

You start earning from the first closed case without any prior expense to recoup. Break-even is **day one.** You don't wait 3–6 years like a traditional business.

We cover the FLEXI allowance scheme in detail on Day 13 — but in short: you can receive up to $4,200/month as a FINtern while you're still passing exams, before your first client.

---

## 7. So what's the catch?

Honest list:

- **Year 1 is harder than a 9-to-5.** You're building a client base from zero. Most of the ramp is in months 2–9.
- **Income ramps; it doesn't arrive.** First case might close in month 2; meaningful monthly income stabilises by months 6–9.
- **The agency you pick matters enormously.** A good one provides leads, training, systems. A bad one just signs you up and tells you to call your friends. Day 8 is entirely about this.
- **You have to actually be good at the craft.** No franchise fee, but no free ride either.

Knowing this, if the structural trade still makes sense — keep reading. Day 3 is about the skills you build along the way, which matter even if you decide in Year 2 that this isn't forever.

## Quiz

**Q1. The closest structural analogy to a tied-agency FA career is:**
- A) A 9-to-5 with commission on top
- B) A franchise — minus the entry fee and royalty ✓
- C) Freelance consulting
- D) An MLM that pays per recruit

**Why:** All four franchise ingredients are present for an FA — brand, systems, marketing, product — but the two ingredients that usually crush franchisees (upfront fee, royalty on revenue) are absent. MLMs fail the comparison because their economics depend on recruiting. Freelance consulting fails because there's no brand or systems to leverage.

**Q2. What most often blocks ambitious people from traditional entrepreneurship is:**
- A) A shortage of good ideas
- B) Upfront capital and multi-year break-even windows ✓
- C) Fear of success
- D) Not enough motivation

**Why:** Ya Kun is $200K plus a 10% royalty. A clinic is $650K and 5–6 years to break even. Buying a business is 6 years of recouping while doing every role yourself. The math — not motivation — is what keeps most people employed. Eliminate the math problem and a different decision becomes available.

**Q3. The FA career is cash-flow positive from month one primarily because:**
- A) AIA pays a starting salary for the first year
- B) There is no capital outlay and commissions pay out immediately ✓
- C) New advisors start with high-net-worth clients
- D) Insurance product margins are extremely high

**Why:** Cash-flow positive means income arrives before any capital needs to be recouped. No capital, no fixed overhead, immediate commissions, plus sponsored exam fees and allowance equals day-one positive. Compare that to a traditional franchise burning capital for 3+ years before break-even. That's structural asymmetry, not a product-margin story.
