# Day 11 — "I Have No Experience / I'm Too Introverted"

> **The one idea for today:** No experience is an advantage. Being introverted is an advantage. Gabriel Kow did $10K/month in his first month as an ORD NSF. Benjamin Gnoh did it in 2 months while entering NTU. Neither had experience. Neither was extroverted.

## What you'll walk away with

By the end of today you should be able to:

1. **Reframe** *no experience* from a liability into a training advantage.
2. **Understand** why introverts often outperform extroverts in relationship-based sales.
3. **See** real mentees of mine who had neither — and hit $10K/month in weeks.

---

## 1. "I have no experience in finance"

This is one of the most common disqualifying thoughts candidates have. It's also one of the least accurate.

Here's the honest picture. Most new advisors I've trained come from:

- Engineering
- Marketing
- Teaching
- Healthcare (nursing, allied health)
- Hospitality
- Corporate functions (HR, ops, finance)
- Fresh graduates with no work experience
- NSFs just out of national service
- University students still studying

**Almost none came in with a finance degree or prior experience at an insurer.** A finance degree is useful. It's not a prerequisite.

### Why no experience is actually an advantage

**1. Nothing to unlearn.** Candidates with financial backgrounds often come in with strong opinions about products, processes, and *"how things should be done."* Usually wrong for sales-side work — the dynamics of client-facing advisory are different from underwriting, back-office finance, or banking. Fresh candidates absorb correct habits faster.

**2. Fresh-eyes credibility.** Clients don't want someone who sounds like a textbook. They want someone who explains things in plain English, without industry jargon. Someone without years of jargon-immersion does this more naturally.

**3. No salary dependency anchor.** Career-switchers from high-salary finance roles often mentally anchor to a safe income they'd be giving up. Candidates from other industries — or fresh grads, NSFs, students — don't carry that weight. They're more willing to do the real work of the first 12 months.

### What actually matters more than experience

- Willingness to learn the products deeply (anyone motivated can do this in 3–6 months)
- Willingness to talk to strangers (harder than product knowledge)
- Willingness to handle rejection without collapse (the real hurdle)
- The 6 C's from Day 9

If you have the 6 C's, product knowledge is a few months of study. If you don't have the 6 C's, 10 years of industry experience won't save you.

## 4. "I'm too introverted"

Second most common disqualifier. Based on a total misunderstanding of what actually works in this career.

### The myth: extroverts dominate sales

Cultural image: charismatic, gregarious, high-energy extrovert sweeping clients off their feet with charm, closing through personality.

### The reality: top advisors *listen* more than they talk

Look at the actual pattern across high-producing advisors and you see something different:

- Many top producers are self-described introverts
- Many struggled with shyness or public speaking early on
- Some had genuine speech or confidence issues in their youth
- What they share isn't extroversion — it's **the ability to listen well and ask good questions**

### My own example

I'm an introvert by nature. I had a stammering problem growing up. I joined Toastmasters during NS specifically because I knew I needed to work on public speaking. I never enjoyed "working a room."

But I could listen. I could ask questions. I could sit in silence long enough for the real answer to surface. That turned out to be the skill that mattered.

Five-time MDRT. Three-time COT. Introvert. These are not contradictions — they're connected.

### Why listening wins

Clients don't want a performer. They want someone who understands them. Introverts are structurally good at this:

- Comfortable with silence (the most valuable 10 seconds in any client conversation)
- Ask more questions before speaking
- Less likely to over-sell
- Less likely to make the meeting about themselves
- Remember details about clients (because they actually listened)

### The silence test

Specific thing that matters disproportionately. In a good client conversation, the advisor asks a question — *"what's the biggest financial worry you have right now?"* — and then *waits in silence* while the client thinks.

The first answer is always surface-level. The *real* answer — the one that matters — comes 5–10 seconds later, after the silence.

Extroverts often jump in before the real answer surfaces. Introverts naturally wait. **The introvert hears the real answer. The extrovert never does.**

One of the most under-appreciated skills in the craft. Introverts have it by default.

## 5. What introverts need to work on (honest list)

Not a free pass:

- **Generating volume of opening conversations** (calling, outreach) — hardest for introverts, drains energy
- **Not disappearing after a full day of meetings** — intentional recovery time matters
- **Self-promotion** (content, personal brand) — introverts often under-do this

All three are trainable. None are deal-breakers. Our training materials specifically account for introverts.

---

## 6. The combined pattern — introvert + no experience

An introvert with no finance experience often assumes they're *doubly* disqualified. In fact, they may be the **most structurally well-suited candidate type.**

Why?

- **No experience** → fast to learn correct habits, no unlearning
- **Introverted** → natural listener, asks better questions, doesn't over-sell
- **Quiet confidence (if you have it)** → clients trust it more than showy confidence

Areas to work on — cold outreach volume, energy management, self-promotion — are skill-gaps, not character flaws. Trainable. The things that *can't* be easily trained — deep listening, patience, genuine curiosity, not over-selling — they already have.

Benjamin and Gabriel both fit this profile. Neither was a "natural salesperson." Both listened well. Both had the 6 C's. Both hit $10K/month in months, not years.

---

## 7. Introversion vs social anxiety — distinguish these

Honest caveat: if your introversion is closer to social anxiety — meaning calling strangers or having face-to-face conversations causes real distress — that's a different conversation.

Some honest signals:

- **Can you hold a 30-minute conversation with a stranger, socially, without major discomfort?** If yes, you're in range.
- **Do you avoid making phone calls to unfamiliar numbers?** If avoiding calls is a dealbreaker for you, first 6–12 months will be uncomfortable. (It gets much better once you build the reflex.)
- **Do you freeze when someone disagrees with you?** If so, handling objections will be a challenge. Trainable — but effortful.

Distinguish between **introversion** (prefer fewer, deeper interactions; drained by crowds) and **social anxiety** (fear of interaction that interferes with functioning). First is an asset here. Second is a genuine obstacle worth addressing before or during a career change.

## 8. The reframe

The fear of *"I don't have sales experience"* or *"I'm too introverted"* is usually a proxy for something deeper: *"I'm not sure I'm the type of person who can do this."*

That's a real concern. But it's the concern Day 9's 6 C's are designed to surface — not the experience or personality concern.

**The 6 C's (not experience, not personality type) are what predict success here.** Gabriel had them. Benjamin had them. Both hit $10K in the first 2 months. That's the receipt.
