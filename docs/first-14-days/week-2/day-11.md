# Day 11 — "I Have No Experience / I'm Too Introverted"

> **The one idea for today:** No experience is an advantage. Being introverted is an advantage. Gabriel Kow did $10K/month in his first month as an ORD NSF. Benjamin Gnoh did it in 2 months while entering NTU. Neither had experience. Neither was extroverted.

## What you'll walk away with

By the end of today you should be able to:

1. Reframe "no experience" from a liability into a training advantage.
2. See why introverts often do better than extroverts in relationship sales.
3. Meet two mentees of mine who had neither going for them and still hit $10K a month in weeks.

---

## 1. "I have no experience in finance"

This is one of the most common reasons people rule themselves out. It's also one of the least accurate.

Most new advisors I've trained come from:

- Engineering
- Marketing
- Teaching
- Healthcare (nursing, allied health)
- Hospitality
- Corporate functions (HR, ops, finance)
- Fresh graduates with no work experience
- NSFs just out of national service
- University students still studying

Almost none came in with a finance degree or prior experience at an insurer. A finance degree is useful. It's not a prerequisite.

### Why no experience is actually an advantage

Nothing to unlearn. Candidates who come from finance often arrive with strong opinions about products, processes, and "how things should be done." Those opinions are usually wrong for sales-side work, because client-facing advisory runs very differently from underwriting, back-office finance or banking. Fresh candidates pick up the right habits faster.

Fresh-eyes credibility. Clients don't want someone who sounds like a textbook. They want plain English, no jargon. Someone who hasn't been marinated in industry-speak for years does this more naturally.

No salary dependency anchor. Career-switchers from high-salary finance roles often mentally anchor to the safe income they'd be giving up. Candidates from other industries, or fresh grads, NSFs and students, don't carry that weight. They're more willing to do the real work of the first 12 months.

### What actually matters more than experience

- Willingness to learn the products deeply (anyone motivated gets there in 3 to 6 months)
- Willingness to talk to strangers (harder than product knowledge)
- Willingness to handle rejection without falling apart (the real hurdle)
- The 6 C's from Day 9

If you have the 6 C's, product knowledge is a few months of study. If you don't have them, 10 years of industry experience won't save you.

## 4. "I'm too introverted"

The second most common reason people rule themselves out. It's based on a misread of what actually works in this career.

### The myth: extroverts dominate sales

The cultural picture is the charismatic, gregarious, high-energy extrovert sweeping clients off their feet with charm and closing through personality.

### The reality: top advisors listen more than they talk

Look at the actual pattern across high-producing advisors and the picture is different. Many are self-described introverts. Plenty struggled with shyness or public speaking early on. Some had real speech or confidence issues growing up. What they share isn't extroversion. It's the ability to listen well and ask good questions.

### My own example

I'm an introvert by nature. I had a stammering problem as a kid. I joined Toastmasters during NS because I knew I needed to work on public speaking. I've never enjoyed "working a room."

But I could listen. I could ask questions. I could sit in silence long enough for the real answer to come out. That turned out to be the skill that mattered.

Five-time MDRT, three-time COT, still an introvert. Those aren't contradictions.

### Why listening wins

Clients don't want a performer. They want someone who understands them. Introverts tend to be good at this because they are comfortable with silence (the most valuable 10 seconds in any client conversation), ask more questions before speaking, are less likely to over-sell, are less likely to make the meeting about themselves, and remember details about clients because they actually listened.

### The silence test

One specific thing matters disproportionately. In a good client conversation the advisor asks a question, say *"what's the biggest financial worry you have right now?"*, and then waits in silence while the client thinks.

The first answer is always surface-level. The real answer, the one that matters, comes 5 to 10 seconds later, after the silence.

Extroverts often jump in before that real answer surfaces. Introverts naturally wait. The introvert hears the real answer. The extrovert often doesn't.

This is one of the most under-appreciated skills in the craft, and introverts get it for free.

## 5. What introverts need to work on (honest list)

This isn't a free pass. There are three real gaps:

- Generating volume on opening conversations (calling, outreach). Hardest for introverts, drains energy.
- Not disappearing after a full day of meetings. Intentional recovery time matters.
- Self-promotion, content, personal brand. Introverts tend to under-do this.

All three are trainable. None are deal-breakers. Our training materials account for this.

---

## 6. The combined pattern, introvert plus no experience

An introvert with no finance experience often assumes they're doubly disqualified. In practice they may be the most well-suited candidate type we see.

Here's why. No experience means fast to learn the right habits, with nothing to unlearn. Introverted means natural listener, asks better questions, doesn't over-sell. Quiet confidence (if you have it) tends to earn more trust from clients than the showy version.

The areas to work on, cold outreach volume, energy management, self-promotion, are skill gaps, not character flaws. All trainable. The things that are hard to train, deep listening, patience, genuine curiosity, not over-selling, they already have.

Benjamin and Gabriel both fit this profile. Neither was a "natural salesperson." Both listened well. Both had the 6 C's. Both hit $10K a month in months, not years.

---

## 7. Introversion vs social anxiety

One honest caveat. If your introversion is closer to social anxiety, meaning calling strangers or having face-to-face conversations causes real distress, that's a different conversation.

Some signals to check against:

- Can you hold a 30-minute conversation with a stranger, socially, without major discomfort? If yes, you're in range.
- Do you avoid phone calls to unfamiliar numbers? If that's a dealbreaker for you, the first 6 to 12 months will be uncomfortable. It gets much easier once you build the reflex.
- Do you freeze when someone disagrees with you? If so, handling objections will be a challenge. Trainable, but effortful.

Introversion (preferring fewer, deeper interactions and being drained by crowds) is different from social anxiety (fear of interaction that interferes with functioning). The first is an asset here. The second is a real obstacle worth addressing before or during a career change.

## 8. The reframe

The fear of *"I don't have sales experience"* or *"I'm too introverted"* is usually a proxy for something deeper: *"I'm not sure I'm the type of person who can do this."*

That's a real concern, and it's the one Day 9's 6 C's are designed to surface, not the experience or personality concern.

The 6 C's, not experience and not personality type, are what predict success here. Gabriel had them. Benjamin had them. Both hit $10K in the first 2 months. That's the receipt.
