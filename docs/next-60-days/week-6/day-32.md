# Day 32 — D Profile

> **The one idea for today:** With a D, cut to the chase in sentence one. Long intros kill the meeting before you get to the Fact-Find.

By the time you close today you'll spot a D profile in under 3 minutes using body language, dressing, word choice, and questions asked, engage a D with the 4 behaviours that land (confidence, options, overview, resourcefulness) and avoid the 4 that don't, and pitch a D using outcome-focused, concise language — 60-second version before any deep-dive is allowed.

---

## What the D is actually looking for

The D sits down in front of you thinking two things:

1. *"Does this person know what they're doing?"* (competence)
2. *"Are they going to waste my time?"* (efficiency)

Everything else is secondary. Warmth is nice but optional. Stories are noise unless they prove competence. Long build-ups feel like an advisor who's hiding something or doesn't know what to recommend.

**The D's time-to-patience graph is short.** You have roughly 2–3 minutes to establish competence and pace, or they mentally check out and the rest of the meeting is going through the motions.

---

## How to spot a D — the 5-signal scan

Within 5 minutes of sitting down, check:

| Signal | D tells |
|---|---|
| **Body** | Stands straight, firm footsteps, takes up space, makes direct eye contact, head-up when thinking |
| **Pace** | Fast — walks fast, talks fast, wants things done fast |
| **Questions** | Bottom-line questions: *"What do I actually need to do?"* *"How much, how long, what's the outcome?"* |
| **Words** | *"Cut to the chase."* *"What's the ROI?"* *"Let's move."* *"Don't waste time on details."* |
| **Dressing** | Red or black (power colours). Clean-cut. Expensive but not flashy. Efficiency-oriented. |

When you see 3+ of these, you're almost certainly with a D. Speed-match them — cut *your* intro, *your* pacing, *your* small talk in half.

---

## The 4 engagement behaviours that land

With a D, you win when:

### 1 · Be confident, super efficient, resourceful
- Arrive 5 minutes early
- Have the Fact-Find agenda printed or clearly on your iPad
- When they ask a question, *answer it.* Don't hedge. If you don't know, say *"I'll find out and get back to you by [specific day/time]"* — and then actually do

The D tolerates *not-knowing*; what they don't tolerate is *not-finding-out*.

### 2 · Make recommendations — don't tell them what they already know
The D doesn't want a 20-minute education on how insurance works. They want you to *recommend* what to do.

Bad: *"So there are three types of critical illness coverage — let me walk you through each…"*
Good: *"Based on what you've shared, I'd recommend A over B and here's why in one sentence. Want me to walk through the details, or do you want to just move on that?"*

### 3 · Give options, not ultimatums — especially on solutions, not problems
The D's need is *control.* Take that need seriously.

Bad: *"The only way to solve this is X."* (Triggers resistance even when X is correct.)
Good: *"There are two reasonable paths — A and B. Here's the tradeoff. Which fits your situation better?"*

### 4 · Show them overview — best and worst case, not just the middle
D's think in scenarios. Give them:
- Best case: *"If markets do +5%/yr, here's where you land."*
- Worst case: *"If markets crash 30% in year 2, here's the drawdown and the recovery window."*
- Realistic case: *"What we're planning for."*

The worst-case number is what earns their respect. D's expect you to *think in risk*.

---

## The 4 mistakes that lose a D

### 1 · Long intros and unnecessary rapport
Warming up a D feels like being polite. To them, it feels like an advisor stalling because they don't have a real answer. Cut the weather chat. Open with *"ready to get started?"* and get started.

### 2 · Hedging language
*"Maybe,"* *"it depends,"* *"we could consider…"* — reads as incompetence to a D. Even when you genuinely need to consider trade-offs, commit to a recommendation and *then* caveat.

Bad: *"We could consider A or maybe B, it depends on your situation…"*
Good: *"I'd go with A. The main tradeoff is [X], which matters if [Y] applies to you. Does it?"*

### 3 · Emotional / story-led pitches
Stories that work beautifully on an I land flat on a D. *"Let me tell you about a client who…"* gets a polite half-listen at best. Lead with numbers, recommendations, and outcome framing. Save stories for closing a specific objection with a relevant parallel.

### 4 · Not knowing when they've decided
D's decide fast — especially on big-picture items. If they say *"okay, let's do it"*, they've decided. Do NOT re-pitch, re-explain, or over-confirm. Move straight to logistics: *"Great. Here's what I need from you to set it up. How do you want to handle the payment — direct debit or cheque?"*

Re-pitching after a D has decided actively unsells the decision.

---

## D-profile blind spots (that hurt the meeting)

Four patterns D's bring that you'll have to navigate:

1. **They boss people around unknowingly.** Don't take it personally. That's just how they communicate.
2. **They can't stand being challenged directly.** If you disagree, frame it as *"one thing worth considering is…"* rather than *"actually, that's wrong."*
3. **They don't get why others aren't as competent as they are.** If you need time to work something out, explain *why* — D's respect process when you name the reason.
4. **When they explode, it's loud.** It almost never lands on you personally — it's usually about the thing they're impatient with. Don't match their volume. Stay calm, stay measured, and the explosion passes.

---

## Common D objections — and the register that lands

A D's objections are sharper, faster, and more direct than other profiles. Three common ones:

### *"This is taking too long."*
**Bad:** apologise and keep going at the same pace.
**Good:** *"Got it. Let me cut to the three things that matter most for you: [X, Y, Z]. Anything else we can handle over email."*

### *"What's the bottom line?"*
**Bad:** *"Well, there are several factors to consider…"*
**Good:** *"Bottom line: $450/month gets you the coverage + accumulation we need. Three-year commitment. That's the deal. Want to go ahead or want me to explain why each piece is there?"*

### *"I don't make decisions on the spot."* (rare for a D, but can happen)
**Bad:** push harder.
**Good:** *"Understood — makes sense. What do you need to see before Thursday to be able to decide?"* — gives them control (their need) while setting a timeline.

---

## The D-tailored pitch — same plan, different framing

Same recommendation ($500K CI + $500/month accumulation). For a D, the opener is bottom-line-first.

### D opening — 90 seconds

> *"Based on what you've shared — two kids, primary earner, tight-ish cashflow — I'd recommend CI of $500K + a $500/month accumulation plan. That handles your biggest risk and builds the buffer you said you need. Three decisions: (1) go ahead, (2) tweak the CI amount, (3) rethink the accumulation piece. Which one?"*

Recommendation upfront. Options for control (their core need). Direct ask. No story, no warm-up.

### D close — Assumptive

Once a D has nodded agreement, skip the *"will you?"* beat. Move to logistics:

> *"Since you have two girls, I'll set up plans for both of them, correct?"*
> *"Tuesday 7pm — same venue — for the signing?"*
> *"Credit card or bank transfer?"*

**Why it fits:** D's expect decisive momentum. Re-pitching after they've decided *unsells* the decision. Assumptive language matches their pace and treats the decision as made.

**Never use:** Procedural (feels slow), long Reassurance (they read it as you hedging), Follow-up (only as last resort — D's decide in-meeting if you pitched right).

## Quiz

**Q1. With a D profile, the 5-minute scan should look for:**
- A) Warm smile, open body language, story-telling
- B) Direct eye contact, fast pace, bottom-line questions, efficiency-oriented dressing ✓
- C) Frowning when thinking, process-oriented questions, detailed note-taking
- D) Shy posture, soft voice, agreeable answers

**Why:** A describes an I. C describes a C. D describes an S. The D signals are speed, directness, and outcome-focus. You'll notice them almost immediately if you're looking — they walk in with momentum, they ask *"so what do we need to decide today?"* in the first 2 minutes, they wear clean no-nonsense clothes.

**Q2. The worst move you can make with a D after they've said *"okay, let's do it"* is:**
- A) Move to logistics immediately
- B) Re-pitch or re-explain why the decision is a good one ✓
- C) Thank them
- D) Send the paperwork that evening

**Why:** D's decide fast and expect you to respect the decision. Re-pitching communicates *"are you sure?"* which plants doubt where there wasn't any. It can actively unsell a decision. The correct move is to *move to logistics* — *"Great. Here's what I need from you…"* — which treats the decision as made and keeps the momentum.

**Q3. A D says *"this is taking too long."* The correct response is:**
- A) Apologise and keep going at the same pace
- B) Cut to the 3 things that matter most and defer the rest to email ✓
- C) Explain why thoroughness matters
- D) Offer to reschedule

**Why:** A ignores the signal. C is advisor-ego defending their process, which the D doesn't care about. D punts the meeting entirely, which kills momentum. B respects the signal by *compressing* — acknowledges the feedback, shrinks the remaining agenda to what matters most, and offers a fallback for the rest. That's the pacing move a D respects.

**Q4. A D's time-to-patience is short. Within the first few minutes of a meeting, the D is evaluating:**
- A) How much you'd charge per hour
- B) *"Does this person know what they're doing?"* and *"Are they going to waste my time?"* ✓
- C) Whether you'd be a good friend
- D) Your family background

**Why:** The D's first-meeting filter is competence and efficiency. Warmth is optional. Stories are noise unless they prove expertise. If the opening 2–3 minutes don't establish both, the D mentally checks out and you spend the rest of the meeting going through the motions. Hitting both filters fast is the price of admission.

**Q5. Hedging language fails with a D because:**
- A) D's dislike the word "maybe" specifically
- B) *"Maybe,"* *"it depends,"* *"we could consider…"* reads as incompetence — even when you genuinely need to consider tradeoffs, a D wants a recommendation first, then the caveat ✓
- C) D's take it personally
- D) It's considered bad manners

**Why:** D's don't want your indecision — they want your recommendation, delivered with confidence, even if it comes with tradeoffs. *"I'd go with A. The main tradeoff is X, which matters if Y applies"* respects their need for a clear call while still acknowledging nuance. *"Maybe A or maybe B, depends on situation"* reads as *"you don't actually know"* — which loses trust fast.

**Q6. With a D, presenting overview should include:**
- A) Just the base case
- B) Best case AND worst case AND realistic case — the worst-case number earns their respect ✓
- C) Only the best case
- D) A single number with no context

**Why:** D's think in scenarios. Presenting only the base case reads as incomplete thinking. Presenting worst case *first* (*"if markets drop 30%, here's the drawdown and recovery window"*) signals you've already stress-tested the plan — which D's interpret as the mark of a competent advisor. Advisors who avoid worst case because *"it's negative"* fail the D filter.

**Q7. A D explodes in volume mid-meeting about something frustrating. The correct move is:**
- A) Match their volume — show you can stand your ground
- B) Stay calm, stay measured — the explosion almost never lands on you personally and usually passes ✓
- C) End the meeting
- D) Apologise profusely

**Why:** D explosions are almost always about the thing they're impatient with — a policy, a delay, a bureaucratic obstacle — not about you. Matching their volume escalates. Staying calm and measured lets the storm pass, and the D then often snaps back to normal and moves on as if nothing happened. Apologising if you didn't cause it (D) signals weakness and damages the trust you've built.
