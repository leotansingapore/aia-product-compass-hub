# Day 35 — C Profile

> **The one idea for today:** With a C, the pitch isn't story — it's a spreadsheet you can defend line by line. Every claim needs to withstand scrutiny.

By the time you close today you'll spot a C profile through their questions, pace, and analytical posture, engage a C with the 4 behaviours that land (professional objectivity, value-for-money framing, worst-case scenarios, data) and avoid the 4 that flatline, and close a C through evidence and time — not through pressure or emotion.

---

## What the C is actually looking for

A C sits down across from you thinking:

1. *"Are the facts in this advisor's presentation correct?"*
2. *"Is this plan the best value — or am I being oversold?"*
3. *"What could go wrong, and what's the plan if it does?"*

Unlike D's (outcomes), I's (connection), or S's (safety), the C wants *rigor.* They'll push back on every number, cross-check everything against their own research, and take longer than any other profile to decide.

**The C is the profile most likely to do more research *after* your meeting than you did *before* it.** If they catch you with a factual error or inconsistency, you lose the trust — possibly permanently.

The good news: once a C is convinced, they're *extremely* loyal and become excellent referrers (to other C's, which is a gold-standard referral).

---

## How to spot a C — the 5-signal scan

| Signal | C tells |
|---|---|
| **Body** | Frowns when thinking. Poker expression, even at your jokes. Long pauses before answering. Cold / disconnected when focused. |
| **Pace** | Slow and deliberate. Pauses to consider. Rarely interrupts. |
| **Questions** | Process-related *why* questions: *"Why this algorithm?"* *"Why this timeline?"* *"How can you be sure?"* *"What's the track record?"* |
| **Words** | *"Too good to be true."* *"I don't make decisions on the spot."* *"Let's look at past track record."* *"Let's play safe."* *"I need to compare."* |
| **Dressing** | Blue or black (practical, easy to match). Clean, functional, unflashy. Minimal statement. |

When you see 3+ of these, you're with a C. Slow your pace, drop the stories, and prepare for the Q&A to outlast the presentation.

## The 4 engagement behaviours that land

### 1 · Be professional and objective — punctuality, intent statement, structure

The C notices the *meta-signals.* Did you arrive on time? Did you frame the meeting upfront? Do you seem organised? Do your recommendations stem from a process or from a gut feel?

The intent statement (from Day 7) matters more for C's than any other profile. It signals that you have a *process* — which is the C's native language.

- Arrive 5 min early
- Open with a short, structured intent statement
- Name the agenda before diving in
- Use consistent framing throughout — *"we're now moving to the proposal section"*

### 2 · Frame everything as value-for-money

C's don't care about feeling good about the plan. They care whether it's the *best decision financially.* Frame recommendations in that register:

Bad: *"This plan gives you peace of mind."*
Good: *"Dollar-for-dollar, this plan delivers $2.3M of coverage for the $430/month commitment. The closest equivalent on the market gives you $1.9M for $460/month. Here's the 5-year and 10-year comparison side by side."*

Specific numbers. Competitive comparison. Math the C can replicate.

### 3 · Lead with the worst-case scenario

C's look for *what could go wrong* before they look for *what could go right.* Pre-empt them.

Good: *"Let's start with the worst case. If markets drop 30% in year 2, here's exactly what happens to your portfolio and your coverage. In that scenario, you can still access [X] and here's the recovery timeline. Now let's look at the base case."*

Leading with worst-case earns their respect because it signals *you've thought about the downside so they don't have to catch you missing it.*

### 4 · Give data, facts, statistics, comparisons — details

Where an I tunes out at 2 minutes of data, a C *lights up*. Give them:
- Detailed coverage tables
- Year-by-year projections
- Historical performance data
- Side-by-side comparisons with alternatives
- Assumptions stated explicitly (*"assuming 4% inflation, 6% market return net of fees"*)

Bring printed spreadsheets. Bring detailed documentation. Offer to email the underlying models so they can check the math themselves. *That's exactly what earns their trust.*

## The 4 mistakes that lose a C

### 1 · Hedging or unsupported claims
Saying *"this is the best plan"* without supporting data gets you a cross-examination. Saying *"this has X benefit"* without a numeric basis invites *"how do you know?"*

Rule: every claim stated in a C meeting must have a supporting data source — a table, a statistic, a specific case. If you don't have it, say: *"I don't have that data on me — I'll email it by Monday."* Don't improvise.

### 2 · Emotional pitches
Stories land for I's. Emotion lands for S's. With a C, they feel like manipulation attempts. The C mentally flags *"this person is trying to bypass my analysis with feeling"* and trust drops.

Keep the pitch factual, even when you're tempted to add a story. The exception: a *case study* with specific numbers (*"Client A, age 42, same portfolio shape, here's what happened"*) — that works because it's a data point, not an emotional appeal.

### 3 · Pressure to decide
C's don't decide on the spot. They need time to research, compare, and think. Pushing a C to decide today produces either:

- Soft agreement that unwinds when they review the math later
- Immediate disqualification — *"this guy is pushy, which means the plan is probably not great"*

The discipline: *never ask a C to commit in-meeting.* Book the follow-up 7–14 days out, let them research, and close on the second meeting. C's close *on second or third meetings*, not the first.

### 4 · Getting a detail wrong and not catching it yourself
One wrong fact in a C meeting destroys trust disproportionately. The C wonders *"what else did they get wrong that I didn't catch?"*

If you notice an error mid-meeting, name it immediately: *"Actually, correction on what I said earlier — the figure is $1,240, not $1,340. Let me recheck the rest."* That honesty *rebuilds* trust. Silence on a noticed error loses it permanently.

## C-profile blind spots

Seven patterns C's bring:

1. **Mono-perspective** — they see their analysis as *the* answer. You may need to surface alternative framings.
2. **Listen to reply, not to understand** — they're often planning their next question while you're talking. Check understanding.
3. **Need to be right** — if you contradict them, do it gently and with data.
4. **Self-righteousness** — can justify their own hesitation as rigour. Sometimes you have to gently challenge: *"I hear that you want more data — what would be enough? Because some information we'll only get by starting."*
5. **Pessimism as *a wet blanket*** — they're not trying to be negative, but they often sound it. Don't take it personally.
6. **Territorial on their own domain** — if they're an engineer, they *know* engineering math. Respect that.
7. **Affect blindness** — they often miss emotional signals. Don't assume they noticed your tonal shift; they probably didn't.

## Common C objections — and the register that lands

### *"Let me compare with the market."*
Expected and correct. Don't fight it.
**Bad:** *"I assure you this is the best option available."*
**Good:** *"Absolutely — and that's the responsible move. Can I make it easier? Here's a side-by-side with the 3 closest competing products. I've included the pros of each — I'm genuinely fine if one of them fits you better. Which ones should we get quotes on so we can finalise next meeting?"*

Lean into their process. Offering to help them compare *increases* the close rate — because it signals you're not hiding weaknesses.

### *"What if [specific edge case]?"*
C's love edge cases.
**Bad:** *"That's unlikely to happen."*
**Good:** *"Good question — let me walk you through exactly what happens in that scenario."* Then do it precisely, with the policy document open. If you don't know, *"I don't know offhand but I'll get you the exact clause by Monday"* — and deliver by Monday.

### *"I'll think about it."*
Expected. C's don't decide in-meeting.
**Bad:** pressure them to commit today.
**Good:** *"Completely expected. A few things that might help: (1) here's the one-page summary you can review, (2) I've emailed you the detailed spreadsheet, (3) let's book Thursday 7pm for our follow-up — if you have questions by then we can discuss, and if not we can move forward. Sound right?"* Two weeks of digestion time + a booked follow-up = C's default happy path.

## The C-tailored pitch — same plan, different framing

Same recommendation ($500K CI + $500/month accumulation). For a C, the opener is structure-first.

### C opening — 90 seconds

> *"Here's the agenda for this 45 minutes. (1) I'll walk through my recommendation in the first 10 minutes. (2) Then 15 minutes on how I arrived at it — the math, assumptions, alternatives I considered. (3) Last 20 minutes is Q&A — I've prepared a comparison table with three alternative products so we can stress-test. My recommendation is CI $500K plus a $500/month accumulation plan, and I've flagged two assumptions that may or may not hold for you. Shall we start?"*

Structured. Data-forward. Assumptions named upfront. Built-in comparison. C's respect the meta-signal of an organised process as much as the content itself.

### C close — Procedural + Follow-up

> *"Completely expected that you'd want to review before deciding. Here's what I'm sending over: (1) the one-page summary, (2) the detailed spreadsheet with my assumptions called out, (3) comparison against the three alternatives I mentioned. Let's book Thursday 7pm for the follow-up. If you have questions by then we'll work through them; if the math checks out we proceed. Sound right?"*

**Why it fits:** C's genuinely need time to research, compare, verify. Procedural gives them the process; Follow-up books a specific decision point 7–14 days out. Facilitates their analysis instead of fighting it.

**Never use:** Assumptive (feels pushy, triggers re-analysis from zero — often loses the deal permanently). Avoid urgency framing (*"sign today or lose the rate"*) — C's read urgency as sloppy.

## Quiz

**Q1. With a C profile, the best opener is:**
- A) A warm story about a client
- B) *"Let me start with the worst case — here's what happens if the market drops 30%…"* ✓
- C) *"Trust me lah, this is the best plan out there"*
- D) *"Let me tell you why I love this product"*

**Why:** C's evaluate downside first. Leading with the worst case earns their respect because it signals you've already thought about what could go wrong — so they don't have to catch you missing it. A would flatline; C would be trust-damaging; D is advisor-ego and reads as unsupported. B is the exact move that earns a C's trust in the first 60 seconds.

**Q2. If you realise mid-meeting that you misstated a fact to a C, you should:**
- A) Hope they didn't notice and move on
- B) Name it immediately: *"Correction on what I said earlier — the figure is X, not Y"* ✓
- C) Wait until the end to clarify
- D) Email the correction later

**Why:** C's notice everything and take notes. Silence on an error means they'll either catch it themselves (trust destroyed) or compound doubt about everything else you said. Naming the error immediately *rebuilds* trust — it signals rigour and honesty, both of which are C core values. Waiting (C) or hoping (A) is always worse than immediate correction.

**Q3. A C says *"I need to compare with the market."* The correct response is:**
- A) *"I assure you this is the best option available"*
- B) *"Let me help — here's a side-by-side with the 3 closest competing products"* ✓
- C) *"We can talk about that next meeting"*
- D) Push them to decide today anyway

**Why:** Fighting a C's comparison instinct is a losing battle — they'll compare whether you help or not. Offering to provide the side-by-side has two effects: it signals confidence in your product (no hiding), and it positions you as *helping* their process rather than *fighting* it. C's who feel helped through their analysis close at far higher rates than C's who feel their process is being skipped.

**Q4. C's typically close:**
- A) On the first meeting
- B) On the second or third meeting after they've researched, compared, and thought ✓
- C) Within 20 minutes
- D) Only through referrals

**Why:** Expecting a C to decide in-meeting produces soft agreements that unwind when they review the math at home. C's need time to cross-check, think, and compare. Book the follow-up 7–14 days out, let them do their process, and close on the second meeting. Pushing for today (A) is the fastest way to lose a C — they'll either say no or say yes and cancel.

**Q5. Emotional pitches and stories land beautifully with I's and ok-ish with S's. With a C, they:**
- A) Work the same as with I's
- B) Feel like manipulation attempts — the C flags *"this person is trying to bypass my analysis with feeling"* and trust drops ✓
- C) Are required for compliance
- D) Help close faster

**Why:** C's evaluate rigour first. A story without supporting numbers reads as a trick — the feeling equivalent of a polished pitch with no data. The exception is a case study with specific numbers (*"Client A, age 42, same portfolio shape, here's what happened"*) — which works because it's a data point wearing narrative clothes. General feel-good stories are a net loss with a C.

**Q6. In a C meeting, every claim you make:**
- A) Should be delivered with confidence regardless of data backing
- B) Needs a supporting data source — a table, a statistic, a specific case. If you don't have it, honestly defer ("I'll email by Monday") ✓
- C) Can be improvised for emphasis
- D) Should be memorised word-for-word

**Why:** C's cross-check claims. A statement without evidence invites *"how do you know?"* If you don't have the backing data, improvising (C) gets caught. Hedging with *"I think it's around X"* reads as unprepared. The honest *"I don't have that on me — I'll email by Monday"* and then actually delivering Monday builds more trust than any improvised answer, because it signals rigour and reliability.

**Q7. A C is pessimistic / wet-blanket in tone. The correct interpretation is:**
- A) They're not interested
- B) They're not trying to be negative — pessimism is a C stance, often mistaken for disengagement; don't take it personally ✓
- C) They're testing you
- D) They'll never buy

**Why:** The C's analytical posture surfaces downsides first. That sounds pessimistic but is actually their engagement mode — they wouldn't bother noting risks if they weren't seriously considering the plan. Treating the tone as rejection (A, D) ends conversations that were about to convert. Recognising the tone as native to the profile and continuing to supply rigour is what gets a C across the line.
