# Day 59 — Reviewing Your 60-Day Numbers

> **The one idea for today:** 60 days in, the numbers tell the truth. Read them honestly — because what you measure now shapes what you build next.

By the time you close today you'll pull your actual 60-day numbers across CAR + FYC + pipeline + referrals, compare against the Week-4 projections you set and diagnose the gap honestly, and identify the 1 metric that most needs to move in Month 3 — with a specific intervention committed to.

---

## The 60-day reckoning

You started this module 60 days ago with $0 FYC and a plan. Today is the honest accounting. Not *"how did it feel?"* — *what are the actual numbers?*

The reason today matters: without an honest read, Month 3 planning runs on narrative instead of data. New FCs who tell themselves *"Month 2 was rough but Month 3 will be better"* without diagnosing *what specifically needs to be different* tend to have the same Month 3.

The diagnosis today is the foundation of the Day-60 12-month plan tomorrow.

## Compare to the Week-4 projections

Open your Day 2 and Day 3 work. What did you project 60 days ago?

- **Projected weekly appointments:** ___ → **actual:** ___
- **Projected close rate:** __% → **actual:** __%
- **Projected case size:** $___ → **actual:** $___
- **Projected 60-day FYC:** $___ → **actual:** $___

The gap between projection and actual is the data. It's almost always in one of 3 directions:

### Pattern 1 — projections were optimistic across the board
Most common for new FCs. You projected 7 appointments/week; actual was 3. You projected 40% close rate; actual was 20%. You projected $750 case size; actual was $500.

**This is normal.** Year-1 projections tend to be optimistic. The learning: recalibrate expectations to real market feedback, not initial ambition.

### Pattern 2 — one metric is much weaker than the others
Appointments on target, Fact-Finds fine, but closes are low (close rate issue). Or appointments low but conversion is excellent (volume issue, not skill issue).

**Best-case diagnosis.** A single broken link is much easier to fix than a system-wide weakness.

### Pattern 3 — Week-4 projections were already too conservative
Less common but real. You under-projected because you didn't yet know what was possible. Actuals exceed plan.

**Re-project upward for Month 3.** Don't anchor to a conservative baseline that understates your trajectory.

## The CAR diagnostic — 60-day version

Apply the CAR framework (Day 3 / Day 23 / Day 29) to the full 60-day window:

### Calls health
- **Sustained weekly calls >= your target?** → Calls healthy
- **Average below target?** → Calls are the bottleneck, even before you look at downstream

### Appointment health
- **Calls → Appointments conversion >= 25%?** → Appointment-setting is working
- **Conversion below 20%?** → Script / targeting issue (script failing to convert warm opens to bookings)

### Referral health
- **Received 1+ warm referral per closed case?** → Referral engine is starting to turn
- **Zero referrals?** → Ask isn't happening, or ask is flatlining (Day 27)

**Pick the weakest link. That's Month 3's focus.** The CAR diagnostic's whole value is forcing you to *pick one* rather than spreading effort thin across all three.

## The close-rate diagnostic

If closes are the weakness, diagnose sub-step:

### Sub-step 1 — are Fact-Finds producing qualified pitches?
If most Fact-Finds don't progress to pitch, the Fact-Find isn't surfacing hot buttons. Revisit Day 40 (Hot Buttons II) and Day 44 (silence).

### Sub-step 2 — are pitches producing trial-close commitment?
If prospects say yes in the meeting but don't follow through, the close technique may not fit the profile. Revisit Day 38 (profile × close matrix).

### Sub-step 3 — are objections killing closes?
If prospects object and the conversation ends, objection handling is the gap. Revisit Day 52–53.

### Sub-step 4 — are deferred decisions never returning?
If *"let me think"* becomes *never hear from them again*, the follow-up close is missing. Revisit Day 38 (Follow-up Close) and Day 17 (CRAB).

Pinpoint which sub-step leaks most. That's where Month 3's re-drilling should focus.

## The honest self-questions

Beyond numbers, four honest questions:

### 1 · Did I actually do the practice submissions each week?
Check the ✅ / ❌ column from Section 2. Each missed submission represents a muscle that wasn't built. Identify the weakest week — that curriculum material bears re-reading.

### 2 · Where's the time actually going?
Audit Week 10 so far. New business % vs relationship % vs systems %. If systems is > 25%, you're hiding (Day 58 failure 3).

### 3 · Which clients (or prospects) do I dread?
The prospects you dread are usually the ones where you're pitching against your own profile or against a hot-button you haven't surfaced. That's not a sign to drop them — it's a sign to diagnose what's specifically uncomfortable.

### 4 · If I'm honest, am I avoiding something?
Most new-FC plateaus have a specific avoidance at their core. Calling specific uncomfortable prospects. Asking for referrals. Handling a specific objection type. Name the avoidance and the path forward clarifies.

---

## Month 3 commitment — pick one thing

Out of all this diagnosis, pick **one thing** to change in Month 3. Not 10. One.

Examples of a good *one thing*:
- *"I'll hit 40 calls per week, every week, protected Monday morning block."*
- *"I'll ask for referrals on every single Fact-Find, using FACT Method verbatim until it's reflex."*
- *"I'll re-record my objection drills for *too expensive* and *let me think* until the technique is clean."*
- *"I'll hold 8 seconds of silence after every loaded Fact-Find question, tracked daily."*

Specific. Measurable. Observable in 4 weeks. *One* thing.

The discipline of narrowing to one is what produces actual change. *"I'll improve prospecting and closing and referrals and content"* produces nothing.

## Quiz

**Q1. The core reason to do the honest 60-day numerical review is:**
- A) To celebrate or mourn the numbers
- B) Month 3 planning needs to run on *data*, not *narrative* — without honest diagnosis, the same patterns repeat ✓
- C) To submit to your mentor
- D) It's required by compliance

**Why:** Without the numerical review, Month 3 becomes *"it'll be better because I'll try harder"* — which produces the same outcomes. Numbers tell you which specific link is broken (Calls, Appointments, Closes, or Referrals), which sub-step inside the broken link leaks, and what the intervention should be. Narrative planning is optimism; data-driven planning is targeted correction.

**Q2. The CAR diagnostic's core discipline is:**
- A) Track all three every week
- B) Pick *one* weakest link and target the Month-3 intervention there, rather than spreading effort across all three ✓
- C) Focus on Calls always
- D) Focus on Referrals always

**Why:** Spreading effort across multiple levers simultaneously produces small movement everywhere and meaningful movement nowhere. The CAR diagnostic forces narrowing — find the single weakest link and fix *that*. Once that link is healthy, the weakest link shifts to something else and you target that. This sequential fixing produces much faster compound improvement than parallel effort.

**Q3. A useful *one thing* commitment for Month 3 is:**
- A) *"I'll improve overall performance"*
- B) *"I'll work harder"*
- C) *"I'll hold 8 seconds of silence after every loaded Fact-Find question, tracked daily"* ✓
- D) *"I'll think about prospecting more"*

**Why:** Specific, measurable, observable in 4 weeks, tied to a known gap — that's what produces actual change. A, B, and D are narrative — they feel like commitments but produce no behavioural change because they're not specific enough to execute on or measure. The *one thing* principle protects against scattered effort: pick a narrow, testable behaviour and drill it hard for a month. Narrow and deep beats broad and shallow.

**Q4. When pulling your 60-day numbers, the rule is:**
- A) Round up anything close to the target
- B) Literally fill them in, even if small — small honest numbers beat large inflated ones because the diagnosis depends on them ✓
- C) Only count successes
- D) Only count failures

**Why:** Inflated numbers produce wrong diagnoses, which produce wrong Month-3 interventions, which produce wrong Month-6 results. Honest numbers — even ugly ones — point to the correct intervention. A 60-day FYC of $3K with low appointment volume is a different diagnosis than a 60-day FYC of $3K with high appointment volume. Rounding up obscures the difference.

**Q5. Pattern 2 (one metric is much weaker than the others) is the "best-case" diagnosis because:**
- A) It means you're already succeeding
- B) A single broken link is much easier to fix than system-wide weakness — you target one intervention, not rebuild from scratch ✓
- C) It always resolves itself
- D) It means the other links are compensating

**Why:** A system with one weak link needs one fix; a system with everything weak needs a rebuild. The pattern-2 diagnosis (e.g., *"appointments healthy, close rate low"*) is actionable — you focus Month 3 on close-rate-specific work (Day 52–53 objection handling, Day 44 silence discipline, Day 38 close techniques). Pattern 1 (all metrics optimistic vs actual) requires broader recalibration. Pattern 2 is the fastest path to Month 6 improvement.

**Q6. The "one thing" commitment rule for Month 3 exists because:**
- A) More than one is illegal
- B) Narrow and deep beats broad and shallow — trying to improve 5 things simultaneously produces small movement everywhere and meaningful movement nowhere ✓
- C) It's easier to track
- D) Mentors require it

**Why:** Behavioural change research consistently shows that focused single-target intervention produces faster improvement than distributed multi-target effort. *"Improve overall performance"* is narrative — it doesn't map to any specific behaviour. *"Hold 8 seconds of silence after every loaded Fact-Find question, tracked daily"* is a single observable behaviour that compounds into close-rate lift. The discipline of narrowing is what makes Month-3 actually different from Month 1–2.

**Q7. Which is a sign you're avoiding the real work?**
- A) Systems work (admin, CE, planning) is >25% of the week
- B) You keep rescheduling specific uncomfortable prospects
- C) You're drafting the perfect Fact-Find form for the 5th time
- D) All of the above ✓

**Why:** Each is a flavour of the same avoidance pattern — doing comfortable work (planning, rescheduling, perfecting documents) to avoid uncomfortable work (calling hesitant prospects, handling objections, closing). The Year-1 plateau diagnostic: when the avoided behaviour keeps getting pushed to *"next week,"* the system hasn't failed — the avoidance is running it. Name the avoidance honestly, then schedule the uncomfortable work into a protected block before the comfortable work gets to expand.
