# Day 43 — The Power Question

> **The one idea for today:** Making a hundred statements is never as powerful as asking one right question.

By the time you close today you'll apply the 6 Golden Rules of asking questions (keyword recall, logical possibilities, contrast, distinct options, non-committal words, authority quoting), test any question with the 3-point checklist (leads to objective · specific · logical and indisputable), and swap your 3 most common statements for questions that get the prospect to arrive at the conclusion themselves.

---

## Why statements underperform questions

A statement:
> *"Insurance is important because it protects your family."*

The prospect hears: *"Here's what you should believe."* They either already agree (in which case the statement is wasted) or they disagree (in which case you have to argue them out of their position, which almost never works).

Same idea, as a question:
> *"If something were to happen to you tomorrow — bluntly — who would be financially affected, and by how much?"*

The prospect has to *think*. They picture a specific scenario. They land on their own answer. You didn't tell them anything — they told themselves.

**That's the whole principle.** People defend statements other people make. They own conclusions they reach themselves. Questions route prospects to their own conclusions.

---

## What the right question does (10 jobs)

A well-constructed question can do any of these jobs:

1. **Open the mind / trigger interest** — breaks the default script
2. **Identify priorities** — surfaces what actually matters
3. **Identify the underlying objection** — gets past surface phrasing
4. **Overcome an objection** — routes around a block
5. **Motivate action** — surfaces the consequence of inaction
6. **Get agreement** — builds a chain of yeses
7. **Generate positive response** — warms the conversation
8. **Shift attention** — redirects focus to what matters
9. **Get the prospect to self-reflect** — their own data becomes the proof
10. **Test the close / trial close** — surfaces readiness without commitment

Statements do at most 1–2 of these. Questions do up to 10. This is why question-heavy Fact-Finds convert at dramatically higher rates than statement-heavy ones.

## The 6 Golden Rules

### Rule 1 — Focus on the keyword, include it in your next question

The prospect just said something. Echo their word back in your next question.

> *Prospect:* *"Bro, honestly I don't believe in insurance. I think it's a scam."*
> *You:* *"If you don't mind me asking — why don't you believe in insurance?"* or *"Thanks for letting me know. May I know the reason why?"*

Repeating their word (*believe*, *scam*) signals you heard them. Forces them to elaborate on their own position — which almost always softens it.

### Rule 2 — Offer logical possibilities to help them answer

If the prospect is hedging, give them a menu of possible answers.

> *Prospect:* *"Let me think about it."*
> *You:* *"Sure — may I ask which aspect you need to think more about? Is it the monthly commitment, or something else?"*

The menu is the prompt. Without it, they say *"just the whole thing"* — which tells you nothing. With it, they pick a specific option — which tells you exactly what to address.

### Rule 3 — Use contrast and impactful word choices

Two versions of the same question:

**Weak:** *"Wouldn't you agree the earlier you start, the better it is?"*
**Strong:** *"Wouldn't you agree we don't have many years in our lives we can afford to waste? Since your time is precious, why not start early so you can taste the fruits of your labour ahead of your peers?"*

Weak has no teeth. Strong uses contrast (*years we can afford to waste* vs *precious time* vs *ahead of peers*) and forces emotional engagement. Both same content; different energy.

### Rule 4 — Use 2 distinctive and contrasting options

Frame the choice as binary, with the *correct* answer obvious.

> *"If you had to choose — would you rather be financially burdened by hospital bills that wipe out your life savings, or transfer that risk to a third party for a small fraction of the cost?"*

Binary. One option is obviously better. The prospect has to pick the better one, which is the one you're recommending. Notice: you didn't *tell* them what to choose. They chose.

**Tail it:** *"You've always struck me as sensible — I'm sure you know the second makes more sense, right?"* — confirms the choice and re-enforces positive identity.

### Rule 5 — Use non-committal words

**Non-committal:** *may*, *might*, *possibility*, *could*, *is there a chance*, *perhaps*
**Avoid:** *must*, *will*, *should*, *definitely*

Weak: *"Do you think I'm the right person for the job?"* — feels high-pressure, they'll hedge
Strong: *"Do you think there's a *possibility* that I might be the right person for the job?"* — low-pressure, they can say yes without committing, which leads to real yes

Non-committal language is counter-intuitive to new FCs who think being direct = being strong. In reality, non-committal language produces more *real* yeses than pressured language produces *fake* ones.

### Rule 6 — Quote respectable others / experts with differing views

When a prospect holds a firm position, don't argue — introduce a respected third party's contradicting position as a question.

> *Prospect:* *"I don't want to invest with insurance companies. ILPs are not good."*
> *You:* *"Why do you think ILPs aren't good?"* (Rule 1)
> *Then:* *"Would you agree investment bankers are experts in investing?"* (buy-in)
> *Then:* *"Would you agree those investment connoisseurs wouldn't hold ILPs in their own portfolios if they weren't good?"* (contradiction via expertise)
> *Then:* *"I have investment bankers as clients and I manage their ILP portfolios — what do you think is one possible reason?"* (reality)

The prospect has to rethink. They can't argue against investment bankers choosing ILPs without contradicting the expertise they just agreed to.

## The 3-point checklist — is this a good question?

Before asking any question, test it:

1. **Does it lead to my objective?** — is there a clear path from this question to where I want the conversation to go?
2. **Is it specific?** — does it force a specific answer, or does it allow a hedge?
3. **Is it logical and indisputable?** — can the prospect disagree without sounding unreasonable?

If all 3 pass, it's a right question. If any fails, rewrite before asking.

---

## Worked example — answering *"why should I work with you?"*

A prospect challenges you:
> *"Why should I work with you? How do I know you're the right advisor?"*

Bad response (statement): *"I have X years of experience, I have Y credentials, Z specialisations…"* — reads as self-promotion, no proof.

Right response (question):
> *"Good question. To date I have over 100 clients, and at least 70% of them were once approached by other FAs offering their services. Yet none have left to work with someone else. They've all stayed with me all these years. What do you think could be a good reason why that's the case?"*

The prospect has to answer the question. Their own answer ("because you're trustworthy," "because you add real value," etc.) becomes the proof — *they* told themselves why you're the right advisor. Infinitely more powerful than you telling them.

## Your 3 most common statements — convert them

Take the 3 statements you find yourself making most often. Convert each to a question.

Example conversions:

| Statement | Question |
|---|---|
| *"You should have CI coverage."* | *"If you were diagnosed with cancer tomorrow — what would that do to your savings and income over the next 3 years?"* |
| *"Starting early is better."* | *"Wouldn't you agree time is our most precious resource? Why would you give up the years that compound hardest?"* |
| *"This is the best plan for you."* | *"Among the options we've looked at — which one do you think gives you the best trade-off between coverage, cost, and flexibility? Why?"* |

The right-hand column is what a Week-8 FC sounds like. Build the habit now.

## Quiz

**Q1. The single biggest reason questions outperform statements is:**
- A) Questions are shorter
- B) People own conclusions they reach themselves; they defend conclusions others make ✓
- C) Questions fill silence
- D) Questions sound more humble

**Why:** This is the fundamental principle. A statement creates an *opposition* — the prospect agrees or disagrees with what you said. A question creates *co-creation* — they arrive at the answer themselves, which becomes their conclusion. They defend their own conclusion far more strongly than they'd ever defend yours. That's why FCs who master question-asking close at dramatically higher rates.

**Q2. Non-committal language (*"may," "might," "possibility"*) outperforms committal language (*"must," "will," "should"*) because:**
- A) It sounds more polite
- B) It lowers pressure so the prospect can say yes without committing — leading to more real yeses ✓
- C) It's less confident
- D) It's more British

**Why:** Committal language creates a *forced-yes* dynamic. Prospects feel boxed in, which triggers defensive hedging. Non-committal language gives them the freedom to say yes as an *exploration* rather than a *commitment* — which paradoxically raises the rate of real yeses. Counter-intuitive to new FCs who equate directness with strength. Strength is in results, not tone.

**Q3. The 3-point checklist to confirm a question is "right" is:**
- A) Short · friendly · warm
- B) Leads to your objective · specific · logical and indisputable ✓
- C) Confident · direct · assumptive
- D) Creative · unusual · memorable

**Why:** Right questions have a clear path to where you're heading (objective), force a specific answer (no hedge), and are logically indisputable (the prospect can't reasonably disagree). If any fails, rewrite — a question that doesn't route you toward your objective wastes the moment; one that allows hedging produces no information; one that's disputable opens a debate you didn't want.

**Q4. Rule 1 says "focus on the keyword — include it in your next question." Why?**
- A) To save time
- B) Repeating their word signals you heard them, and forces them to elaborate on their own position — which almost always softens it ✓
- C) To confuse them
- D) For legal reasons

**Why:** When a prospect says *"insurance is a scam"*, answering with *"why do you think insurance is a scam?"* does two jobs at once: it shows you actually listened (which earns goodwill), and it makes them explain their own position (which usually exposes the weakness in it). Most strong opinions soften under elaboration. This single reflex is the most reliable de-escalation tool in Fact-Find.

**Q5. Rule 4 uses "2 distinctive and contrasting options" to frame the choice as binary with the correct answer obvious. The reason this works:**
- A) Prospects like simple choices
- B) The prospect has to pick the better option — which is the one you're recommending — but they chose it themselves, so they own it ✓
- C) Binary choices are less confusing
- D) It avoids legal issues

**Why:** *"Would you rather be wiped out by a $200K hospital bill, or transfer that risk for a small fraction of the cost?"* has one obviously right answer. The prospect picks the correct option. You didn't tell them — they told themselves. That's the core principle: people defend their own conclusions. Framing a binary with an obvious-better option routes them to the conclusion while preserving their ownership of it.

**Q6. Why is non-committal language (*"may," "might," "possibility"*) more effective than committal language on the question *"am I the right person for the job?"*?**
- A) It sounds humble
- B) Low-pressure framing makes yes feel explorable rather than binding — leading to more *real* yeses than pressure produces *fake* ones ✓
- C) It shortens the question
- D) It avoids compliance issues

**Why:** *"Am I the right person for the job?"* feels like a commitment question — prospects hedge. *"Is there a possibility I *might* be the right person?"* feels like an exploration question — they can say yes without feeling boxed in. That yes then becomes the foundation for a real yes later. Counter-intuitive to new FCs who equate directness with strength; strength is measured in results, not tone.

**Q7. Rule 6 (quote respectable experts with differing views) works by:**
- A) Citing authorities the prospect respects
- B) Introducing a respected third party's contradicting position as a question so the prospect has to rethink — they can't argue against an expertise they just agreed to ✓
- C) Making the FC sound smart
- D) Dropping names

**Why:** The move isn't *"an expert said X, so believe it"* — that's authority-worship and most prospects resist it. The move is: establish agreement that the expert group is respected (*"investment bankers are experts at investing, right?"*), then present a contradiction to the prospect's stated view (*"why would they hold ILPs in their own portfolios?"*). The prospect has to rethink without losing face — they're not contradicting you, they're contradicting their prior position using the expertise they just endorsed.
