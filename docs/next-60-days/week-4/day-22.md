# Day 22 — Cold Prospecting + ABCD Promises

> **The one idea for today:** Warm and cold markets don't use the same emotional register. Warm runs on vulnerability; cold runs on authority. Get that one thing wrong and both channels fail.

By the time you close today you'll tell apart warm-market voice (vulnerability, friendship, easy-out) from cold-market voice (authority, framework, value-first) and pick the right one for each opener, apply the 5 pillars of cold prospecting (niche · framework · profile · sharp DM · follow-up) to design a repeatable cold outreach, and deliver the ABCD Four Promises at the top of any Fact-Find to disarm skepticism in 30 seconds.

---

## The single biggest cold-market mistake

New FCs reach into cold market with warm-market language:

> *"Hey! Hope you've been well — I've been meaning to reach out about something personal…"*

Sent to a stranger? It dies. Ignored, or worse — reported.

That same language *to a friend* is honest and disarming. To a cold contact, there's nothing to be vulnerable about — you don't know each other yet, so honesty about hesitation sounds like weakness, not warmth. The cold prospect is scanning for one signal: *"does this person know what they're doing?"* Vulnerability answers the wrong question.

The fix is emotional-register switching:

| Dimension | **Warm market** | **Cold market** |
|---|---|---|
| **Opener** | Personal reference to shared history | Named pain point of their niche |
| **Credibility** | Existing relationship | Framework + social proof |
| **Tone** | Vulnerable, honest | Professional, value-first |
| **Ask** | Easy-out, no pressure | Clear CTA with value hook |
| **Follow-up** | Value drop (generosity) | Multi-format value drip |

Most new FCs fail both channels because they mix the registers. Warm them up the warm way. Cold them up the cold way. Never the opposite.

## The 5 pillars of cold prospecting

```mermaid
flowchart LR
    P1["1 · Niche"] --> P2["2 · Framework"]
    P2 --> P3["3 · Profile"]
    P3 --> P4["4 · Sharp DM"]
    P4 --> P5["5 · Follow up"]
```

### Pillar 1 — Define a highly targeted niche

If you market to everyone, you market to no one. Specificity is where cold conversion lives.

Not *"business owners"* — **tech founders raising Series A** or **F&B owners with 2+ outlets**.
Not *"parents"* — **parents of primary-school kids in the East**.

Narrowness = credibility = conversion. A cold prospect thinks *"this person is specifically for me"* only when your content is specifically for them.

### Pillar 2 — A named framework

Generic pitch (*"I help people grow their wealth"*) forgets itself the moment they scroll past. Named frameworks don't.

**Examples:**
- **P.I.S.A** — Protect · Income · Scale · Assets
- **F.A.T** — Foundation · Accelerate · Transfer
- **A.P.E.X** — Audit · Protect · Expand · eXit

A framework gives the prospect a *name* to remember you by. Don't invent one for the sake of it — but if your planning process already has 3–4 clean phases, naming them earns compound trust over dozens of touchpoints.

### Pillar 3 — Profile-as-storefront

The profile work from Day 10 matters 10× more in cold market. The only trust signal the cold prospect has before replying is your profile:

- **Framework in the bio** — what you do, in the niche's language
- **Credibility indicators** — client results, industry achievements, specific numbers
- **Social proof** — testimonials, case studies, screenshots
- **Active recent content** — at least 2 posts/week
- **Polished, no junk** — remove anything unprofessional

If any of those are missing, the cold DM dies in the profile-check step, no matter how good the message was.

### Pillar 4 — Sharp, value-driven DMs

Cold DMs must be **concise, relevant, engaging**. No long intros. No hard sell.

Structure:
- Address a **known pain point** of the niche (shows you know them)
- Offer **value upfront** — an ebook, a checklist, an industry report
- Feel **warm, human, insightful** — not spammy

Bad cold DM:
> *"Hi, I saw your profile and I think I could help with your financial planning. Would you like to book a call?"*

Strong cold DM:
> *"Hi [name] — saw you're running a 2-outlet F&B operation. Most owners I work with in your stage run into the same cashflow trap at the 3rd outlet expansion. I put together a 1-page audit we use to stress-test it — happy to send if useful. No pitch, no call required."*

Same length. Entirely different response rate. The difference: named pain point + specific value + low-commitment offer.

### Pillar 5 — Follow up consistently (with value, not asks)

Most cold prospects don't reply on round 1. Not rejection — busy, missed it, saved for later and forgot.

Rules:
- **Never spam** *"just checking in"* — that carries zero signal
- **Every follow-up carries new value** — a different insight, a trend reference, a relevant resource
- **Mix formats** — text, voice note, short video — so you stand out from the noise

The structured cold follow-up sequence doubles or triples the first-round response rate. It's where the real ROI of cold lives.

## The Unconventional Cold Call

If you're doing phone cold outreach, the standard opener (*"Hi, I'm a financial advisor calling to see if you'd be interested…"*) gets hung up on within 3 seconds.

The unconventional opener breaks the pattern with honesty:

### Stage 1 — The favour opener
> *"Hi Mark — I actually need to ask you a favour, please don't hang up right away. Honestly, I've called the last 50 people today and every single one of them hung up on me. I'm just hoping you'll be the kind soul to give me one minute of your time. I promise I'm not here to sell you anything you don't need — and if at any point you're not interested, you can hang up and I'll still be grateful. Is that okay?"*

### Stage 2 — Research-not-sales framing
> *"What I'm actually doing — me and my team are trying to understand the critical financial-planning issues that professionals aged 30 to 45 are navigating. Would you mind answering a few quick questions?"*

### Stage 3 — The 3 hot-button research questions
1. *"What do you find most challenging about managing your finances right now?"*
2. *"When you think about your future, what financial goals or concerns come to mind first?"*
3. *"What would make you feel more confident about your retirement plans?"*

### Stage 4 — Thank + non-intrusive value offer
> *"Really appreciate you taking the time. Since you've been so helpful, I'd like to send you our Retirement Confidence Index as a thank-you — would that be useful?"*

**Why it works:** the favour opener suspends the *"he's selling me something"* assumption. The research framing gives a non-sales reason to be on the call. The questions *are* the rapport + diagnostic. The value drop at the end is the natural bridge to a Fact-Find later.

**Not all 50 will convert.** Cold is a volume game. A 5–10% meeting-conversion rate is a healthy cold-call script.

## The ABCD Four Promises

Whether the appointment came from warm or cold, the **first 30 seconds of the Fact-Find** set the frame. The ABCD Promises are the opener that disarms skepticism before you ask the first real question.

Deliver all four in order, in roughly this form:

| Letter | Promise | Script |
|---|---|---|
| **A** | **Assurance** | *"Before we start — this meeting isn't to sell you anything. I'm here to understand you and your goals. If we end up with a proposal, it's because I shared something valuable and you felt comfortable moving forward."* |
| **B** | **Best Interest** | *"If we do reach the proposal stage, my promise is that what I plan for you is exactly what I'd do for myself in your shoes."* |
| **C** | **Confidentiality** | *"Everything you share with me today stays in strictest confidence."* |
| **D** | **Disclosure** | *"With that said, I hope you can share openly — because the quality of the recommendation depends on the quality of what I learn."* |

**Why all four matter:**
- Assurance removes the *"is he going to push me?"* worry
- Best Interest answers *"why should I trust him?"*
- Confidentiality answers *"is it safe to share?"*
- Disclosure reframes openness as *their* responsibility, not yours

4 sentences, 40 seconds. Done before the first Fact-Find question. The Fact-Find runs entirely differently after the ABCD than before — prospects open up faster because the frame has already answered the concerns they hadn't voiced yet.

## Where cold sits in the week

Week 4's 30-outreach target does **not** require cold. For most new FCs in the first 60 days, cold is:

- 0–5 of the 30 outreaches — **optional and experimental**
- A chance to test one cold DM with your named framework or one unconventional cold call
- Not the lifeblood of Year 1

Warm + Semi-Warm is ~75–85% of the revenue for the first 2 years. Cold is a *volume supplement*, not a replacement. Don't burn a week learning cold scripts at the expense of drilling the Market Survey for your Semi-Warm list.

The reason cold is on the curriculum this week: knowing the register-switch (warm vs cold) *prevents* you from mixing them in your warm-market scripts. The cold structure clarifies what warm *is not*.

## Quiz

**Q1. Warm market and cold market require different emotional registers. Which mapping is correct?**
- A) Warm = authority / cold = vulnerability
- B) Warm = vulnerability / cold = authority + framework ✓
- C) Both use the same tone
- D) Warm = pitch / cold = storytelling

**Why:** Warm market has pre-existing context — vulnerability and honesty work because you both know each other already. Cold market has no context, so the prospect is scanning for competence signals — frameworks, niche clarity, social proof. Mixing the two (vulnerable cold openers or authority-heavy warm messages) fails both channels because neither audience gets what they need.

**Q2. The 5 pillars of cold prospecting are:**
- A) Niche · Framework · Profile · Sharp DM · Follow-up ✓
- B) Reach · Engage · Convert · Retain · Refer
- C) Awareness · Interest · Desire · Action · Loyalty
- D) Market · Message · Media · Measure · Modify

**Why:** The 5 pillars map the entire cold funnel. Niche = who you're talking to. Framework = why they remember you. Profile = whether they trust you after clicking through. Sharp DM = whether they reply. Follow-up = whether they convert. Missing any one pillar collapses the funnel at that stage.

**Q3. The ABCD Four Promises delivered at the top of a Fact-Find stand for:**
- A) Ask · Build · Close · Deliver
- B) Assurance · Best Interest · Confidentiality · Disclosure ✓
- C) Acknowledge · Background · Confidence · Deal
- D) Always · Be · Closing · Deals

**Why:** Each promise answers a different implicit worry the prospect arrives with. Assurance answers *"are you going to push me?"* Best Interest answers *"is the advice actually for me?"* Confidentiality answers *"is it safe to share?"* Disclosure reframes openness as *their* responsibility. Delivered in 40 seconds at the top, the ABCD changes the rest of the Fact-Find from defensive to cooperative.

**Q4. Day 22 contrasts a bad cold DM (*"Hi, I saw your profile and I think I could help with your financial planning. Would you like to book a call?"*) with a strong one. The critical difference is:**
- A) The strong one is longer
- B) The strong one names a known pain point, offers specific value upfront, and has a low-commitment offer ✓
- C) The strong one uses hashtags
- D) The strong one includes emojis

**Why:** Length isn't the differentiator — the bad and strong examples are similar length. The strong version names the prospect's niche-specific pain (*"F&B operators at 2 outlets run into the same cashflow trap at the 3rd outlet expansion"*), offers a specific value artifact (a 1-page audit), and makes the commitment tiny (*"no pitch, no call required"*). Each element removes a reason to ignore.

**Q5. The "unconventional cold call" opener is structured around:**
- A) A direct pitch
- B) A "favour, don't hang up" frame where you name your context honestly, ask for 1 minute, and promise they can hang up any time ✓
- C) A warm-market-style vulnerability opener
- D) A price drop

**Why:** Standard cold-call openers get hung up on in 3 seconds because the prospect's pattern-match is already firing. The favour opener interrupts that pattern — the honesty is so unusual that the prospect stays on for the 10-second window needed to land the real ask (research, not sales). The promise they can hang up any time removes the need to hang up preemptively.

**Q6. The D in the ABCD Four Promises (Assurance · Best Interest · Confidentiality · Disclosure) reframes what?**
- A) The pitch
- B) Openness — it reframes openness as *their* responsibility, not yours, because the quality of advice depends on the quality of what they share ✓
- C) The price
- D) The product

**Why:** After Assurance (no pressure), Best Interest (advice is for them), and Confidentiality (safe to share), the fourth promise flips the listener's role. Instead of being interrogated, they're the ones who have to show up honestly for this to work. That reframing is why the Fact-Find after ABCD feels cooperative — the prospect has signed on to being *open*, not just *answering*.

**Q7. For a typical Week-4 new FC, how many of the 30 outreaches should be cold?**
- A) 30 — cold is the fastest path
- B) 0–5, optional and experimental — warm + Semi-Warm is ~75–85% of Year-1 revenue ✓
- C) 15 — half and half
- D) 0 — cold is never useful

**Why:** Cold conversion is a small fraction of warm conversion for a new FC, and warm also compounds through referrals. Making cold the majority of Week-4 volume trades a high-conversion channel for a low-conversion one. 0–5 cold outreaches lets you drill the register-switch (Day 22's entire purpose) without letting cold displace warm-market work that's actually paying.
