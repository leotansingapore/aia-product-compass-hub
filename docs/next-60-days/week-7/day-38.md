# Day 38 — Hot Buttons II

> **The one idea for today:** A hot button you haven't surfaced is a hot button you can't use. Today is about the questions that pull them out.

By the time you close today you'll have 9 social hot-button questions that surface personal motivation without feeling like a sales interview, 6 sales hot-button questions that specifically surface financial-planning hot buttons, and a habit of mapping each hot button to a specific product benefit so the pitch activates the emotion they already surfaced for you.

---

## Why surfacing is 80% of the work

You can only use a hot button you've surfaced. And most new FCs don't surface them — they ask *"what are your financial goals?"* and accept the flat answer at face value.

That question is too direct. Prospects give rehearsed answers (*"save for retirement, protect my family, grow wealth"*) that sound like *goals* but have no emotional heat behind them. You can pitch to those answers all day and never activate a real hot button.

**The fix:** ask questions that prospects *can't* answer generically. Questions that force a specific memory or image to the surface — which brings the emotion with it.

---

## 9 social hot-button questions

These work in any conversation — warm-up coffee, Fact-Find, casual chat. They sound like *interest in the person*, not sales interview. But each one surfaces hot-button territory:

1. *"What's one thing you couldn't live without?"*
2. *"What's the single biggest fear you have in life?"*
3. *"What's something that keeps you up at night?"*
4. *"What ongoing thing in your life frustrates you the most?"*
5. *"What are the top few criteria you have when considering a travel destination?"*
6. *"What are the top 3 things you look for in a partner?"*
7. *"If you could relive one experience from the past, what would it be?"*
8. *"If you have a bucket list, what's the #1 thing on it that's non-negotiable?"*
9. *"What are some personal values or beliefs you'd never compromise on?"*

**Use 2–3 per meeting** — not all 9. Scattered throughout. The *frustrates* question early surfaces current pain; the *bucket list* question later surfaces forward-looking hot buttons. The *values* question surfaces identity-level hot buttons.

---

## 6 sales hot-button questions

These are specifically financial-planning framed. Use them during the Fact-Find itself:

1. *"Suppose we never got you started on financial planning — what are the worst implications you can imagine for your future self?"*
2. *"If we manage to achieve all your financial goals we've set, how would you envision your dream retirement looking?"*
3. *"What are the top 3 things that matter most to you when considering who to work with as an advisor?"*
4. *"Who would you be most worried about if you were prematurely gone one day?"*
5. *"What's the worst experience you've had with financial planning or a financial advisor?"*
6. *"What do you think is the most critical component of every great portfolio?"*

**The killer question is #4:** *"Who would you be most worried about if you were prematurely gone one day?"* — it activates the loved-ones hot button directly, and most prospects genuinely haven't pictured the answer before. The silence after you ask is real.

Use these late in the Fact-Find, not at the start. They need the rapport you've built in the first 30 minutes to land.

---

## Mapping hot buttons to product benefits

Once you've surfaced a hot button, map it to a specific piece of your recommendation. The mapping is what makes the pitch *land*.

| Hot button surfaced | Product benefit it maps to |
|---|---|
| *"My dad's stroke recovery is eating everything they saved"* | **CI coverage** — specifically the lump-sum benefit that doesn't require recovery to be "complete" |
| *"I don't want my kids struggling like I did as a kid"* | **Term protection + education savings** — so they're covered if something happens + funded if nothing does |
| *"I never want to rely on my kids financially when I'm older"* | **Retirement income plan** — annuity-style structure that generates income |
| *"I work too hard to keep losing money to old policies I don't understand"* | **Portfolio audit + restructuring** — your value as an advisor, not a product |
| *"What if I get CI and can't work — my whole family depends on my income"* | **Disability income + CI** — bundled coverage |

The mapping isn't product-first (*"here's my CI plan — let me find a reason for you to want it"*). It's hot-button-first (*"you said X matters — here's the specific thing that addresses X"*).

---

## The callback technique

During the pitch, reference the hot button explicitly:

> *"When I was putting this together, I kept coming back to what you said earlier — 'I never want my kids struggling like I did.' That's the reason I'm recommending $500K of CI rather than the $250K most advisors would suggest at your income level. Because if something happens, $250K covers 3 years of your family's current lifestyle. $500K covers 8. That gap is what separates your kids having time to adjust from your kids having to compromise immediately. That's what your comment was about."*

Three elements of a good callback:

1. **Quote them directly** — *"what you said earlier — 'I never want…'"*
2. **Connect the product to the hot button** with specific numbers
3. **Paint the concrete difference** — not *"more coverage is better"*, but *"3 years vs 8 years"*

The callback converts the pitch from *generic* to *personal*. Done well, it's often the moment the close decides itself.

---

## Capture discipline — the iPad / notebook habit

You can't callback a hot button you didn't capture. Build the habit:

- When a hot button surfaces, immediately type 3 words into your iPad notes
- Mark it with a `🔥` or `**` so you can find it later
- Reference it in your pitch construction during the break between Fact-Find and recommendation

Example capture during Fact-Find:

```
Amir, 38, 2 kids (6 and 3)
- Spouse Ruth, works PT
- Cashflow: $8K/mo, savings thin
🔥 Dad had stroke at 62, watching it eat their retirement
🔥 "I don't want Ruth going through what Mum is going through"
- Current coverage: 1 term, 2 ILPs (messy), CI $100K
🔥 Said "I grew up with not enough" (value-level)
```

When it's time to pitch, you have 3 specific hot buttons to reference. The pitch writes itself.

---

## Hot-button hygiene

One caution: don't overuse hot-button activation. A good FC surfaces hot buttons *to understand the prospect*, not to manipulate.

- **Surfacing:** asking real questions, listening to real answers, building the recommendation around real needs. That's legitimate.
- **Manufacturing:** creating fear where no real gap exists. That's manipulation.

The ethical line: **only hit hot buttons where the product actually addresses a real concern.** If someone has full coverage and adequate retirement, don't manufacture doomsday scenarios to activate loss aversion. Move on.

Hot-button work is a sword — use it to cut real problems, not to wound prospects into agreement.

## Quiz

**Q1. The most powerful sales hot-button question from Section 3 is often:**
- A) *"What are your financial goals?"*
- B) *"Who would you be most worried about if you were prematurely gone one day?"* ✓
- C) *"What's your budget?"*
- D) *"Do you have an existing advisor?"*

**Why:** Question B activates the loved-ones hot button directly and most prospects haven't pictured the concrete answer before. The silence after you ask it is real — that's the hot button firing. Question A is a generic goals question that produces rehearsed answers. B forces a specific image, which brings the emotion with it.

**Q2. Mapping a hot button to a product benefit means:**
- A) Finding the product you want to sell and inventing a reason they should want it
- B) Starting from a hot button they surfaced and naming the specific product element that addresses it ✓
- C) Selling them the most expensive option
- D) Comparing your product to competitors

**Why:** The mapping runs hot-button → product benefit, not the reverse. If you start from a product and backfill the reason, the prospect feels sold-to. If you start from a concern they actually raised and walk them to the specific element of your recommendation that addresses it, they feel *heard*. Same product; completely different received experience.

**Q3. The "callback" technique during pitch involves:**
- A) Calling the prospect back on the phone after the meeting
- B) Quoting them directly during the pitch and connecting the product to the specific hot button they surfaced ✓
- C) Referring back to your own sales collateral
- D) Returning a referred lead's phone call

**Why:** A callback in pitch craft means verbally referencing what the prospect said earlier — *"when I was putting this together, I kept coming back to what you said about [X]…"*. The explicit quote + specific product linkage + concrete difference (*"3 years vs 8 years"*) converts a generic pitch into a personal one. Most new FCs surface hot buttons and then forget them by pitch time. Capture + callback is the discipline that closes that loop.

**Q4. Why is *"what are your financial goals?"* a weak question for surfacing hot buttons?**
- A) It's too long
- B) Prospects give rehearsed answers (*"save for retirement, protect family, grow wealth"*) that sound like goals but carry no emotional heat ✓
- C) It's too personal
- D) It's not financial-planning specific enough

**Why:** Direct goal questions invite canned responses that match what the prospect thinks they *should* say. No emotional heat means no hot-button firing. Questions that force specific memory or image (*"what's keeping you up at night?"*, *"worst experience with an advisor?"*) bypass the rehearsed script and surface real material. Generic questions produce generic answers; specific questions produce specific emotions.

**Q5. After asking a strong hot-button question (like *"who would you be most worried about if you were prematurely gone?"*), the correct next move is:**
- A) Follow up with another question immediately
- B) Sit in silence and let the prospect process for 20+ seconds — the silence IS the hot-button firing ✓
- C) Pivot to product
- D) Rephrase the question

**Why:** The question does the activation; the silence is the work. Filling the silence (A, D) interrupts the emotional processing and resets the prospect back to "conversation mode" before the hot button fully fires. Pivoting to product (C) waste the moment entirely. 20+ seconds of silence feels uncomfortable but is exactly where the hot-button → decision linkage is forming.

**Q6. Mapping a hot button to a product benefit requires:**
- A) Selling the biggest plan
- B) Starting from the hot button the prospect surfaced and naming the specific product element with specific numbers that addresses it ✓
- C) Explaining every product feature
- D) Waiting until after the close

**Why:** Generic "more coverage is better" doesn't land. *"You said you don't want Ruth going through what your mum is going through. $500K of CI covers 8 years of your family's current lifestyle; $250K covers 3. That gap is what your comment was about"* — specific quote + specific numbers + concrete difference. That's mapping. The prospect sees themselves in the solution, because the solution was built from their words.

**Q7. The "hot-button hygiene" ethical line is:**
- A) Use every hot button you find for maximum conversion
- B) Only activate hot buttons where the product actually addresses a real concern — manufacturing fear where there's no gap is manipulation ✓
- C) Avoid hot buttons entirely
- D) Only activate positive hot buttons

**Why:** Hot-button work is a sword — useful against real problems, wounding when used against phantom ones. If a prospect is genuinely over-covered already, painting CI doomsday scenarios manufactures emotion that serves you, not them. The sustainable rule is tie every hot-button activation to a real gap + a product element that genuinely solves it. Avoiding them entirely (C) abandons the most honest part of advisory work (helping prospects see what they'd otherwise defer on).
