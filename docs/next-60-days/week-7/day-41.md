# Day 41 — Pitch Mechanics: Confidence + Closing

> **The one idea for today:** Assertive language closes at ~70%. Hedged language closes at half that. The fix isn't a new script — it's a tight set of verbal patterns and a profile-matched close.

By the time you close today you'll have swapped 5 passive / hedging phrases for their assumptive equivalents in your live pitch, picked up the Assumptive Position so belief, self-talk, and language move together, and matched the 4 closing techniques (Assumptive · Procedural · Reassurance · Follow-up) to DISC profiles using the compatibility matrix.

---

## The 70% rule

> **"When salespeople demonstrate confidence and ask assertively for what they want, prospects say yes about 70% of the time."**

That's the observed close rate across industries when belief and language line up. Two things carry it:

1. **Belief** that the prospect will want what you're offering
2. **Language** that matches that belief

When belief and language align, the prospect unconsciously matches your certainty. When they diverge (you believe but sound tentative, or the other way around), the prospect picks up the dissonance and stalls.

**The 70% number isn't magic. It's just what happens when the default frame is *of course this is happening* rather than *I hope this happens.*** The frame shows up in the first 5 words of every sentence.

## The passive → assumptive swap

Same content, different framing. Read both columns aloud — notice how the receiver's ear processes them:

| Passive (weak) | Assumptive (strong) |
|---|---|
| *"Is this a good time?"* | *"The reason I'm calling is…"* |
| *"Would you maybe want to consider…"* | *"What we're doing next is…"* |
| *"What do you think?"* | *"Why don't we go ahead and set that up?"* |
| *"Maybe we could meet sometime next week…"* | *"Tuesday 7pm or Thursday 11am — which works?"* |
| *"I was just wondering if…"* | *"Quick question —…"* |
| *"We could perhaps consider adding…"* | *"I'd add X here. The reason is [Y]."* |
| *"I'm sorry to bother you…"* | *"[Name], I've got something worth 3 minutes."* |

Passive phrases leak apology into the frame. The prospect's ear hears *"I don't fully believe in what I'm about to say."* Assumptive phrases skip the apology — and the prospect fills the missing apology with *confidence*.

**The drill for this week:** pick 3 passive phrases you say most often. Replace with assumptive equivalents. Rehearse until the assumptive version is what your mouth says automatically under pressure.

## The Power of Because

A quiet research finding: **asking for something with the word *"because"* increases compliance rates measurably — even when the reason is weak.**

| Without because | With because |
|---|---|
| *"Can I have 15 minutes of your time?"* | *"Can I have 15 minutes of your time, because I'd like to understand your current setup?"* |
| *"Can we book Thursday?"* | *"Can we book Thursday because I want to walk through the comparison with you live?"* |
| *"Would you sign here?"* | *"Would you sign here, because that starts the coverage from today?"* |

The word itself activates the reasoning-and-compliance circuitry even when the reason is almost tautological. Use it in any ask where compliance matters.

## The Assumptive Position — the frame, not the close

The **Assumptive Close** (§6 below) is one tactic. The **Assumptive Position** is the posture underneath every tactic.

> **The Assumptive Position:** *"What I'm asking for is going to happen. My job is to make it easy."*

Applied concretely:
- You assume the meeting will be booked → you offer 2 specific times instead of *"when are you free?"*
- You assume the Fact-Find will happen → you arrive 5 min early with the agenda printed
- You assume the recommendation will be acted on → you come to the presentation with the signup form ready
- You assume referrals will be given → you ask at every appointment

Some of the best sales teachers call the Assumptive Position the single most important sales posture — not because it guarantees the yes, but because it reshapes every tactical decision around the *expected* yes. Most tactics land harder from this posture than from a *"let's see what happens"* one.

**The belief → language chain.** Your language is a readout of your belief. You can't sustain assumptive language on top of hopeful belief — the belief leaks through. The posture comes first; the phrases follow.

## The 4 Closing Techniques

Four closes to have in your toolkit. Each fits different profiles and moments:

### Close 1 — Assumptive Close
Skip the *"will you?"* beat. Assume the yes and move to logistics.

> *"Since you have two girls, I'll set up plans for both of them, correct?"*
> *"Tuesday 7pm — same venue — for the signing?"*
> *"Credit card or bank transfer?"*

**Fits best:** D (expects decisive momentum), *sometimes* I when verbal yes is already in.
**Don't use on:** S (feels pressure), C (feels pushy, triggers re-analysis).

### Close 2 — Procedural Close
Walk them through the next steps in order. No ambiguity.

> *"Let me explain the procedure — first we do X, then Y, then Z. I'll need a cheque for $30,000 payable to [entity]. Once those steps are done, you officially have the plan in place."*

**Fits best:** C (they respect the structure), S (no pressure, just process).
**Don't use on:** I (too dry — they tune out).

### Close 3 — Reassurance Close
Use social proof + address their specific hesitation + move forward.

> *"Alan, this is our best-selling savings plan — particularly strong among clients who have many options. They still choose this. Let's take the first step today."*

Or longer, objection-addressing version:

> *"Jenny, I understand you're hesitant — the job uncertainty is real. But the uncertain future is just that — uncertain. The certain part is that your portfolio has a critical gap right now that could cost you 6 figures. Even if you're unemployed for 6 months, your savings sustain. I'd rather remove the certain risk than bet on the uncertain future. Shall we proceed?"*

**Fits best:** I (social proof), S (gentle pressure + assurance combo).
**Don't use on:** D (too long), C (unless social proof is data-backed).

### Close 4 — Follow-up Close
The *last resort* — you didn't close in the meeting. Set the follow-up discipline.

> *Before they leave:* *"If there's one thing that might be holding you back, what would that be?"* (Iceberg question.)
> *Book the follow-up date immediately* — specific day/time.
> *Follow up within 3 days* — not longer.
> *When you follow up, don't ask:* *"Do you have any questions?"* — instead: *"Over the past 3 days you've reviewed the recommendation. Are we good to proceed?"*

**Fits best:** C (genuinely need research time), S (need processing time).
**Never use as the *default* close** — follow-up close is where decisions go to die if you don't re-anchor. Only use when closes 1–3 didn't land.

## Profile × Close compatibility matrix

| Profile | Assumptive | Procedural | Reassurance | Follow-up |
|---|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| **D** | ✅ Best | ⚠️ If they trust the process | ⚠️ Keep short | ❌ Rarely needed |
| **I** | ⚠️ After warm yes | ❌ Too dry | ✅ Best (social proof) | ⚠️ Decay risk — close fast |
| **S** | ❌ Feels like pressure | ✅ Great | ✅ Great | ✅ Honest fit |
| **C** | ❌ Feels pushy | ✅ Best | ⚠️ Only if data-backed | ✅ Default for C |

**The one mismatch that destroys deals:** **Assumptive close on S or C.** It reads as pressure to the S and pushiness to the C. S will either fake-agree and unwind (48-hour ghost) or hard-reject. C will re-analyse from zero and often disqualify you entirely. Two opposite failure modes, one cause. Don't do it.

---

## Follow-up discipline — don't leave without the date

If a prospect is walking out without signing, the single most important move is to lock the next meeting *before they leave.*

- **No open-ended "I'll text you"** — that's where deals die
- **Specific day + time** — *"Thursday 7pm, same venue"*
- **Book on their calendar in your presence** — so it's real

Then the follow-up itself:
- **Within 3 days max.** Longer = decay.
- **Don't open with *"any questions?"*** — invites fresh objections. Open with *"ready to proceed?"*
- **Send a reminder message 24 hrs before** — *"Looking forward to Thursday. Quick recap of what we agreed…"* — re-anchors why they were interested.

## Quiz

**Q1. The 70% rule says:**
- A) 70% of prospects will close regardless of language
- B) When salespeople demonstrate confidence and ask assertively, prospects say yes about 70% of the time — versus roughly half that with hedged language ✓
- C) 70% is the highest close rate possible
- D) 70% of objections come from price

**Why:** The 70% number captures the effect of belief + language alignment. Assertive *"what we're doing next is…"* framing lets the prospect match your certainty. Hedged *"maybe we could perhaps consider…"* framing signals doubt the prospect then mirrors. The gap between the two styles is the largest single language lever most FCs have.

**Q2. The 4 closing techniques are:**
- A) Trial · Alternative · Urgency · Assumptive
- B) Assumptive · Procedural · Reassurance · Follow-up ✓
- C) Hard · Soft · Indirect · Direct
- D) Open · Close · Reveal · Commit

**Why:** Assumptive skips the permission beat and moves to logistics. Procedural walks them through steps in order. Reassurance uses social proof and addresses specific hesitation. Follow-up is the last-resort close when the first three didn't land — it re-anchors the decision on a second meeting rather than letting the prospect walk into limbo.

**Q3. The biggest deal-destroyer across profiles is:**
- A) Too much data
- B) Using Assumptive Close on an S or C ✓
- C) Offering too many options
- D) Not being confident enough

**Why:** Assumptive Close (*"credit card or bank transfer?"*) respects D's decisiveness. On an S, it feels like pressure — they fake-agree to restore harmony and unwind in 48 hours. On a C, it feels pushy — they re-analyse from zero and often disqualify you. Two opposite failure modes, one cause: wrong close on wrong profile. Matching close-to-profile is the single highest-leverage move today.

**Q4. A C-profile prospect says *"let me think about it."* Your best close is:**
- A) Assumptive — push for the decision now
- B) Reassurance — tell them everyone chooses this plan
- C) Procedural + Follow-up — walk through the process and book a specific follow-up meeting to close ✓
- D) Urgency — tell them the offer expires Friday

**Why:** C's genuinely need time to research — pushing violates their decision process and kills the deal. The best C close facilitates their analysis (Procedural, data-forward) and *books the follow-up* 7–14 days out. That way they get the time they need + you have a locked re-anchor. Urgency (D) is the fastest way to get a C to walk.

**Q5. The Assumptive Position differs from the Assumptive Close because:**
- A) They're the same thing
- B) The Assumptive Position is the *posture* — *"what I'm asking for is going to happen; my job is to make it easy"* — while the Assumptive Close is one specific tactic inside that posture ✓
- C) The Position is for beginners; the Close is for seniors
- D) The Position is about price

**Why:** The Close is a technique used in the last 30 seconds of a meeting. The Position is the expected-yes frame that shapes how you arrive, how you offer options, how you prepare paperwork. Every tactic lands harder from this posture than from a *"let's see what happens"* posture — because your language reflects your belief, and assumptive language can't sit on top of hopeful belief without leaking.

**Q6. Reassurance Close (social proof + address hesitation + forward move) lands best on:**
- A) D and C
- B) I and S ✓
- C) Only I
- D) Only S

**Why:** I's respond to social proof (*"high-net-worth clients choose this"* matters). S's respond to gentle assurance + addressing a specific hesitation. Both profiles buy with emotional confirmation, which is what Reassurance provides. D's find it too long; C's reject it unless the social proof is data-backed (*"72% of clients in your income bracket…"*).

**Q7. Follow-up Close follow-up: when you re-connect within 3 days, you should NOT open with:**
- A) *"Ready to proceed?"*
- B) *"Here's a recap of what we discussed"*
- C) *"Do you have any questions?"* ✓
- D) *"I believe you've reviewed the recommendation — are we good to move forward?"*

**Why:** *"Any questions?"* invites fresh objections that weren't there 3 days ago and gives the prospect permission to dig. The correct opener assumes the intervening time was used to review and moves directly to commitment. Recap (B) + direct ask (A, D) re-anchors why they were interested and asks for the decision cleanly.
