# Day 4 — Defence + Expansion: 5 Objections + GPP+UCC Canonical Pair

> **Today's frame:** The objections to UCC are a single underlying objection wearing different clothes. Every answer routes back to the staircase: claim, reset, claim again. The cross-sell is the canonical GPP+UCC pair — two halves of the same protection design.

This is the longest module. Part 1 covers the 5 objections (the conviction-story heart). Part 2 covers the canonical GPP+UCC pairing + 6 restructure cases. Estimated read + drill: ~65 minutes.

---

# Part 1 — Top 5 Objections (the Conviction-Story Heart)

> **The one idea for Part 1:** Don't argue. Reframe what the prospect is actually choosing. Existing bundled cover collapses on first claim. Cancer/heart recur most often. Early CI catches things at year zero. Robert's case study (S$100K → S$500K across 8 years) is the proof. Competitor restrictions (Prudential's related-illness block, GE's three-claim cap) mean similar plans aren't actually similar.

By the end of Part 1 you should be able to recite all 5 conviction stories from memory, deploy the brochure-page-10 Robert case study without notes, and recognise which objection arc the prospect is on.

---

## Objection 1 — "I already have CI in my GPP / PLP / whole-life plan"

**The frame:** Existing bundled plans collapse on first claim. UCC decouples CI from death and savings.

**The script:**

> *"What do you think happens, right? If you claim, do you think you'll still get back this 20k surrender value? The answer is no. The moment you claim for CI, there's no more death. The moment you claim for CI, there's no more surrender value as well. Because all of it is actually interlinked."* [Video 4]

> *"Even if they claim again for CI, it will not affect your death. Even if they claim for CI, it will not affect the investment side of things."* [Video 9]

**The conviction:**
- Bundled CI plans (GPP, PLP, ASCC, whole-life-with-CI-rider) collapse on first claim — death cover reduces, surrender value disappears
- UCC is pure CI — claiming on UCC leaves death cover and savings completely untouched
- The fix isn't to cancel GPP/PLP — it's to LAYER UCC on top for Early CI + recurring claims (Day 4 Part 2)

**Sources:** Video 4, Video 8 (Case Studies), Video 9

---

## Objection 2 — "Multi-claim sounds rare — is it worth the premium?"

**The frame:** Most CI claims are heart and cancer, both of which commonly recur. The Enhancer rider price is small relative to the protection value.

**The script:**

> *"Most claims are for cancer and heart attacks, and those often recur. That's why the Enhancer is important."* [Video 2]

> *"For just $7 more a month, you get coverage if cancer or heart attack comes back."* [Video 2]

**The conviction:**
- Cancer recurrence rates and second heart events are statistically significant — far from "rare"
- Robert case study: 4 separate claims totalling S$500,000 across 8 years from a single S$100,000 base [Brochure p.10]
- ~$7/month for the Enhancer covers exactly the recurrence scenario most likely to happen

**Numbers:** Robert at age 43, S$100,000 base UCC. Multi-stage stroke claim, then major-stage Liver Cancer with Catastrophic, then Heart Attack at early stage, then two Liver Cancer relapses. Total payouts S$500,000+ across 8 years. [Brochure p.10]

**Sources:** Video 2, [Brochure p.10]

---

## Objection 3 — "Early CI seems redundant — I'd rather wait until major"

**The frame:** Early detection rates have risen dramatically with imaging technology. Without Early CI, the prospect cannot claim during the years they need cash most — diagnosis, treatment, recovery.

**The script:**

> *"Nowadays technology is very advanced and if you don't cover for early CI, then there's a big gap in your portfolio."* [Video 9]

**The conviction:**
- Modern imaging (PET, MRI, advanced screening) catches conditions at stages that didn't have payouts a decade ago
- The years the prospect needs cash MOST are the diagnosis-to-recovery years — not the worst-case major stage
- UCC covers 42 Early Stage and 35 Intermediate Stage conditions, with the same S$350,000 per-episode cap on each [Brochure p.13, PS p.3]

**Sources:** Video 9, Brochure p.3, p.13

## Objection 4 — "What's the actual probability I'd recur?"

**The frame:** Don't make medical claims. Use the brochure's illustrative case study as the proof.

**The script:**

> *"It's ultimate assurance knowing your coverage fully resets every 12 months from the last claim."* [Brochure p.3]

Then walk through the brochure's Robert example [Brochure p.10]:

| Robert's claim history | Stage | Payout |
|---|---|---|
| Multi-stage Stroke | Early | S$X |
| Liver Cancer | Major + Catastrophic | S$X |
| Heart Attack | Early Stage | S$X |
| Liver Cancer relapse 1 | Enhancer | S$X (50%) |
| Liver Cancer relapse 2 | Enhancer | S$X (50%) |

Total claims: S$500,000+ across 8 years from S$100,000 base.

**The conviction:**
- The brochure illustrates the relapse arithmetic without making medical-probability claims
- The mechanic is what matters — the policy works the way it's designed, regardless of what probability the prospect assigns

**Sources:** [Brochure p.10], PS p.7

## Objection 5 — "Competitor X's multi-claim plan looks similar"

**The frame:** Two specific competitor mechanics block follow-up claims that UCC pays.

### vs Prudential

> *"For Prudential, you cannot claim because it's a related CI. Because coronary artery disease versus bypass versus heart attack — as long as it's related to the same area, all the future claims related to heart conditions are not claimable anymore."* [Video 5]

UCC has no related-illness block.

### vs Great Eastern

> *"For Great Eastern, you can only claim if it is less than three times. After three times, the policy will cease."* [Video 5]

UCC has no claim count cap on major-stage claims.

### Pricing benchmark (illustrative, time-bound — verify in iPOS)

UCC vs competitors on S$100,000 cover [Video 1]:

| Competitor | UCC advantage |
|---|---|
| Prudential | ~11% cheaper |
| Great Eastern | ~6% cheaper |
| Manulife | ~29% cheaper |
| Singlife | ~22% cheaper |

UCC covers **150 conditions** vs Great Eastern Great Critical Cover ~53 conditions.

**Important:** Cite competitor pricing only when current iPOS quotation matches. Video 1 figures are illustrative and time-bound.

**Sources:** Video 1, Video 5, Video 9

## The single line that handles 80% of pushback

When a prospect drifts back to "let me just stick with what I have":

> *"You're right that what you have today works for one event. The question is what works for two, three, or four events across the next 30 years. UCC isn't replacing what you have — it's filling the gap your existing plan doesn't address. Layer it. Don't replace it."*

# Part 2 — Cross-sell + Bundling: GPP + UCC Canonical Pair

> **The one idea for Part 2:** GPP and UCC are not alternatives — they are halves of the same protection design. GPP carries Major CI + death + savings + Premium Waiver of life cover. UCC carries Early CI + recurring claims + special conditions. Two appointments, never one. Then 6 real restructure cases close the cross-sell.

By the end of Part 2 you should be able to deploy the canonical packaging line verbatim, recommend the right restructure pattern per prospect (Proactive Life, GPP+ASCC, endowment+GPP), recognise when UCC pairs with APA for the protection-floor-then-accumulation sequence, and book a UCC-only appointment when the prospect is already a GPP holder.

---

## The canonical GPP + UCC pair

Direct from the GPP curriculum GPP Day 4 Part 2:

> *"I do not sell the Early CI part — I just purely sell the Major CI part. And then I package the rest on a UCC for the Early CI part. The life plan gives the coverage for the Major CI part, the death part, and the savings part. For subsequent claims, recurring claims, and Early CI claims, that's where the UCC would actually come in."* [GPP Day 4 Part 2, original from GPP Phase 6 packaging video]

This is the most-quoted line across both tracks.

The bridge from GPP to UCC:

> *"Major CI is locked in via GPP. For the Early CI plus recurring-claim coverage, we'd layer a separate plan called UCC — Ultimate Critical Cover. Different appointment, but it pairs naturally."* [GPP Day 4 Part 2]

```

Bucket 4: ACCIDENT                  → Solitaire PA
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

GPP and UCC sit in Bucket 2 together. **Two-appointment discipline.**

## UCC + APA — the protection-floor-then-accumulation sequence

> *"The good thing about APA and the term rider is that if you pass away, you will get whatever value in the APA, plus the coverage of the term rider. UCC does not affect the term rider, even if you claim multiple times for UCC, it will not affect the term rider, will not affect the APA."* [Video 8]

Decoupled architecture: claim CI on UCC → APA value untouched. The investment side keeps growing regardless of CI claims.

### The decoupling principle (from Video 4)

> *"Instead of having all three merged together where one claim destroys everything, let's decouple your coverage. You get a pure CI plan where you can claim as many times as you want — and it doesn't affect your death coverage or your savings at all."* [Video 4]

This is the framing for any restructure conversation. Don't lead with "cancel your existing plan" — lead with "decouple."

## When NOT to restructure

If the prospect's existing plan has:
- A claim history (new UCC underwriting may decline)
- Pre-existing conditions that surfaced after the original underwriting (similarly)
- A favourable legacy premium rate that won't be matched

— DON'T restructure. Layer UCC on top of the existing plan instead. The discipline is to add coverage, not lose existing coverage.

## Cross-sell discipline rules

1. **Never bundle UCC into the GPP close.** Two separate appointments. Always.
2. **Always run the discovery questions before pitching.** Prospects who have GPP often think they have full CI cover — they don't.
3. **Use the canonical packaging line verbatim.** It's the most-quoted source line — landing it word-for-word builds conviction.
4. **Don't restructure without due diligence.** Health history, claim history, legacy rates all matter.
5. **Pair with APA for the floor-then-accumulation sequence** when the prospect has cashflow to redirect after restructure.
