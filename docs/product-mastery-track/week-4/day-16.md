# Part 1 — UCC in 5 Minutes: What It Is, Who It's For, Why It Exists

> **The one idea for Part 1:** UCC is a regular-premium, non-participating CI plan covering 73 critical illnesses across three severity stages. Three plan terms (To 65 / 75 / 85), no cash value, but unlimited claims for major-stage CI with a 12-month reset between claims — the Ultimate Reset Benefit.

By the end of Part 1 you should be able to: state the three plan terms, name the 42/35/73 condition counts cold, distinguish UCC from GPP/PLP single-claim CI in one sentence, and recognise the 4 trigger conditions that make UCC the right product.

---

## What UCC actually is

A SGD-denominated, **non-participating regular-premium critical illness plan** issued by AIA. Three plan terms: **To Age 65, To Age 75, To Age 85** [Product Summary p.1].

Distinguishing features:
- **No cash value** — pure CI, no surrender benefit, no maturity benefit
- **150 medical conditions** across three stages: 42 Early Stage, 35 Intermediate Stage, 73 Major Stage [Brochure p.13]
- **Unlimited claims for major-stage CI** with 100% reset of Current Insured Amount 12 months after the latest admitted claim — the **Ultimate Reset Benefit** [Product Summary p.7]

The line that opens every UCC conversation:

> *"It's basically just a recurring critical illness plan without any cash value, with so it purely just covers for the CI part. So it doesn't cover for death, the answer is no, it's only going to cover for the critical illness side of things."* [Video 4]

Then the differentiation:

> *"The main selling point is the unlimited claims for major stages and 100% reset of your coverage every year."* [Video 3]

## UCC vs single-claim CI plans (the distinction that matters)

This is the gap UCC closes:

| Single-claim CI (GPP, PLP, SFT, bundled whole-life) | UCC |
|---|---|
| One CI claim — coverage exhausted | Unlimited major-stage claims |
| Claim CI → death cover reduced (accelerated) | CI claim does NOT affect death cover (separate product) |
| Claim CI → surrender value gone | No cash value to lose |
| Same illness recurrence — not covered | Enhancer rider covers 5 named relapse conditions |

The script:

> *"Once you claim once, can you claim again? The answer is no. If you claim once, can you claim for death? The answer is no as well. If you claim once, can you claim for savings? The answer is no."* [Video 4]

Then the bridge:

> *"That's why UCC exists — it decouples CI from death and savings, and it lets you claim again."*

## The 4 trigger conditions for UCC

Use these as your internal qualification — if at least one is true, UCC is on the table:

| Trigger | Why it points to UCC |
|---|---|
| No Early CI cover | UCC has 42 Early-Stage conditions native |
| No multi-claim CI cover | UCC's Ultimate Reset Benefit |
| Existing CI bundled with death/savings (one-claim collapse) | UCC decouples |
| Coverage shortfall vs 5-year income-replacement need | UCC layers on top |

Every UCC sale starts with at least one of these gaps. If none apply, UCC may not be the right product right now.

## The 5-minute intro script

> *"This is AIA Ultimate Critical Cover — UCC. The reason we layer this on top of your existing CI cover: three things.*
>
> *One — it's pure CI. No cash value, no death cover, no savings tied in. That decoupling matters: if you claim CI, your other policies are untouched.*
>
> *Two — multi-claim. After each major-stage CI claim, your full coverage resets to 100% twelve months later. So you can claim again, and again. Most plans pay once and stop.*
>
> *Three — 150 conditions across three stages. Early-stage detection nowadays catches things at year zero — UCC pays at that early stage too, while bundled plans usually only pay on major stage.*
>
> *Three terms — To 65, To 75, or To 85. Which makes more sense for you?"*

That's 60 seconds. End with the question — they pick the term, the close has started.

# Part 2 — The Signature Pitch Concept: The Multi-Claim Staircase

> **The one idea for Part 2:** One picture closes UCC. The heart-condition progression visual showing claim → 12-month wait → reset → claim again is the entire conviction in one diagram. While walking the prospect up the staircase you simultaneously beat competitors (Prudential's "related illness" block, Great Eastern's three-claim cap).

By the end of Part 2 you should be able to: draw the staircase from memory, recite the heart-condition progression script, deploy the per-episode caps without notes, and use the staircase to crush single-claim competitor comparisons.

---

## The staircase visual (draw it on whiteboard)

```
Major Stage Claim 1: Coronary Artery Disease     Claim S$100,000
                              ↓
                        12 months wait
                              ↓
Major Stage Claim 2: Coronary Artery Bypass      Claim S$100,000
                              ↓
                        12 months wait
                              ↓
Major Stage Claim 3: Heart Attack                Claim S$100,000
                              ↓
                        12 months wait
                              ↓
Major Stage Claim 4: Heart Transplant            Claim S$100,000
                              ↓
                        Total claimed: S$400,000+
                        from S$100,000 base coverage
```

After each step, ask:

> *"Can you still claim again? Yes."*

The repetition lands the multi-claim mechanic. The brochure illustrates this with **Robert at age 43** — 4 separate claims totalling S$500,000 across 8 years from a single S$100,000 base [Brochure p.10]. Reference this when the prospect asks for proof.

## The verbatim Phase-2 script

> *"The moment you claim, let's just say for coronary artery disease, you can claim that first amount, $100,000, $200,000, and after you claim once, the great thing about this plan is that it will help you to claim again a second time, you can claim a second time for coronary artery bypass, as long as you wait for one more year."* [Video 5]

> *"Even after multiple times of claims, can you still claim again? The answer is yes — you can claim again."* [Video 5]

> *"All you need is just one picture. That's all you need."* [Video 5]

The brochure framing:

> *"It's ultimate assurance knowing your coverage fully resets every 12 months from the last claim. With this benefit, you are covered against different critical illnesses with unlimited claims for major stages."* [Brochure p.3]

## The per-episode caps (memorise)

| Stage | Cap |
|---|---|
| Early Stage | S$350,000 per life across all UCC policies (same insured) [PS p.3] |
| Intermediate Stage | S$350,000 per life [Brochure p.13] |
| Combined Early + Intermediate | 500% of coverage amount in aggregate [PS p.4] |
| Angioplasty & Other Invasive Treatment for Coronary Artery (Major) | 10% of Current Insured Amount, capped at S$25,000 [PS p.3] |
| Survival period | 7 days from diagnosis or surgery date [PS p.3] |

## Differentiation while walking the staircase

When a prospect cross-shops, drop the competitor mechanics into the staircase walk:

### vs Prudential

> *"For Prudential, you cannot claim because it's a related CI. Coronary artery disease versus bypass versus heart attack — as long as it's related to the same area, all the future claims related to heart conditions are not claimable anymore."* [Video 5]

UCC has no "related illness" block — different illness, different claim, regardless of body area.

### vs Great Eastern

> *"For Great Eastern, you can only claim if it is less than three times. After three times, the policy will cease."* [Video 5]

UCC has no claim count cap on major-stage claims. Reset, claim, reset, claim — for as long as the policy is in force.

### Sample pricing benchmark (illustrative, time-bound)

UCC vs competitors on S$100,000 cover [Video 1]:
- ~11% cheaper than Prudential
- ~6% cheaper than Great Eastern
- ~29% cheaper than Manulife
- ~22% cheaper than Singlife

UCC covers 150 conditions vs Great Eastern Great Critical Cover ~53 conditions per Video 1.

**Always verify current iPOS quote before citing competitor pricing — these figures move.**

## Same-illness recurrence — Enhancer matters

The base UCC is unlimited across **different** CIs but pays only **once per illness**. Same-illness recurrence (e.g. cancer relapse) requires the **UCC Enhancer rider** — covered in detail Day 3 Part 1.

The verbatim:

> *"UCC's unlimited payout does not mean unlimited claims for the same illness. The reset is not recurring for the same condition."* [Video 2]

> *"The most common CIs — cancer, heart attack — often recur. But the base plan only covers once. That's why the Enhancer matters."* [Video 2]

The Enhancer adds 5 named relapse conditions at 50% of cover per episode (capped at 100% total) plus a one-time Catastrophic 100% top-up that doesn't reduce base cover.

## When NOT to pull out the staircase

- **Prospect already understands multi-claim mechanics** — skip to numbers
- **Time-boxed appointment <15 min** — staircase needs 3 minutes; truncate to verbal version
- **Prospect's primary need is death cover, not CI** — wrong product, pivot to GPP or term

## Quiz
1. **How many medical conditions does UCC cover, and how are they distributed across the three stages?**
- A) 100 conditions: 30 Early / 30 Intermediate / 40 Major
- B) 150 conditions: 42 Early / 35 Intermediate / 73 Major ✓
- C) 150 conditions: 50 Early / 50 Intermediate / 50 Major
- D) 120 conditions: 35 Early / 42 Intermediate / 43 Major

**Why:** UCC covers 150 conditions split 42 Early / 35 Intermediate / 73 Major Stage [Brochure p.13].

2. **Which of these is NOT one of UCC's three available plan terms?**
- A) To Age 65
- B) To Age 75
- C) To Age 85
- D) To Age 99 ✓

**Why:** UCC is offered only as To Age 65, To Age 75, or To Age 85 [Product Summary p.1].

3. **What is the Ultimate Reset Benefit?**
- A) A 6-month reset of premiums after a claim
- B) Restoration of Current Insured Amount to 100% twelve months after the latest admitted claim ✓
- C) A reset of the survival period after each claim
- D) An automatic upgrade of the plan term after the first major-stage claim

**Why:** Twelve months after the latest admitted claim, the Current Insured Amount is restored to 100%, with no cap on the number of times the benefit is granted while the basic policy is in force [PS p.7].

4. **What does it mean that UCC is "pure CI"?**
- A) It only pays out if the diagnosis is in its earliest stage
- B) It has no cash value, no surrender benefit, and no maturity benefit — covering only critical illness ✓
- C) It only covers cancer-related conditions
- D) It is sold without any optional riders

**Why:** UCC is a non-participating regular-premium CI plan with no cash value, no surrender benefit, and no maturity benefit; claiming on it leaves death cover and savings policies untouched [PS p.1, Video 4].

5. **What is the per-life cap on combined Early + Intermediate stage payouts for the same critical illness across all UCC policies?**
- A) S$100,000
- B) S$250,000
- C) S$350,000 ✓
- D) S$500,000

**Why:** Combined Early + Intermediate stage payouts for the same CI on the same insured are capped at S$350,000 across all UCC policies [Brochure p.14].

6. **Angioplasty and Other Invasive Treatment for Coronary Artery is paid at what level?**
- A) 100% of Current Insured Amount
- B) 50% of Current Insured Amount, capped at S$100,000
- C) 10% of Current Insured Amount, capped at S$25,000 ✓
- D) 25% of Current Insured Amount, no cap

**Why:** The major-stage payout for Angioplasty is 10% of Current Insured Amount, capped at S$25,000 per episode [PS p.3].

7. **What is the survival period that applies to all UCC claims?**
- A) 14 days from diagnosis
- B) 7 days from diagnosis or surgery date ✓
- C) 30 days from diagnosis
- D) No survival period required

**Why:** The survival period is 7 days from the date of diagnosis or surgical procedure for any claim to be payable [PS p.3].

8. **A prospect says: "I already have CI in my whole-life plan — why do I need UCC?" Which is the strongest immediate response from the script?**
- A) "Whole-life plans are obsolete; you should cancel and replace."
- B) "Once you claim once, can you claim again? No. If you claim once, can you still claim for death? No. UCC decouples CI from death and savings." ✓
- C) "Whole-life premiums are too expensive — UCC saves money."

**Why:** The verbatim discovery script surfaces the bundled-collapse problem by walking the prospect through each "no" answer, then bridges to UCC's decoupled design [Video 4].

9. **While walking a prospect up the staircase, they say "Prudential's plan looks similar." What is the correct competitor mechanic to drop in?**
- A) Prudential's plan caps total claims at three before the policy ceases
- B) Prudential blocks future claims that are "related" to the same body area, so heart-related conditions become non-claimable after the first heart claim ✓
