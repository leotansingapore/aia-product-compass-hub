# Part 1 — The Numbers — Premiums, Illustrations, Returns, Fees, Charges

> **The one idea for today:** APA is *deliberately* structured to look bad in years 1–10 and great from year 11 onward. The 3.9% supplementary charge ends at year 10. The 5% / 8% bonuses pay forever. The break-even crossover is around year 34 at $12k/year premium — and after that, AIA is effectively paying the client. Once you can draw this curve, the "fees are too high" objection collapses.

By the end of today you should be able to: state every key number from memory, walk through the break-even math on a whiteboard, linearize any projection into "$X/month of effective passive income," and present the supplementary charge as a feature, not a bug.

---

## The structural inversion (this is the heart)

Most FCs lose APA cases because they treat the 3.9% supplementary charge defensively. Don't. Lean into it:

> *"After 10 years there's no more supplementary charge. But the bonuses are perpetual. The trick for this plan is to really hold long-term."*

Draw this on the whiteboard if you have to:

```
Years 1–10:    Charges: 3.9% p.a.    Bonuses: 5%–75% welcome (years 1–3)
Year 10+:      Charges: 0%           Bonuses: 5% special perpetual
Year 21+:      Charges: 0%           Bonuses: 8% special perpetual
```

The whole plan is engineered for the long-hold client. If your prospect won't hold 10 years, **APA is the wrong product** — say so.

---

## The bonus mechanics (memorise)

### Welcome Bonus

- Paid across years 1–3
- **5% to 75%** of annualized premium
- Higher premium tier + longer IIP = higher bonus
- Tier breakpoints: $4.8k / $7.2k / $12k annual premium

### Special Bonus

- **5% per year from year 10 onward** (10-year IIP)
- **8% per year from year 21 onward**
- Calculated on **annualized premium**, not account value (this is the most-missed detail)
- Perpetual while premiums are paid

The script that prevents the most common confusion:

> *"5% of your annualized premium, not 5% of the account value. Please take note, there's a difference."*

Most prospects assume "5%" means 5% of their pot. It doesn't — it means 5% of their *annual premium*, added to the pot. On a $12k/year premium, that's an extra $600/year credited from year 10 onward. Forever.

## The supplementary charge

| Detail | Value |
|---|---|
| Rate | **3.9% per annum** of account value |
| Frequency | Monthly deduction |
| Duration | First 10 policy years only (10-yr IIP). Drops to 0% after that |
| What it covers | Distribution costs (commission), ongoing servicing, AIA admin |

The disclosure script that builds trust:

> *"Yes, there is a 3.9% charge in the first 10 years. After year 10, it goes to zero. The trade-off is the bonuses: you get welcome bonus in the first 3 years, then 5% special bonus from year 10, then 8% from year 21. The charges are front-loaded. The bonuses are forever."*

Don't hide this. Lead with it. Saying it out loud is the credibility move.

## The illustration discipline (4% vs 8%)

AIA's iPulse / Sales Illustrator shows two projection rates. Your discipline:

- **Anchor on 8%.** That's the historical average for the Elite Adventurous portfolio.
- **Show 4% as the conservative floor.** Never present 4% as the headline.
- **Never present 8% as guaranteed.** Always disclose: "It's not guaranteed 8% all the time — it can be a bit higher, it can be a bit lower."

The exact phrasing:

> *"Don't look at the 4% — 4% is really on the conservative side. Most of the time our portfolio has grown roughly 8%. It's not guaranteed 8%, it can be higher, it can be lower."*

The 4% number stays in the illustration as compliance protection. Your job is to direct attention to 8% while making the disclosure honestly.

## The linearization technique

This is the move that makes a 45-year projection feel real to a 22-year-old.

The standard close illustration (memorise these numbers):

```
Age 20, $200/month, Elite Adventurous, 8% projected
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
Total premium paid (over 45 years): $96,000
Projected value at age 65:           $411,600
Net gain:                            $315,600
```

Now linearize:

```
$315,600 gain ÷ 45 years ÷ 12 months = ~$583/month
```

The script:

> *"For every $200 you put in, you're effectively earning back $583 a month in the long run. That's much more impactful than quoting a lump sum 40 years away."*

A 22-year-old can't picture $411,600. They CAN picture $583/month of passive income for life. Always linearize.

> *"Linearize the returns — take the maturity result, minus what they put in, divide over the years, divide over the months."*

## The total cost of distribution

When the prospect probes total fees, the disclosure that wins:

> *"Total cost over the 45 years is $6,194. Divide this by 45 years — every month is actually just about $11. Very cheap."*

Then the reframe:

> *"You're hiring an investment consultant for $11 a month."*

This is the honesty-as-credibility move. You're showing the absolute number, then making it feel sane.

## The break-even math (the close on "fees")

This is the chart you draw when a sharp prospect says "the fees are too high." Calculate the year where cumulative bonuses overtake cumulative charges:

| Annual Premium | Break-even year |
|---|---|
| $2,400/year (minimum) | ~Year 40 |
| $12,000/year | ~Year 34 |

After break-even, the plan is **net-positive from bonuses alone** — AIA is paying the client.

The script:

> *"Look at the math: at $12k/year premium, by year 34 the cumulative bonuses are bigger than the cumulative charges. From that point onward, AIA is effectively paying YOU. That's why this plan only works long-term — you have to hold past the break-even."*

This is the line that separates FCs who can defend the plan from FCs who can't.

## Surrender, withdrawal, and pause schedules

These are the numbers RNF will sample-audit you on. Know them cold.

### Surrender charge schedule (10-year IIP)

| Year | Charge |
|---|---|
| 1–2 | 100% (gets back nothing) |
| 3 | 80% |
| 4 | 70% |
| 5 | 60% |
| 6 | 50% |
| 7–9 | (sliding down) |
| 10 | 5% |
| 11+ | 0% |

### Partial withdrawal

- **Min withdrawal:** $1,000
- **Min remaining policy value:** $10,000
- **Charge year 3 onward:** ~$53 per $1,000 withdrawn (5.3% effective)
- **After year 10:** drops to 0%

### Premium pass

- 1 pass per 5 annual premiums paid
- 10-yr IIP = 1 pass · 15-yr = 2 passes · 20-yr = 3 passes
- Pause up to 12 months without charge

### Premium reduction charge

- 70% of reduction value at year 5
- 60% of reduction value at year 15
- (you almost never need this — most clients increase, not decrease)

## The death benefit math

The capital guarantee on death:

> *"No matter how low the portfolio goes, you at least get the full amount that you put in. There's some capital guarantee in case of market downturn."*

Specifically:

```
Death benefit = HIGHER of:
  • 100% of total regular premiums paid + top-ups − withdrawals
  • Current policy value
```

Plus, in the **first 2 policy years only**, accidental death within 90 days of accident pays an **additional 100% of total regular premiums.**

This is the "what if I die in 5 years?" answer in 2 sentences:

> *"If something happens to you, your beneficiary gets at least everything you put in — even if the market is down 50%. In the first two years, accidental death pays double."*

# Part 2 — Top 5 Client Questions — Crisp, Scripted Answers

> **The one idea for today:** Every APA prospect asks the same five questions in some form. Today you memorise the crisp answer to each — number, analogy, micro-commit. Tomorrow you face the harder *objections.* These are the questions; tomorrow are the attacks.

By the end of today you should be able to: deliver each of the 5 answers in under 30 seconds, end each answer with a question that hands the conversation back, and recognise which Day 7 objection is hiding behind a Day 6 question.

---

## The pattern (use it for every answer)

```
1. State the number or fact (10 seconds)
2. Add the analogy or reframe (10 seconds)
3. End with a question that builds micro-commit (10 seconds)
```

Memorise the pattern, then memorise the 5 answers.

---

## Question 1 — "Why ILP and not just a unit trust or fund?"

The answer:

> *"Three things that you cannot get from a direct fund: one — the welcome bonus in the first 3 years. Two — the loyalty bonus from year 10 onwards. Three — a capital-guaranteed death benefit that pays your beneficiary even in a market crash. Plus — and this is real — you get me. I'm in your corner for the next 30+ years.*
>
> *Direct funds give you the investment. APA gives you the wrapper that *adds* on top of the investment. Does that distinction make sense?"*

The closing question is the micro-commit. They'll say "yes."

The verbatim AIA framing:

> *"It's a pure investment plan — none of the fees go into the insurance part. There's no insurance charges. It's 100% investment, the full amount is being invested."*

---

## Question 2 — "Can I take money out anytime?"

The answer:

> *"Technically yes — partial withdrawal is allowed. But the design is for long-term. If you withdraw in the first 10 years, there are charges. After year 10, no charge. Minimum withdrawal is $1,000, and you need to keep at least $10,000 in the plan.*
>
> *Honestly? My job is to tell you not to. Every withdrawal in the first 10 years is AIA earning a fee from you. The plan is built to compound. So treat it like your retirement account, not your emergency fund. Got an emergency fund separately, right?"*

That last sentence is the micro-commit AND a fact-find — you're checking they have an actual emergency fund.

The verbatim source:

> *"You can withdraw, but try not to withdraw in the first 10 years — there will be some charges."*
>
> *"Just to make things simple, I just tell them you're not supposed to pause the plan in that 10 years."*
>
> *"Best is don't even cancel or withdraw anything at all because there will be some charges."*

---

## Question 3 — "What if I die before maturity?"

The answer:

> *"Capital guarantee. Even if the market is down 50% the day you die, your beneficiary gets back at minimum everything you put in. The exact wording: the higher of total premiums paid plus top-ups minus withdrawals, OR current policy value. Whichever is higher.*
>
> *And — bonus feature — if it's accidental death within the first 2 policy years, your beneficiary gets an *additional* 100% of premiums paid. So if you'd put in $20,000 and got hit by a car, your beneficiary gets $40,000.*
>
> *Have you set up your beneficiary nomination already? If not, we'll do it as part of this paperwork."*

The closing question moves you into Phase 7 paperwork mode.

> *"No matter how low the portfolio goes, you at least get the full amount that you put in. There's some capital guarantee in case of market downturn."*

---

## Question 4 — "What if I can't pay one year? Can I pause?"

The answer:

> *"Yes. The plan has a feature called Premium Pass — you get one pass for every 5 years of premiums paid. So a 10-year IIP gives you 1 pass, a 15-year gives you 2, and a 20-year gives you 3. Each pass lets you skip up to 12 months of premium with zero charge.*
>
> *Beyond that, there's also Premium Holiday — but it carries a charge, so we treat it as a last resort.*
>
> *Most clients never use either. But it's nice to know it's there. Your salary's stable for the next few years, right?"*

That last sentence quietly anchors stability. They'll say yes.

---

## Question 5 — "Can I take dividends now? I want passive income today."

The answer:

> *"Possible — from year 10 or 11, you can switch into dividend mode using GDIF or AGDIF. Yield's about 5–7% per year on the account value.*
>
> *But honest math: if you have $10,000 in there, that's about $600 a year — $50 a month. You're earning $6k/month right now. Is $50/month meaningful to you?*
>
> *The smarter move is reinvest. Let it compound. Take dividends when you actually need them — at retirement. Make sense?"*

The verbatim:

> *"Is $600 per year significant for you? If you're earning 6k per month and you're getting $600 per year of dividends, I do not think there is any significance in taking out the dividends now."*
>
> *"Would you prefer dividends now when you don't need it, or in the future when you actually need it?"*

That second question almost always closes the dividend objection in 5 seconds. They'll always say "future."

## The riders question (sometimes asked, sometimes you bring it up)

Even if not asked, you should plant the seed during the answer to Question 3:

> *"Speaking of protection — APA also has 5 optional riders we can layer on. The most common is ECPWP — that's Enhanced Critical Payor Waiver. If you get a major critical illness or become disabled, AIA waives your future premiums but the plan keeps growing. We can talk about that on the next visit."*

That's a Day 8 cross-sell teaser — drops the seed for the next appointment.

---

## The "what if AIA goes bankrupt?" question (rare but loaded)

If asked:

> *"AIA is one of the largest life insurers in Asia, regulated by MAS in Singapore. Policies are also protected under the Policy Owners' Protection Scheme up to certain limits. Practically — AIA's been operating since 1919 and is publicly listed in Hong Kong with a market cap north of US$70B. The risk is non-zero but it's not the risk we should be worrying about."*

Don't dwell. Answer factually, move on.

---

## The "what about taxes?" question

> *"In Singapore, investment growth inside the policy is not taxed — there's no capital gains tax in Singapore. When you withdraw or surrender, no tax. Death benefit to beneficiary, no tax. CPF: APA is funded with cash, not CPF, so it's outside the CPF system entirely.*
>
> *The exception: if you ever move overseas and become tax-resident in another country, that country may tax it. We handle that case-by-case if it ever comes up."*

For US-residency or US-citizen prospects, flag immediately and refer to specialised cross-border advice — APA's not the right vehicle for them.

## The handover-to-day-7 transition

If a prospect asks Question 1, 2, 3, 4, or 5 — they're curious, they're not objecting. Today's answers handle them.

But if they ask:

- *"Why not just buy S&P500?"*
- *"Why not just DIY ETFs?"*
- *"The fees are too high"*
- *"Other companies have lower charges"*
- *"I want my dividends now (not later)"*

— those are **objections**, not questions. They need the conviction stories from Day 7. Recognise the difference: a question opens a door; an objection slams one shut.
