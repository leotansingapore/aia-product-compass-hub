# Day 2 — Fact-Find + 7-Phase Pitch Flow

> **Today's frame:** Pre-fit the recommendation through a 5-minute fact-find, then run a 7-phase appointment in 20–30 minutes where every phase ends with a client micro-commitment.

This is a merged module — Part 1 covers the first half (originally Day 03), Part 2 covers the second half (originally Day 04). Estimated read + drill time: ~55 minutes. Take it in two sittings if you need to.

---

# Part 1 — The Fact-Find — What to Ask Before Pitching APA

> **The one idea for today:** APA is sold by *pre-fitting* the recommendation to the client's life — not by leading with product. The fact-find is intelligence gathering that doubles as a follow-up engine. Every birthday, ORD date, university start, and wedding date you capture becomes a calendar wish; every wish becomes a re-open.

By the end of today you should be able to: run a 5-minute fact-find that captures life stage, cash flow, risk appetite, existing portfolio, and 3+ calendar milestones — without it feeling like an interrogation.

---

## The fact-find has two jobs

1. **Pre-fit the recommendation.** A 22-year-old NSF and a 35-year-old project manager both deserve APA, but the IIP, premium tier, fund route, and rider stack are different. The fact-find tells you which.
2. **Build the follow-up calendar.** Every meaningful date you capture is a future re-open. Birthdays, graduation, ORD, posting, marriage, kids — all of them.

If you finish a fact-find without capturing 3+ dates for your calendar, you didn't fact-find. You interrogated.

---

## The five blocks (do them in this order)

### 1. Life stage + key dates (capture into calendar immediately)

The soft way to ask birthday:

> *"How old are you this year? Have you turned [age] already? When are you turning [age]?"*

That sequence — without ever using the word "birthday" — gets you the date naturally. Drop it into your CRM the second they say it.

For young adults / NSF, also capture:

- POP date · posting · ORD date
- University start / graduation
- First job / project review cycle
- Anticipated wedding date or relationship milestones

For 30-somethings:

- Marriage anniversary
- Children's birthdays / school transitions (P1, secondary, JC, uni)
- Mortgage milestones
- Job-change anniversaries

**Rule:** capture, then *use it*. Send a Happy Birthday on the day. Send a "happy ORD" on the day. The discipline turns the fact-find into a 12-month referral pipeline.

> *"If you wish them and they're not a client yet, it's going to be a lot easier to close next time."*

### 2. Cash flow snapshot

The two questions that anchor every APA recommendation:

> *"How much are you earning and spending now?"*
>
> *"How much have you saved up in your bank so far?"*

Get specific. *"About $5k a month"* is good. *"$5,800 gross, $1,800 expenses, $4k saveable"* is better. The more specific the number, the more confident your recommendation can be.

For young adults / NSF, the working benchmark:

| Profile | Typical income | Typical save |
|---|---|---|
| NSF | ~$800/mo allowance | $200–600/mo saveable |
| Junior PMET (1–2 yrs in) | $4k–6k | $1.5k–2.5k |
| Mid-career PMET (5+ yrs) | $7k–12k | $2k–4k |

Use these to sense-check whether the client is over- or under-stating their save rate. If a junior PMET claims "$3k a month into investments," ask gently — most aren't.

### 3. Risk appetite

> *"There's a few kinds of portfolios that we can choose from — low-risk, medium-risk, high-risk. For young adults, usually I prefer them to go with a slightly higher-risk portfolio."*

Notice the soft frame at the end — you're already nudging the recommendation. For young clients (under 35), default to **Elite Adventurous.** For 35+ with kids, default to **Elite Balanced.** For 50+ approaching retirement, default to **Elite Conservative.**

Don't ask *"what's your risk tolerance, 1 to 10?"* — that's a survey question. Ask:

> *"If your portfolio dropped 30% in a year, would you sell, hold, or top up?"*

That answer is your real risk read.

### 4. Existing portfolio (what they already do)

This is the question that determines which **Day 7 objection** you'll need to pre-handle:

> *"Are you already investing? Stocks, ETFs, robo-advisors? Tell me what you're doing now."*

Translate their answer into your prep:

| Their answer | The objection you'll face |
|---|---|
| "I buy S&P500 / SPY / CSPX" | Day 7 Objection 1 — Perfect / Imperfect / Partially Perfect |
| "I use StashAway / Endowus / Syfe" | Day 7 Objection 2 — perpetual fee math |
| "I pick stocks myself" | Day 7 Objection 2 — DIY exhaustion + tool vs service |
| "I have an old ILP from another company" | Day 7 Objection 4 — competitor charges comparison |
| "I just keep cash in DBS / OCBC / UOB" | Day 2 retirement reverse-engineer is your whole pitch |

**You should know within 2 minutes of meeting the prospect which objection arc you're running.**

### 5. The goal anchor

Every APA recommendation must hook to one of these life goals:

- Retirement
- Children's education
- House upgrade / second property
- Wedding / family planning
- Financial independence by [target age]

The question that gets you the goal:

> *"If everything goes right financially over the next 30 years — what does that look like for you?"*

The answer is the goal you'll anchor the Day 2 retirement-income reverse-engineer to.

---

## The bank-and-cadence close-setup

End every fact-find with this question — even if you haven't pitched yet:

> *"By the way, which bank do you use for your salary credit?"*

Two reasons. One: you need it for GIRO setup at the close. Two: asking it now positions implementation as inevitable. By the time you reach Phase 6 (close), the bank account is already on the table.

Then the cadence question:

> *"Most people prefer monthly debits — easier on cash flow than annual. Does monthly work for you?"*

> *"Most clients monthly, not yearly — cash flow tighter."*

That's the default close cadence.

---

## The "guess your number" check (advanced)

For sharper prospects, sneak in this check before Day 2's whiteboard:

> *"How much do you think you'd need in your retirement pot to live comfortably from 65 to 85?"*

If they say a number under $1M, your Day 2 reverse-engineer is going to land HARD. If they say "$2M" or higher, they're already half-convinced — your Day 2 will be quick agreement rather than shock.

This calibrates how hard you need to swing the contrast.

---

## What a complete fact-find looks like

5–8 minutes max. You should walk away with:

- ✅ Date of birth + 3+ other calendar milestones
- ✅ Income / expenses / savings (specific numbers)
- ✅ Risk read (the 30% drawdown question)
- ✅ Existing portfolio (which objection arc you're running)
- ✅ Life goal (what to anchor the recommendation to)
- ✅ Bank account + monthly cadence preference

If you walk away missing any of these, you'll fumble the close. Go back and ask.

---

## The fact-find is also the close build-up

Every question you ask is also a **micro-commitment** opportunity:

> *"You said you save $1,500 a month — that's already in the top 20% of your peers. Most people your age save zero. So you've got the discipline. The question is just where to direct it."*

That sentence is a fact-find moment AND a setup. You're not selling yet — but you're already framing them as a saver who needs *direction*, which is what APA provides.

## Drill (Part 1)

1. **Run a 5-minute fact-find on yourself** out loud. Capture all 5 blocks. Notice which questions you stumble on.
2. **Pull up your last 3 first-meeting prospects.** For each, list which of the 5 blocks you missed. That's why you didn't close (or had to come back).
3. **Pre-write your "30% drawdown" question** — practice phrasing it in your own words 3 different ways. Pick the one that sounds most like you.
4. **Calendar 3 birthdays** of prospects-not-yet-clients in your phone today. Set them as recurring annual reminders.

# Part 2 — The 1-1 Pitch Flow — Seven Phases in 20–30 Minutes

> **The one idea for today:** A complete APA appointment is engineered, not improvised. Seven phases. Each phase ends with a small client "yes." By the time you reach Phase 6, the close is already made — you're just collecting the signature.

By the end of today you should be able to: name all 7 phases in order without notes, time each phase to within 1 minute of target, recognise which micro-commitment each phase is hunting for, and run the full appointment in under 30 minutes.

---

## Why this matters

The two ways FCs lose APA cases:

1. **Skipping the problem build-up** — leaping to "$200/month buys you this plan" before the prospect feels why they need it. The brain rejects solutions to problems it hasn't felt.
2. **Running over time** — a 60-minute first appointment is a lost appointment. Energy and attention die at minute 35.

The 7-phase flow solves both. Each phase has a hard time budget and a target micro-commitment.

> *"Telling someone to save $200 a month is not really a solution — you have to contrast it with a problem first."*

---

## The 7 phases

```
Phase 1    Phase 2        Phase 3     Phase 4      Phase 5     Phase 6    Phase 7
Rapport →  Credibility →  Problem  →  Solution  →  Numbers  →  Close  →   Implementation
```

| Phase | Target time | Target micro-commit |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Rapport + milestone capture | 5 min | "Yes, that's my birthday / ORD" |
| 2. Credibility | 2 min | "Looks legit" (no pushback on you) |
| 3. Problem build | 5 min | "Yeah, that retirement number is scary" |
| 4. Solution | 5 min | "OK, outsourcing makes sense" |
| 5. Numbers | 5 min | "Monthly is fine" / "Option B sounds smarter" |
| 6. Close | 3 min | "Let's do it" |
| 7. Implementation | 3 min | SMS auth complete, calendar set |
| **Total** | **~28 min** | 8+ small yeses |

---

## Phase 1 — Rapport + milestone capture (5 min)

This is your Day 3 fact-find compressed into 5 minutes. Capture:

- Birthday + 3+ calendar dates
- Income / expenses / savings
- Existing portfolio
- Goal anchor

The framing that opens the room:

> *"Before I show you anything, I want to understand your situation first. Do you mind if I ask a few questions about your current finances?"*

This signals you're not here to sell — you're here to fit. The prospect relaxes immediately.

**Phase 1 micro-commit target:** the prospect actively shares numbers and dates without hesitation.

---

## Phase 2 — Credibility (2 min)

Don't talk about yourself. Hand over a tangible artefact that does the talking. Options:

- A book (e.g., *Financial Fitness Forever* by Paul Merriman)
- A short intro video (60–90 seconds)
- A printed one-pager about your client portfolio shape

The book move is especially effective for prospects who've never met an FC:

> *"This book contains over 100 different tips and things you need to avoid when it comes to investing and saving. All this is so important because just by having a change of 1–2% in your portfolio can mean the difference between getting $1.2 million or $1.6 million in your portfolio. It's a huge compounding effect."*

The book becomes the *authority* — you're just the guide. The prospect keeps the book to read between this meeting and the next.

**Phase 2 micro-commit target:** the prospect accepts the book / watches the video without resistance.

---

## Phase 3 — Problem build (5 min)

This is where you draw the **Day 2 retirement reverse-engineer.** Make them guess first. Reveal the $2.3M number. Pause.

The line you say *during* the silence after the reveal:

> *"The best time to start was yesterday. The next best time to start is today."*

Don't fill the silence with talking. Let the number sit. The prospect's silence is them processing — interrupting it kills the moment.

**Phase 3 micro-commit target:** prospect verbalises the problem back to you. *"Yeah, $2 million is a lot. I haven't really thought about it."* That's the green light to move on.

---

## Phase 4 — Solution (5 min)

Now you introduce APA — but you frame it through the contrast first:

> *"Always give them this sense of comparison. The brain always learns things through comparison."*

Walk the four foundational rules (you'll use them again at Phase 6 for the close):

> *"There are four things you must do to be financially free:*
>
> *One — earn more.*
> *Two — spend less.*
> *Three — get covered with insurance.*
> *Four — automate your investing process.*
>
> *I can't really help you with one and two — that's on you. Three is for another conversation. But four — automating your investing — that's exactly what APA does."*

Then use the **container metaphor** from Day 1:

> *"Think of APA as a container. The container gives you 100% allocation, welcome bonus, special bonus from year 10, capital-guaranteed death benefit. The actual investments inside the container — that's where we customise to you."*

**Phase 4 micro-commit target:** *"OK, outsourcing makes sense."* Or a nod when you say "automate."

---

## Phase 5 — Numbers (5 min)

Open the iPulse / Sales Illustrator. Show **8% projected** with **4% as the conservative floor**. Never present 4% alone.

> *"Don't look at the 4% — 4% is really on the conservative side. Most of the time our portfolio has grown roughly 8%. It's not guaranteed 8%, it can be higher, it can be lower."*

Then linearize the number (this is the move that makes a 45-year projection feel real):

> *"For every $200 you put in, you're effectively earning back $583 a month in the long run. That's much more impactful than quoting a lump sum 40 years away."*

The standard close illustration to memorize:

| Variable | Value |
|---|---|
| Age | 20 |
| Premium | $200/month |
| Fund | Elite Adventurous |
| Projected return | 8% |
| Total premium paid (45 yrs) | $96,000 |
| Projected value at 65 | $411,600 |
| Net gain | $315,600 |
| Linearized monthly equivalent | ~$583/mo for life |

Then the 3-options ladder — let the client self-select:

> *"You can start at $200, $250, or $300 a month — which one feels right?"*

The three-option frame is a Phase 5 closing technique. They never reject all three.

> *"$50 more per month means about $600,000 more in projected returns. Just throwing it out there."*

That's the urgency anchor for upgrade.

**Phase 5 micro-commit targets:** *"Monthly is fine"* AND *"Option B sounds smarter"* (or whichever option they pick — what matters is that they pick).

---

## Phase 6 — Close (3 min)

The close is just the formal version of decisions already made. Wrap with the four rules from Phase 4:

> *"Recap: earn more — that's on you. Spend less — that's on you. Get insured — separate conversation. Automate investing — that's what we set up today."*

Then the option-A vs option-B frame:

> *"Option A: keep doing what you're doing. Save in cash, hope inflation is kind, hope you have the discipline for 40 years. Option B: automate $250 a month into APA, capital-guaranteed death benefit, bonuses from year 10, and you stop having to think about it.*
>
> *Which one would you prefer — option A or B?"*

They say B.

> *"Save first before you spend, and really just spend below your means."*

That's the bridge into Phase 7.

**Phase 6 micro-commit target:** *"Let's do option B."*

---

## Phase 7 — Implementation (3 min)

The execution sprint:

> *"Just help me to verify your name, your NRIC, contact number, email address — make sure it's correct."*

Then bank + cadence (you already captured this in Phase 1):

> *"Every month once the pay comes in, you'll just be debited on a monthly basis into the bank account. DBS, right?"*

Then SMS auth:

> *"After this, I'm just gonna send you this SMS for you to authenticate."*

Then calendar:

> *"We can review next time when you graduate from your university, in a few years time. I'll wish you on your birthday before that."*

That last sentence is the *handoff* into the post-close relationship. Birthday wish in [N] months. Graduation review in [N] years. The relationship doesn't end at sign-up.

**Phase 7 micro-commit target:** SMS authenticated, GIRO confirmed, calendar entries set.

---

## The pacing rule

If you're approaching the 30-minute mark and you're still in Phase 5, you're losing them. Compress the numbers, jump to the option-A/B close, and book the implementation for the next session. **Better to close in two visits than to lose in one.**

---

## What a single appointment yields (benchmark)

A senior FC running this flow at full discipline can do:

- 200+ APA cases per year
- ~$400K annual sales from APA alone
- 50%+ close rate on first appointments

That's the benchmark. The flow above is calibrated to it.

## Drill (Part 2)

1. **Recite the 7 phases out loud** — name, time budget, micro-commit. Do it 3 times until the names come without thinking.
2. **Pick a real prospect** (or simulate). Run Phases 1–4 in front of a mirror. Time it. Target: 17 minutes total. Most FCs run 22+ on the first try — find your bloat.
3. **Memorise the standard close numbers:** $200/mo at age 20, 8% projected, $96k in, $411,600 at 65, $315,600 gain, $583/mo linearized. Recite without looking.
4. **Practice the option-A/B close** in 3 different tonalities — neutral, slightly playful, slightly serious. Pick the one that sounds most like you.
