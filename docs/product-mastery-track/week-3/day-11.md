# Day 1 — GPP Foundations: What It Is + The Rocket-Ship Diagram

> **Today's frame:** GPP is AIA's participating whole-life plan engineered for the prospect who wants serious CI/death cover but won't pay for pure term that "expires worthless." The product's signature move is the booster mechanism — high temporary cover layered on top of a smaller lifetime base. Get the booster diagram into your hand and 80% of the pitch follows naturally.

This is a merged module — Part 1 covers the foundations (what GPP is, who it's for, the four key numbers). Part 2 covers the signature pitch concept (the rocket-ship + travel-class diagram). Estimated read + drill time: ~55 minutes. Take it in two sittings if you need to.

# Part 1 — GPP in 5 Minutes: What It Is, Who It's For, Why It Exists

> **The one idea for Part 1:** Three things in one wrapper — death + TPD coverage, optional accelerated CI rider, and guaranteed cash value to age 100. You pay for a limited term (15/20/25 years), the cover is for life. The cash refund at the end is the entire reason this product exists.

By the end of Part 1 you should be able to: (1) describe GPP in two sentences without notes, (2) state the three pay terms and the booster cut-off ages, (3) recognise the three trigger points where GPP is the right call, (4) recognise when GPP is the *wrong* product (pure budget shoppers, term-only mindset).

---

## What GPP actually is

A SGD-denominated **participating whole life plan** issued by AIA. Built around two components:

- **Accumulator** — base policy. Lifelong cover to age 100. Has guaranteed and non-guaranteed cash value. Participates in the AIA participating fund (bonuses are not guaranteed).
- **Booster** — additional sum-assured layered on top. No cash value. Drops off at 65 or 75 (you pick at signup).

Result: heavy cover during the high-responsibility years (mortgage, kids, peak earning), lifetime cover after the booster falls off, and at the end of the day — *cash back*.

The line that opens every GPP conversation:

> *"It covers for death, coverage and disability and also for critical illness. This is our most popular policies, especially when you meet someone that is interested to get covered for critical illness."*

Pair it immediately with the cash-value reframe:

> *"Some people, they also want a bit of cash value at the end of it. They don't just want to pay and then they don't get back anything."*

That second sentence is the entire reason GPP beats pure term in this prospect's head.

## The three things that make GPP different

### 1. Limited pay, lifelong cover

Pay for **15, 20, or 25 years** — choose at signup. Cover runs to age 100 regardless. Premiums lock in for the entire pay term and never re-price.

> *"It is a limited payment term, meaning from now until 50 years old, you pay, but you get coverage for life."*

### 2. Booster that drops off at 65 or 75

The booster is high temporary cover that disappears when the prospect's financial obligations naturally shrink. Choose 65 (default — kids grown, mortgage paid) or 75 (if late-stage parenthood / second marriage).

### 3. Cash refund at maturity

If no claim is made, the prospect gets all premiums back **plus accumulated bonuses** by age 65 or earlier. This is the line that flips term-sceptics:

> *"The good thing about our plan is that you get money back. At the end of the day, at 65 years old, you'll still get all the money back, maybe plus even some interest."*

## The "buying a house" framing

The single most useful reframe for a first-time prospect:

> *"Whole life: it's like buying a house. You pay the mortgage for 20 years. It's a bit more expensive in term, but at least you own the house forever.*
>
> *Term life: you only pay until 65 but after 65 there's no more coverage. So it's like renting a house — you're just paying that rent every month, even though it's cheaper."*

This installs the *limited-pay-but-lifelong* mental model in 30 seconds.

## Three trigger points to reach for GPP

Use these as your internal qualification — if at least one is true, GPP is on the table.

| Trigger | Why it points to GPP |
|---|---|
| **No existing CI/whole-life cover** | Build the protection floor; lifetime base + temporary booster covers both today and post-65 |
| **Calculator shows a CI/death shortfall** | Layer GPP only for the gap (e.g. 5x annual income for CI), not as a full restart |
| **Prospect uncomfortable about "not getting money back"** | Cash-value refund is the conversion lever — pure term loses against this prospect |

## Who GPP is for (target market)

- Anyone aged 18–60 with 15+ years of working life ahead
- Someone who values **certainty + cash back** over the lowest possible premium
- Someone with dependants (spouse, child, ageing parents) — the booster years are the high-responsibility years
- Cases land most often in the **$300k–$500k death cover band** with 3x multiplier as the default

## Who GPP is NOT for (anti-market)

This is the disclosure you say to a prospect to *build* trust:

> *"This plan isn't right for someone whose only goal is the cheapest possible premium. If you genuinely don't care about cash back at maturity and just want maximum cover per dollar until 65, term is the right tool. We can compare them side by side."*

Saying this out loud is the move that separates trustworthy FCs from product-pushers. Use it.

## The four numbers you must know cold

| # | Number | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **15 / 20 / 25** | The three pay-term choices (years) |
| 2 | **65 or 75** | Booster cut-off ages — pick at signup |
| 3 | **2x / 3x / 5x** | Multiplier choices (default = 3x) |
| 4 | **$3M aggregate** | Total CI cover cap across all AIA CI policies |

If a prospect probes any of these and you fumble, you've lost the room.

## The 5-minute intro script

Practise this out loud once before your next prospect:

> *"This is AIA Guaranteed Protect Plus — our flagship whole-life-with-booster plan. The reason we use this plan instead of pure term: three things.*
>
> *One — limited pay, lifetime cover. You pay for 15, 20, or 25 years, but you're covered until age 100.*
>
> *Two — a booster that gives you heavy cover during your high-responsibility years (mortgage, kids, peak earning) and drops off at 65 when those obligations shrink.*
>
> *Three — at the end of the day, even if you make no claim, you get all your premiums back plus bonuses. Term gives you nothing back. This gives you something back.*
>
> *Think of it like buying a house versus renting. Term is renting — cheaper monthly but you own nothing at the end. GPP is buying — bit more expensive monthly, but the house is yours for life."*

That's 60 seconds. Time it. If yours runs to 90, cut adjectives.

# Part 2 — The Signature Pitch Concept: Booster + Accumulator (Rocket-Ship + Travel-Class Diagram)

> **The one idea for Part 2:** Every GPP pitch hinges on one drawing — a base "spaceship" plus detachable "booster rockets." The base (Accumulator) is permanent. The booster is temporary. Pick the multiplier (2x = first class, 3x = business, 5x = economy) and you've simultaneously chosen the price, the cash value, and the size of the cliff at 65. Master this single diagram and the rest of the pitch flows from it.

By the end of Part 2 you should be able to: draw the rocket-ship diagram from memory, explain the 2x/3x/5x multipliers using the travel-class analogy, and produce the multiplier recommendation (default 3x) for a sample prospect in under 90 seconds.

---

## Why this concept works

A multiplier table is unmemorable. A rocket ship is unforgettable. The contrast is the conversion.

> *"Think of this booster, it's like a rocket ship. When you launch a spaceship, the booster rockets are at the side to make sure the spaceship launches, and then after a certain time, the booster rockets will drop off."*

The metaphor lands because the prospect's life arc IS a launch — heavy thrust during the working years, a glide phase post-65 when obligations have shrunk.

---

## The two-component anatomy

```
Total cover during working years    Total cover after age 65
─────────────────────────────       ────────────────────────
                                    
   ┌──────────────────┐             ┌──────────┐
   │     BOOSTER      │             │          │
   │   (temporary)    │             │          │
   │   no cash value  │             │   BASE   │
   └──────────────────┘             │  +bonus  │
   ┌──────────────────┐             │          │
   │       BASE       │             │          │
   │   (Accumulator)  │             │          │
   │   has cash value │             │          │
   │   to age 100     │             │          │
   └──────────────────┘             └──────────┘
```

The base is the lifetime spine. The booster is the high-thrust temporary layer. After 65, only the base + non-guaranteed bonuses continue.

The verbatim:

> *"At 65, you typically have fewer financial obligations. Kids are grown, house is paid off, retirement savings are built. You need less coverage then, which keeps costs affordable now."*

That sentence is the rationale for the cliff — and the pre-empt for the "what happens at 65" objection (Day 3).

---

## The travel-class analogy (multiplier choice)

The 2x / 3x / 5x decision is the price-vs-base tradeoff. Sell it as a class of seat:

| Multiplier | Class | What you get | When to recommend |
|---|---|---|---|
| **5x** | Economy | Cheapest premium · weakest base · biggest cliff at 65 | Budget-tight prospect, young, willing to revisit later |
| **3x** | Business *(default)* | Balanced premium · moderate base · moderate cliff | Almost every prospect — start here |
| **2x** | First class | Highest premium · strongest base · smallest cliff | Prospect prioritising legacy / post-65 cover, higher income |

The verbatim:

> *"5x is like economy class — very cheap, gets you there. 3x is business class. 2x is first class."*
>
> *"Even though it's cheapest, the 5x is not as good in terms of the coverage and the cash value."*
>
> *"Usually I just go for 3x, it's somewhere in between."*

The default-3x discipline: **if you don't have a strong reason to deviate, you're recommending 3x.** Save the multiplier conversation for prospects who specifically push back on premium or specifically prioritise legacy.

---

## The reference numbers (memorise these)

### $300k cover example, age 25 non-smoker, 25-yr pay, drop-off 65

| Multiplier | Base SA | Booster SA | Premium / yr | Yield |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 5x | $60k | $240k | ~$2,200 | ~1.25% |
| **3x (default)** | **$100k** | **$200k** | **~$2,600** | **~2%** |
| 2x | $150k | $150k | ~$3,800 | highest |

### Coverage drop at 65

- 5x → loses ~80% of cover
- 3x → loses ~67% of cover
- 2x → loses ~50% of cover

### Smaller-case example (most common)

$200k cover, age 25, 3x, drop-off 65 → ~$1,946/year. Bonuses keep cover ~$120k post-65.

### Minimum policy size

2x → $25k SA, ~$50–60/month minimum premium. (You almost never run this small.)

## The "rocket-ship" delivery script

Practise this out loud — it should take 60–90 seconds:

> *"Let me show you how this plan is structured. *(Draw two stacked rectangles.)* The bottom one — that's the base. We call it the Accumulator. It runs for life. It builds cash value. It pays out at 100 if no claim is made.*
>
> *The top one — that's the booster. *(Tap it.)* This is temporary cover. It gives you heavier protection during your working years — when the mortgage is highest, kids are still in school, your obligations are biggest. At 65, the booster falls off. Like a rocket ship — the side boosters drop after launch. By then, your kids are grown, house is paid, retirement's in place. You don't need the same level of cover.*
>
> *Three multipliers to pick from. Five-times — like economy class, cheapest. Three-times — business class, balanced. Two-times — first class, strongest base. Most of my clients sit at three-times. That OK with you?"*

End with the question. Micro-commit on the multiplier before you move to Phase 4 quotation (Day 2 Part 2).

---

## When NOT to pull out this diagram

- **Prospect is term-only mindset** — diagram makes them feel the plan is complex; lose them
- **Prospect already understands whole-life mechanics** — skip to numbers, draw only if they ask
- **Time-boxed appointment <20 min** — diagram is 3 minutes; truncate to "limited pay, lifetime cover, cash back" verbal version

The diagram is for *uncertain* prospects in *normal-length* appointments. Read the room.
