# Day 4 — Defence + Expansion: Top 5 Objections + Cross-sell

> **Today's frame:** This is the day GPP is won or lost. The 5 objections all loop back to the same question: *"could I get the same outcome cheaper somewhere else?"* The answer is consistent — GPP is not the cheapest; it is the most durable. Limited pay + lifelong cover + cash back + booster during peak years. No other product does all four under one premium.

This is the longest module in the track. Part 1 covers the 5 objections (the conviction-story heart). Part 2 covers cross-sell + bundling. Estimated read + drill: ~65 minutes. Take in two sittings.

---

# Part 1 — Top 5 Objections (the Conviction-Story Heart)

> **The one idea for Part 1:** Don't argue against pure term — reframe it. Term wins on premium per dollar of cover *until 65*, then disappears. GPP locks the premium for the entire pay term, refunds your premium plus interest, and keeps a base for life. The buying-vs-renting analogy is the closest thing to a single-shot conviction frame this product has.

By the end of Part 1 you should be able to: recite the buying-vs-renting frame from memory, defend the booster mechanic against pure-term BTID, deploy the sure-win framing for the "what if I never claim" objection, and use the diversification reframe for sophisticated investors.

---

## The conviction-story principle

You will not change a sharp prospect's mind by *attacking* term. You will change it by *reframing* what they're actually choosing.

The frame: term and GPP solve different problems.

- **Term** = high cover for a fixed window, then it ends. Renting.
- **GPP** = lower cover per premium dollar, but locked in for life and refunded at maturity. Buying.

Both are valid tools. The question is which problem the prospect is solving.

---

## Objection 1 — "GPP is too expensive vs Term"

This is the most common objection. The buying-vs-renting frame handles 80% of it.

### The script

> *"You're right that the monthly premium is higher. Let me show you why over a lifetime, GPP often costs LESS.*
>
> *Term insurance — cheaper initially, but the premiums increase significantly with age, especially upon renewal at 65 or older. Over a lifetime, the total cost of a term policy can be much higher than GPP — and at the end you have nothing.*
>
> *GPP — same premium for 25 years, then nothing more to pay. Cover for life. Cash back at maturity.*
>
> *Term is like renting a house — cheaper monthly but you don't own it at the end. GPP is like buying a house — bit more expensive monthly but you own it forever and can sell it back."*

### The verbatim (videos 4 + 7 — substantially identical)

> *"GPP as buying a house: pay premiums for a limited period (e.g. 20–25 years). After this period, the policy is yours, providing lifelong coverage and cash value accumulation."*

> *"Term insurance as renting a house: you pay premiums throughout the term, but once it ends you lose coverage and there's no residual value or cash benefit."*

> *"Term insurance: cheaper initially, but the premiums increase significantly with age, especially upon renewal at 65 or older. Over a lifetime, the total cost of a term policy can be much higher."*

> *"GPP offers guaranteed lifelong coverage and potential cash value. It's a guaranteed win — whether you pass away early, live long, or face critical illnesses, there's a payout."*

### The lapse-discipline angle (extra punch from video 4)

> *"If you stop paying for your term policy, it lapses immediately. But for GPP, even if you stop paying, if there is cash value, the plan still continues and does not lapse for a period of time."*

This is the answer to the *"what if I lose my job"* hesitation — even if Premium Pause isn't activated, the plan doesn't immediately collapse like term does.

---

## Objection 2 — "Whole life is overkill — I just want pure protection"

The reframe: the booster IS pure protection. It's term-like cover stacked on top of a permanent base.

### The script

> *"Look at the booster part — that's exactly pure term cover. It's high-cover, temporary, drops at 65 like a term policy would.*
>
> *The difference is GPP layers it on top of a permanent base. The base is what makes the cash refund possible. Without the base, you'd have term — and at 65 it'd be gone with nothing back.*
>
> *So you're not paying extra for 'overkill.' You're paying extra for the base that gives you the cash refund and the lifetime spine. The booster on top is the same temporary high cover you'd get from term."*

### When to validate and pivot

If the prospect genuinely doesn't want lifetime cover (specific reason — e.g. *"I don't have dependants who'll need legacy money"*) — pivot. Pitch term instead. Respect the close; it builds trust for future referrals.

---

## Objection 3 — "I already have CI cover"

Don't argue. Run the gap calc.

### The script

> *"OK — what's the sum-assured on your existing CI cover?*
>
> *And your annual income is $___?*
>
> *Industry benchmark for CI cover is 5 years of income — recovery period plus family income replacement. So at $___/year income, the benchmark is $___ in CI cover.*
>
> *You currently have $___. The shortfall is $___. We can layer GPP only for the gap — not as a full restart. Sound fair?"*

### The verbatim approach (from Phase 1)

> *"Based on CST, you need at least 5 years of income for CI coverage. Currently you have a shortfall."*

The trust move: never propose replacing existing CI. Layer for the gap. Replacement-pitching is what destroys FC trust at scale.

---

## Objection 4 — "Why pay for cover I might never use?"

The sure-win framing — the source's most quoted line.

### The script

> *"That's the beauty of this plan. Three outcomes:*
>
> *One — you get cancer or another major CI. The plan pays out 100%. You're financially protected during recovery.*
>
> *Two — you pass away. The plan pays out the death benefit to your family.*
>
> *Three — you live a long, healthy life and never claim. The plan refunds all your premiums plus bonuses by age 65. You actually make money on it.*
>
> *No matter which path your life takes — you get something back. It's a sure-win."*

### The verbatim

> *"Kind of like a sure-win kind of thing. Because if you don't claim, you get the money back. If you claim, you also get the money back. So no matter what, you also get the money back."*

> *"It's just a limited payment and a lifelong coverage. You only have to pay for about 15 to 20 years for a very limited amount of time, but you can actually get lifelong coverage."*

---

## Objection 5 — "I'd rather BTID — Buy Term and Invest the Difference"

This is the most sophisticated objection — usually from prospects who've read Ramit Sethi or watched Dave Ramsey. Don't dismiss it. Reframe with diversification.

### The script

> *"BTID is mathematically defensible — IF you actually invest the difference, AND your investments outperform GPP's ~2% guaranteed-floor yield, AND you maintain the discipline for 25+ years.*
>
> *Most BTID adherents fail on at least one of those. But let's say you're disciplined.*
>
> *Even then — you've got 100% of your protection budget tied to market performance. If we layer GPP into your portfolio, you get a guaranteed-return floor underneath the investment risk. APA or your DIY equity stack handles upside; GPP locks down a baseline.*
>
> *It's diversification — not either/or."*

### The verbatim diversification frame

> *"BTID acknowledgement: GPP allows for a diversified financial strategy, combining guaranteed returns with potential investment gains, ensuring not all of the client's financial plan relies on market performance."*

This reframe converts the most defensible BTID prospects — the ones who *will* invest the difference. The undisciplined ones convert on the cash-value question alone.

## The single line that handles 80% of pushback

When a prospect drifts back to "but term is just cheaper":

> *"You're right — term is cheaper today. The question is what you want at 65. With term you have nothing. With GPP you have cover for life and your money back. Pick the one that solves your real problem."*

Then silence. Let it sit.

## Drill (Part 1) — 60 minutes, the longest drill of the track

1. **Recite the buying-vs-renting frame** out loud, 3 times. Time it — should be under 60 seconds.
2. **Memorise the term-renewal escalation point** (~5%/yr of age delay, premiums explode at 65 renewal).
3. **Recite the sure-win three outcomes** without looking. Each outcome in one sentence.
4. **Memorise the diversification reframe** for BTID — say it in your own words.
5. **Run a roleplay.** Pair with a peer (or simulate). Have them throw all 5 objections at you in random order. Score whether you used the script + the analogy + the closing question.
6. **Identify the objection you're weakest on.** Write the answer out by hand. Re-read tomorrow.

# Part 2 — Cross-sell + Bundling: What Pairs With GPP

> **The one idea for Part 2:** GPP is rarely the whole answer. The Phase 6 packaging move is GPP for death + Major CI + savings core, plus UCC bolted on for Early CI / recurring claims. On top of that, the Mum-to-Baby rider for expectant clients and Payer Benefit for child policies are the highest-leverage adjacent sells. Don't rely on GPP for accident — TPD definitions are too restrictive.

By the end of Part 2 you should be able to: name the 4 buckets and which products solve each, recommend the GPP + UCC packaging move, deploy the Mum-to-Baby pitch for any expectant client, and recognise when to recommend a separate accident plan.

---

## The cross-sell hierarchy

```
1. Major CI + death + savings core
   GPP @ 3x / 25-yr / drop-off 65
        ↓
2. Layer Early CI separately
   via UCC, NOT inside GPP
        ↓
3. Add accident cover
   SEPARATE plan (Solitaire PA / Centurion PA)
        ↓
4. Hospitalisation
   HSGM (separate appointment)
        ↓
5. Family riders
   Mum-to-Baby · Payer Benefit · OPAI
        ↓
6. Spouse cross-sell
   Lock spouse premium at current age
```

Each step is a different conversation. Don't try to close all six in one appointment.

---

## Step 1 — Why GPP for Major CI + UCC for Early CI (the source's explicit pattern)

The source is unambiguous on this:

> *"I do not sell the Early CI part — I just purely sell the Major CI part. And then I package the rest on a UCC for the Early CI part. The life plan gives the coverage for the Major CI part, the death part, and the savings part. For subsequent claims, recurring claims, and Early CI claims, that's where the UCC would actually come in."*

### Why this packaging works

| Coverage type | GPP | UCC |
|---|---|---|
| Death cover | ✅ | — |
| Major CI (one claim) | ✅ | — |
| Savings / cash value | ✅ | — |
| Early CI | Optional rider — $$$ | ✅ Native |
| Recurring CI claims | ❌ Single CI claim | ✅ Multi-claim |
| Special conditions | Rider only | ✅ Native |

The GPP Early CI rider is expensive and accelerated (eats into death). UCC is multi-claim and not accelerated. **Packaging is cheaper AND covers more.**

### When to deploy

In the Phase 5 layering moment (Day 2 Part 2), present Major CI as the GPP base and tease UCC as the *next conversation* — don't bundle in this appointment.

> *"Major CI is locked in via GPP. For the Early CI plus recurring-claim coverage, we'd layer a separate plan called UCC — Ultimate Critical Cover. Different appointment, but it pairs naturally. Want me to book us a 30-minute follow-up?"*

---

## Step 2 — The four-bucket Total Wealth gap (don't rely on GPP for accident)

The source is explicit about GPP's TPD limitation:

> *"Please don't rely on GPP for your accident policy because it's very hard to achieve these definitions. You have to lose both eyes, two limbs, one eye and one limb."*

GPP TPD covers full disability only. For partial disability (lost finger, partial hearing, broken back recovery) you need a dedicated **Personal Accident** plan (Solitaire PA or Centurion PA).

### The 4-bucket recommendation map

| Bucket | Primary product | Secondary / layered |
|---|---|---|
| **CI + Death + Savings** | GPP (today) | UCC (Early CI / recurring) |
| **Hospitalisation** | HSGM | Separate appointment |
| **Accident** | Solitaire PA / Centurion PA | Separate plan |
| **Wealth accumulation** | APA | Long-term, separate appointment |

Always name all 4 buckets in Phase 1. GPP only solves the first. Tell them so. Trust compounds.

---

## Step 3 — The family-riders play

### Mum-to-Baby (the pregnancy / planning-kids pitch)

For prospects who are pregnant or actively planning kids — this is the highest-leverage rider in AIA's stack.

The verbatim:

> *"If you have any clients who are expecting or planning for kids, this is really important — make sure they buy a plan for the kid and add on this Mum-to-Baby Protect rider."*

What it does:
- Covers pregnancy complications for the mother
- Guarantees future cover for the child
- **No medical underwriting on the child** — locks in insurability before any future health issue surfaces

The script:

> *"Are you pregnant or planning soon? There's a rider called Mum-to-Baby — covers complications during pregnancy AND guarantees your child's insurability with no medical underwriting later. Even if your child develops a condition at age 5 that would normally lock them out of cover — if we set this up before birth, they're covered for life. Worth setting up before any health questions come up later."*

### Payer Benefit (CPWP) — for child policies

For when the parent is paying for a child's GPP/APA/etc.

The verbatim:

> *"If you buy this for your first kid, then if death or TPD or CI happens to the parent, the plan will be free for the kid for the rest of the term."*

The pitch: parent's premium-paying capacity is itself a risk. Payer Benefit waives the child's premium if the paying parent dies, becomes TPD, or gets CI.

### OPAI (Option to Purchase Additional Insurance)

Lets the policyholder buy further cover with **no underwriting** at certain life events:
- Marriage
- Birth of child
- Death of spouse
- Reaching age 18 (for child-bought policies)

Conditions:
- Must exercise within 90 days of the event
- Expires at age 55
- One-time use per event

This is a long-tail benefit — plant the seed at delivery, not at the close.

---

## Step 4 — Spouse cross-sell (the highest-conversion adjacent sell)

If your prospect is married, the spouse is your next appointment — guaranteed. Use the close to seed it:

> *"By the way — does your wife have CI cover? Because every year she delays, the premium goes up about 5%. If we set hers up now too, you both lock in current rates for life. Want me to book a 30-minute follow-up with her?"*

The dual-policy lock-in is the most natural cross-sell in the entire AIA stack — and almost always closes within 60 days.

---

## Step 5 — Layering with PLP (whole-life floor) for legacy clients

For higher-net-worth prospects who want a guaranteed-base whole-life floor in addition to GPP's booster:

- **GPP** — handles spike-cover years (booster + cash value)
- **PLP (Pro Lifetime Protector)** — handles legacy / estate floor with a higher base SA

This is a 2-product play for clients with $200k+ household income or significant estate-planning concerns. Don't pitch PLP in the same appointment — book separately.

---

## The cross-sell discipline rules

1. **Always name all 4 protection buckets in Phase 1.** Never let the prospect think GPP is the whole answer.
2. **Never bundle UCC into the GPP close.** Two appointments.
3. **Always check Mum-to-Baby for expectant or planning-kids prospects.** Highest-leverage rider in the stack.
4. **Always tee up the spouse close.** Single most reliable adjacent sell.
5. **Don't rely on GPP for accident or hospitalisation.** Refer to PA plan and HSGM.
