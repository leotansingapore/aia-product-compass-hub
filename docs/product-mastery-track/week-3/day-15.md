# Day 5 — Compliance + Close: Disclosures, Four-Hoops Close, Paperwork, Delivery

> **Today's frame:** Disclose proactively, close in layers. The non-guaranteed bonus, accelerated CI, and Premium-Pause-as-loan are all things RNF will sample-audit you on — and all things that build trust if delivered as credibility moves rather than buried in fine print. The close is the formal version of decisions already made through the four hoops.

This is the final module. Part 1 covers compliance + risk disclosures. Part 2 covers the four-hoops layered close, paperwork, and delivery. Estimated read + drill: ~50 minutes.

---

# Part 1 — Compliance + Risk Disclosures (RNF-Relevant Phrasing)

> **The one idea for Part 1:** Three things must land in your verbal walkthrough — the non-guaranteed bonus, the accelerated CI mechanic, and Premium Pause as a loan. Saying them out loud is the credibility move. Burying them in BI fine print is how FCs lose audits.

By the end of Part 1 you should be able to: deliver the four required disclosures cleanly, recognise when GPP is the wrong product for the prospect (and say so), and pass an RNF sample audit without a coaching note.

---

## The principle

A prospect who hears the risks *up front* and still signs is a prospect who signed for the right reasons. They won't free-look out at week 2. They won't complain to MAS. They'll refer you.

A prospect who only finds out about risks at week 3 — by reading the policy contract — feels deceived. That's the cancellation. That's the complaint. That's the RNF audit.

> *"A bonus rate is used in the policy illustration — the actual benefits payable may vary according to the future experience of the participating fund. Investment rate of return is not guaranteed. Past performance is not indicative of future performance."*

Say this *out loud* in the appointment, in plain language. Don't bury it in a 60-page contract.

---

## The four required disclosures

### Disclosure 1 — "Bonuses are not guaranteed"

GPP is a participating plan. Anything past the guaranteed cash value column in the BI is **non-guaranteed** and depends on the participating fund's performance.

> *"Just to be very clear — the cash value I'm showing you has two parts. The guaranteed column is locked. The illustrated total is guaranteed PLUS non-guaranteed bonuses based on how AIA's participating fund performs. The actual amount could be higher or lower than what I'm showing.*
>
> *Historically AIA's par fund has performed well, but the future is not guaranteed. We've shown both columns so you can see the floor and the projection."*

The verbatim:

> *"A bonus rate is used in the policy illustration — the actual benefits payable may vary according to the future experience of the participating fund."*

### Disclosure 2 — "Cash values may be lower than premiums paid (early years)"

> *"In the early years — typically the first 5 to 10 years — the cash value of the policy will be lower than the total premiums you've paid. That's because the costs of distribution and the risk component are front-loaded.*
>
> *By the end of the pay term, the cash value catches up. By age 65, you should be back to premium parity or above. By age 100, you're well above. But early surrender means you take a loss.*
>
> *Honest version: if you can't commit to at least 10 years, this is the wrong plan. We should look at something else."*

The verbatim:

> *"The policy involves high costs — case rate of value payable may be less than total payment paid."*

That last sentence is the RNF-protection move. **Say it out loud.**

### Disclosure 3 — "Accelerated CI reduces death benefit"

This is the most important mechanic to disclose proactively (covered in detail Day 3 Part 1):

> *"One thing I want to flag clearly — if you make a CI claim, the death benefit gets reduced by the claim amount. So if your death cover is $500k and you claim $100k for early-stage cancer, your remaining death benefit is $400k. That's how accelerated CI works.*
>
> *It's not stacked on top — it's drawn down from. This keeps the premium affordable. If you want CI fully stacked on top of death, that's a different product structure (more expensive)."*

The verbatim:

> *"If you claim for early CI, do you still get the death coverage? Yes, but the death coverage will actually be reduced."*

### Disclosure 4 — "Premium Pause is a loan, not a waiver"

> *"There's a feature called Premium Pause — useful if you lose your job. But I want to be clear: it's a 12-month interest-free LOAN, not a waiver. You still owe the premiums; you just have 12 months to pay them back interest-free. After that, interest accrues.*
>
> *Most clients never use it. It's there as a safety net, not a benefit."*

The verbatim:

> *"There will be a 12-month period where you don't have to pay, but take note this is not free money — you still have to pay back. There is no interest charged on this loan, but you just have to pay it back. After 12 months, there will be interest on the outstanding loan."*

---

## The "anti-market" disclosure (the trust move)

The single biggest credibility move in a GPP appointment:

> *"This plan is not for everyone. The anti-market for GPP is people who:*
>
> *- Want the absolute lowest possible premium per dollar of cover*
> *- Are confident they'll invest the difference and outperform 2% (and have the discipline)*
> *- Need their money back in under 10 years*
> *- Don't value the cash refund at maturity*
>
> *If any of those describe you, GPP is the wrong plan. We should be looking at term + a separate investment plan instead. Are you OK committing for 25 years and accepting that the cash value is lower than premiums paid in the early years?"*

If they hesitate on the 25-year commitment, **stop the close.** Recommend pure term instead. RNF would rather you walk away from a wrong-fit case than book a complaint.

---

## The exclusions block

State these proactively. Don't make the prospect ask.

### Standard exclusions

- **Suicide** — not covered in the first policy year. Covered after year 1.
- **Pre-existing conditions** — anything pre-existing or any surgery before policy start is hard-excluded.
- **War and warlike operations**
- **Deliberate self-endangerment** (illegal activity, intoxication, professional sports per policy)

The verbatim:

> *"Anything that's pre-existing, any surgery or pre-existing condition that happened before that, all of those things will not be covered."*

### Waiting periods

| Condition type | Waiting period |
|---|---|
| Most listed CI conditions (heart attack, stroke, cancer, etc.) | 90 days |
| ADHD-related conditions | 1 year |
| Survival required after diagnosis | 7 days |

### Premium Pause restrictions

- Minimum 3 years of premiums paid
- 6-month prior employment required
- 2-month deferment when unemployed
- 12-month pause + 12-month interest-free repayment then interest accrues
- **Full-time MPs cannot exercise Premium Pause** (specific exclusion)

---

## The 14-day free-look (state at delivery)

> *"After AIA issues the policy, you have 14 days from receipt to free-look it. If you change your mind in that window, AIA refunds the premium paid. Some adjustments may apply if the participating fund value has moved, but you don't get to keep any profit — refund is capped at premiums paid."*

State this proactively at Phase 7 delivery. Don't wait for them to ask.

---

## The aggregate caps (mention if they push on coverage)

| Cap | Value |
|---|---|
| Total CI cover across all AIA CI policies | $3M aggregate |
| Early CI per-episode cap | $350k |
| Single Major CI claim per condition | 100% per claim, 1 claim per condition |
| Special-condition payout | 20% of SA, capped $25k each, max 5 claims, ends at age 21 |

If a prospect wants more than $3M CI cover via AIA, you can't write it. Refer to a multi-insurer top-up strategy.

---

## When to walk away from a case

Three scenarios where the right move is to NOT close:

1. **Time horizon under 10 years.** GPP is wrong for them. Recommend pure term or a savings plan.
2. **Cannot afford the minimum without sacrificing the emergency fund or higher-priority protection.** Build the foundation first.
3. **Genuinely term-only mindset (no value placed on cash refund).** Pitch term and respect the close. Trust compounds.

Saying *"I don't think GPP is right for you, let me show you something else"* is the most under-appreciated trust-building move in the whole product set. Use it.

---

## The RNF-clean closing block

Deliver this as one block right before the iPOS+ binary close:

> *"Before we authenticate, four things to make sure I've covered:*
>
> *One — bonuses in the illustration are not guaranteed. The actual cash value depends on AIA's participating fund performance.*
>
> *Two — in the early years, your cash value will be lower than what you've paid. By age 65, it catches up. If you surrender early, you take a loss.*
>
> *Three — if you make a CI claim, the death benefit reduces by that amount. It's accelerated, not stacked.*
>
> *Four — Premium Pause is a 12-month interest-free loan, not a free waiver.*
>
> *Any of those four concern you?"*

If "no" — RNF audit pass. Move to Phase 7 paperwork.

If "yes" — slow down. Address it. Better to spend 15 more minutes than to book a complaint.

---

## Drill (Part 1)

1. **Memorise the four disclosures** word-for-word. Recite each in under 30 seconds.
2. **Practise the anti-market disclosure** out loud, 3 times. Notice if it feels uncomfortable — it should. The discomfort is the credibility.
3. **Memorise the closing disclosure block** (4 things, ~75 seconds total). This is what an RNF audit listens for.
4. **Pick 1 prospect from your pipeline** for whom GPP is the wrong product. Write the script for telling them so. Practise it.

# Part 2 — The Four-Hoops Layered Close, Paperwork, Delivery

> **The one idea for Part 2:** By Phase 6 the prospect has cleared 4 hoops — they've agreed they need CI cover, that they need to buy now, that 3x is the right multiplier, and that Early CI is worth adding. The "close" is just the binary that confirms hoop 4. Then paperwork (FHR sign-off, BI walkthrough, Vitality enrolment) and the next-conversation setup (spouse, child, hospitalisation).

By the end of Part 2 you should be able to: deliver the four-hoops binary close in under 4 minutes, run the iPOS+ paperwork without dragging, set up the next conversation cleanly, and recognise when to slow the close vs when to push through.

---

## The four-hoops progressive close

The source's most important closing principle: **never collapse the four decisions into one.** Each hoop is its own micro-yes:

| Hoop | What the prospect agrees to | When |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | "I need CI cover" | Phase 1 (cash-value question + CI gap) |
| 2 | "I need to buy now (not later)" | Phase 3 (now-vs-+10-years contrast) |
| 3 | "3x multiplier vs 2x" (or your recommended multiplier) | Phase 2 + 4 (illustrator + iPOS+) |
| 4 | "I need Early CI added" | Phase 5 (Apple Store layering + binary) |

Phase 6 close is then trivial — the prospect has already said yes to four things in a row. The signature is the formal acknowledgement of decisions already made.

---

## The Phase 5 → Phase 6 binary

The actual close-the-deal moment is the single binary at end of Phase 5:

> *"Mr Prospect, would you prefer without Early CI or with Early CI?"*

That's the close. Most prospects pick *with*. The reasoning is built in by Phase 5's transparency walkthrough (73 vs 150 conditions, ~$60/month differential).

The reinforcement immediately after:

> *"You're doing a good thing. Getting early coverage is cheaper now for you."*

This is the post-decision validation — important because the prospect just made a real money commitment, and the brain immediately starts doubting. Reassure.

---

## The half-half coverage split

The affordability lever for Phase 5:

> *"It's only $60 more per month to get this additional $150K Early CI coverage."*

The default split:

```
$300k CI total =
   $150k Early CI rider
+  $150k Major CI (within GPP base)
```

This keeps the Early CI rider price down while still giving meaningful coverage at every CI stage. Half-half is the standard recommendation; only deviate if the prospect specifically asks.

The standard close numbers (memorise):

| Variable | Default value |
|---|---|
| Cover | $300k |
| Multiplier | 3x |
| Pay term | 25 yrs |
| Drop-off | 65 |
| Major CI only premium | ~$3,200/yr |
| With half-half Early CI | ~$3,995/yr |
| Differential | ~$795/yr (~$60/mo) |
| Cash value at 100 | ~$285k |

---

## Phase 6 — Reinforce the four buckets at signing

The verbatim value-stack reassurance:

> *"Death disability settled, CI settled, early CI settled, savings settled — basically all four areas are now adequately covered."*

> *"Constantly reinforcing, reassuring the customer that you're doing a good thing."*

This is the post-close emotional anchor. The prospect just spent thousands of dollars annually for the next 25 years. They need to *feel* safe about that decision, not just rationally accept it.

---

## Phase 7 — Implementation (3 minutes)

The execution sprint. No talking — only doing.

### Step 1 — Visual confirm on screen

Open the iPOS+ application screen. Verify on-screen details with the prospect:

- Name, NRIC, contact, email
- Bank account for GIRO
- Beneficiary nomination

### Step 2 — Bank + cadence

Most clients monthly. Confirm.

### Step 3 — FHR sign-off

Walk through the Financial Health Review. Get signature.

### Step 4 — BI walkthrough + free-look reminder

> *"You'll get the full Benefit Illustration in your email. The 14-day free-look starts when you receive the policy contract. If anything in the BI doesn't match what we discussed, call me first — we can usually resolve it without using the free-look."*

### Step 5 — Vitality enrolment (if applicable)

If the prospect has Vitality Platinum status, the Power-Up Dollar bonus boost stays at the higher rate. If not, encourage enrolment as a separate step:

> *"Optional but worth it — Vitality Platinum keeps your first-year bonus boost from declining over time. Enrolment takes 10 minutes through the AIA app. Want me to walk you through it?"*

### Step 6 — Calendar the next conversation

This is the most-skipped step. **Don't skip.** Use specific anchors:

| Trigger | What to discuss |
|---|---|
| 3-month policy review | UCC cross-sell (Early CI / multi-claim) |
| Next family milestone (engagement, marriage, pregnancy) | Mum-to-Baby + spouse cross-sell |
| Job change | Premium Pause review, salary update |
| 30 / 35 / 40 birthday | Mid-term protection review, top-up |

Set the calendar entry **in front of the prospect.** They see you do it. Trust signal compounds.

## The post-close 90-day architecture

Same discipline as the APA track — the 90 days post-issue are the highest-trust window of the entire relationship.

### Day 7–14 — Free-look check-in

> *"Hey [name], the policy contract should be with you by now. Just checking — anything you want to ask before the 14-day free-look window closes?"*

### Day 30 — GIRO confirmation

> *"Hey [name], just confirming the first GIRO debit went through. All good on your end?"*

### Day 60–90 — Cross-sell + referral ask

> *"Hey [name] — quick check-in. Plan's humming, GIRO debiting, no issues. Two things on my end:*
>
> *One — should we book that 30-minute follow-up for UCC (the Early CI multi-claim layer we discussed)?*
>
> *Two — I run my business through introductions. Is there one or two people in your circle who you think would benefit from the same conversation we had?"*

---

## The "case is closed" definition

A GPP case is NOT closed at SMS auth. A case is closed when:

1. ✅ FHR signed
2. ✅ iPOS+ application submitted
3. ✅ First GIRO debit confirmed (Day 30)
4. ✅ 14-day free-look window passed without cancellation
5. ✅ Vitality Platinum enrolled (if applicable)
6. ✅ At least 1 next-conversation booked (UCC, spouse, Mum-to-Baby, etc.)

If you stop at iPOS+ submission and never run #3–6, you closed half a case.

---

## When to slow the close vs push through

### Slow down if:
- Prospect hesitated on any of the four hoops
- Prospect couldn't articulate the cash-value reframe back to you
- Prospect introduced a new concern in Phase 5 or 6
- Prospect's spouse / decision-maker isn't present and they want to "discuss"

### Push through if:
- All four hoops cleared cleanly
- Prospect verbalised at least 2 micro-commits in Phases 3–5
- The objection lifecycle was handled in Day 4 territory
- Prospect is asking implementation questions ("when does the first debit happen?")

If in doubt: book the second appointment. Better to close in two visits than to lose in one. Same discipline as APA.

---

## The benchmark (what good looks like)

A senior FC running this close discipline at full strength produces:

- **1–3 GPP cases per week** (closed)
- **50%+ first-appointment close rate** (with Early CI half-half attached most of the time)
- **$3.5k–$4.5k average annual premium** (the $300k / 3x / 25-yr / half-half Early CI default)
- **2+ adjacent sells per closed case** within 90 days (UCC, spouse, Mum-to-Baby, etc.)

That's the target the entire 5-day track is calibrated to.

## Drill (Part 2)

1. **Recite the four-hoops sequence** (CI cover · buy now · 3x · with Early CI). Time it. Target: 30 seconds.
2. **Practise the binary close** ("with or without Early CI?") in three tonalities — neutral, slightly playful, slightly serious. Pick the one that sounds most like you.
3. **Memorise the standard close numbers** ($3,200 vs $3,995, ~$60/mo differential, $285k cash at 100). Recite without looking.
4. **Write your Day 30 GIRO check-in template** — exact message you'd send. Save it as a snippet.
5. **Pick your last 3 closed cases.** For each, audit: did you do all 6 of the "case is closed" steps? If no, what stopped you?
