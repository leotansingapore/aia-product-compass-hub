# Part 1 — The Numbers: Premiums, Riders, Accelerated CI Mechanics, Illustration Discipline

> **The one idea for Part 1:** GPP's headline numbers (~2% IRR, $48k → $192k cash) genuinely beat a bank / FD with cover attached. But the catch most FCs miss: a CI claim *reduces* the death benefit by the claim amount. This is accelerated CI. Disclose it upfront — burying it costs cases later.

By the end of Part 1 you should be able to: state every key number from memory, walk through accelerated-CI mechanics in 30 seconds, defend the cash-value math against bank / FD comparisons, and present the Income Drawdown Facility (IDF) when a prospect asks about post-pay-term cashflow.

---

## The structural inversion (this is the heart)

Most FCs lose GPP cases because they treat the cash value as the headline. It's the *anchor*, not the headline. The headline is the protection — and the cash value is the bonus that makes the protection feel free.

Lead with this when a prospect probes the math:

> *"If you put it in your bank, you don't get coverage and you also don't get any interest. Over here, you get both the coverage and the returns. Isn't that like killing two birds with one stone?"*

Then the post-pay-term reassurance:

> *"Even though you stopped paying right after 25 years, your cash value is actually escalating over time."*

That second sentence handles the "what happens after I stop paying" objection — answered before they ask.

---

## The CI rider math

This is where most FCs fumble. Internalise the structure cold:

### Major CI rider

- Covers **73 conditions**
- Pays 100% of CI sum-assured per claim
- Sum-assured cap: bound to the policy

### Early CI rider

- Covers **~150 conditions** + **15 special conditions**
- Pays 100% per claim
- Max **5 claims** total
- Max **$350k per episode**
- 1 claim per condition

### Special conditions (within Early CI)

- Pays **20% of SA**, capped at **$25k each**
- Max **5 claims**
- End at age 21

### Aggregate cap

> *"Major CI total cap: $3M aggregate across all AIA CI policies."*

The verbatim:

> *"Major CI covers 73 conditions. If you go for the early CI one, it covers about 150 different conditions. And also it covers 15 special conditions, only 1 claim per condition up to 5 claims. Every time you claim for any of the critical units, you will get a 100% payout."*

> *"Early CI claim limit is about $350K. So if you bought $1M of Early CI coverage, you can't claim the full $1M, you can only claim $350K each time."*

## Accelerated CI — the disclosure that protects you

This is the single most important mechanic to disclose proactively:

> *"If your death coverage is $500,000 and you chose to add on $100,000 worth of CI coverage, once you claim for that, your $500K death coverage will be reduced by $100,000."*

The script:

> *"One thing I want to be very clear on — if you make a CI claim, the death benefit gets reduced by the claim amount. So if your death cover is $500k and you claim $100k for early-stage cancer, your remaining death benefit is $400k. That's how accelerated CI works. It's not stacked on top — it's drawn down from."*

Saying this *out loud during the pitch* (not at the close) is the trust move. Burying it in BI fine print is how FCs lose audits.

## The Income Drawdown Facility (IDF)

Optional structured withdrawal post-multiplier-expiry. Two utilisation modes:

### 50% utilisation

- Withdraw **5% of accumulator/yr for 10 yrs**
- 50% of SA remains as cover
- Plan continues

### 100% utilisation

- Withdraw **10% of accumulator/yr for 10 yrs**
- Policy terminates at end of 10-yr drawdown
- No remaining cover

### Worked example ($100k cover / 2x / IDF 100%)

```
$10k/yr × 10 yrs = $123k total income drawn
vs
$146k surrender value if not used
```

So IDF 100% gives up ~$23k of surrender value in exchange for a structured 10-year income stream. Use this when prospect asks about retirement cashflow specifically.

## Illustration discipline (3 rules)

1. **Untick Early CI first.** Show Major CI only at Phase 4. Layer Early CI in at Phase 5 (Apple Store move).
2. **Show only the multiplier you're proposing.** Don't display the 2x/3x/5x comparison table unless the prospect specifically asks — it creates analysis paralysis.
3. **Lead with the surrender value column.** Not the death benefit column. The cash growth is the conversion lever.

## Question 1 — "Why am I paying and getting nothing back?"

The answer:

> *"You're not. The whole point of this plan is that you DO get money back. By age 65, even if you make no claim, you'll have your premiums refunded plus accumulated bonuses. The plan's yield is around 2% p.a. — better than a bank, AND you've had cover the entire time.*
>
> *Think of it as killing two birds with one stone — coverage AND a savings vehicle. Your bank gives you neither cover nor meaningful interest. Make sense?"*

The closing question is the micro-commit.

The verbatim from source:

> *"Worst case is that you don't get cancer, don't pass away, don't get any illness — you will still get the money back."*

> *"If you put it in your bank, you don't get coverage and you also don't get any interest. Over here, you get both the coverage and the returns."*

---

## Question 2 — "What happens at 65 when the booster drops off?"

The answer:

> *"The base — what we call the Accumulator — keeps running. It's lifelong. Plus the non-guaranteed bonuses that have accumulated. So if your $300k cover today is $100k base + $200k booster, then at 65 you still have your $100k base plus bonuses — usually adding up to around $120k of cover.*
>
> *And by 65, your obligations are different. Kids grown, mortgage paid, retirement built. You need less cover. The booster falls off when you don't need the high cover anymore. Make sense?"*

The verbatim:

> *"Think of it like a reducing term rider. At the start your booster is quite a lot, but over time the coverage of the booster will start to go down."*

(Note: the source phrasing is slightly imprecise — the booster doesn't *gradually* reduce in modern GPP variants; it drops at 65. Use the cleaner "drops at 65" framing in your delivery.)

---

## Question 3 — "Can I withdraw my money if I need it?"

The answer:

> *"Yes — but only after the booster expires (so post-65 if you picked drop-off 65). Before that, the cash value is locked into the cover.*
>
> *Withdrawal is proportional. If you take out 50% of the accumulator, your cover drops by 50%. So we usually use the Income Drawdown Facility — a structured way to take 5% or 10% per year over 10 years. Better than ad-hoc withdrawals.*
>
> *For pre-65 emergencies, this isn't the right plan. That's what your emergency fund is for. You have one, right?"*

That last sentence is the fact-find AND micro-commit.

The verbatim:

> *"If you redraw 50%, then your coverage will be reduced by 50%."*

---

## Question 4 — "If I claim CI, do I still get the death payout?"

This is the **accelerated CI** question. Answer it the same way you handled the Day 3 Part 1 disclosure:

> *"Yes — but the death cover is reduced by the claim amount. So if your death cover is $500k and your CI cover is $150k and you claim the full $150k for cancer, your remaining death cover is $350k.*
>
> *That's how accelerated CI works — it's drawn down from the death benefit, not stacked on top. It keeps the premium affordable. If you want CI fully stacked on top of death cover, that's a different (more expensive) product structure.*
>
> *Does that distinction make sense? Want me to walk through the numbers?"*

The verbatim:

> *"If your death coverage is $500,000 and your CI coverage is $150,000, then your total coverage for death will actually be reduced if you claim for the whole early CI or the CI."*

---

## Question 5 — "What if I lose my job and can't pay?"

The answer:

> *"There's a feature called Premium Pause. After you've paid premiums for at least 3 years, if you lose your job, you can pause payments for up to 12 months.*
>
> *But — important — it's not free money. It's an interest-free loan for the first 12 months. After that, you have 12 months to pay it back interest-free. Beyond that, interest accrues.*
>
> *Most clients never use it. But it's there. Your salary's stable for the next few years, right?"*

The verbatim:

> *"There will be a 12-month period where you don't have to pay, but take note this is not free money — you still have to pay back."*

The numbers cheat sheet:

| Premium Pause requirement | Detail |
|---|---|
| Minimum premium history | 3 years paid |
| Prior employment requirement | At least 6 months |
| Deferment period unemployed | 2 months |
| Pause window | 12 months no payment |
| Interest-free repayment | 12 months |
| Worked example ($100/mo regular) | During repayment $100 + $100 (loan) = $200/mo |

## The handover-to-day-4 transition

If a prospect asks Question 1, 2, 3, 4, or 5 — they're curious. Today's answers handle them.

But if they ask:

- *"GPP is too expensive vs Term"*
- *"Whole life is overkill — just want pure protection"*
- *"I already have CI cover"*
- *"Why pay for cover I might never use?"*
- *"I'd rather buy term and invest the difference"*

— those are **objections**, not questions. They need the conviction stories from Day 4 Part 1. Recognise the difference: a question opens a door; an objection slams one shut.

## Other questions worth knowing

### "What about suicide?"

> *"Not covered in the first policy year. After year 1, it's covered."*

### "What about pre-existing conditions?"

> *"Hard exclusion. Anything pre-existing or any surgery before the policy starts is not covered. That's why I'd recommend buying when you're young and healthy — every year of delay risks something showing up that locks you out."*

The verbatim:

> *"Anything that's pre-existing, any surgery or pre-existing condition that happened before that, all of those things will not be covered."*

### "How long do I wait after diagnosis before I can claim?"

> *"7-day survival period after diagnosis. 90 days waiting for most listed conditions. 1 year for ADHD-related conditions."*

### "What about Vitality?"

> *"Vitality Platinum optional enrolment maintains the Power-Up Dollar bonus declining schedule — without it, the first-year boost (e.g. +15% on 3x) reduces yearly. With Vitality Platinum, the boost stays. Worth setting up; we'll cover it at delivery."*
