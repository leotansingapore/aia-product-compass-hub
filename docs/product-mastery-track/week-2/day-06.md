> **Today's frame:** PLP is a regular-premium investment-linked policy that combines lifetime protection (to age 100) with a wealth accumulation engine. One plan, two engines. Master the Plus-vs-Max distinction and the lifeboat analogy; the rest of the curriculum builds on these.

# Part 1 — PLP in 5 Minutes: What It Is, Who It's For, Why It Exists

> **The one idea for Part 1:** PLP is a "two-in-one" plan — one premium dollar splits between protection (the floor) and investment (the engine). It evolves with the buyer: protection-focused in their 20s-40s, retirement-focused in their golden years.

By the end of Part 1 you should be able to: (1) describe PLP in two sentences without notes, (2) name the Plus vs Max distinction (locked at issue, not alterable), (3) state the four most-quoted numbers cold, (4) recognise when PLP is the wrong product.

---

## What PLP actually is

A SGD-denominated, **regular-premium investment-linked policy (ILP)** issued by AIA. Combines lifetime protection (to age 100) with a Mercer-guided wealth accumulation engine. [Brochure p.1, Product Summary p.1]

The line that opens every PLP conversation:

> *"Pro Lifetime Protector is a one-stop protection and investment solution that helps you stay on top of your changing needs."* [Video 0]

Then the "two-in-one" framing:

> *"It's a two-in-one plan. A lot of people really love this idea of having a two-in-one because they don't have to pay for an additional plan for insurance — they can combine both."* [Video 5]

The brochure positioning:

> *"Mould critical illness coverage into a flexible investment plan. Sculpt the future you envision with life protection and coverage for 150 multi-stage critical illnesses as you fulfil your wealth accumulation goals."* [Brochure p.1]

> *"There is no one-size-fits-all plan for life. Which is why we've designed a one-size-fits-you plan to meet your needs."* [Brochure p.1]

## The Plus vs Max distinction (locked at issue)

This is the most important structural decision a PLP prospect makes — and it **cannot be altered after issue**.

| Feature | Plus | Max |
|---|---|---|
| Death benefit calculation | Insured Amount **+** Policy Value | **Higher** of Insured Amount or Policy Value |
| Sum-at-Risk over time | Constant | Decreases as policy value grows |
| Insurance charges over time | Stay constant | Drop to zero when Policy Value > Insured Amount |
| Total payout at death | Insured + accumulated value | Whichever is bigger (no double-pay) |
| Best for | Maximum legacy / death benefit | Self-funding insurance + retirement income |

The default recommendation is **Max** for most prospects — once policy value crosses the Insured Amount, insurance is essentially free.

The verbatim:

> *"For Max, the death benefit is the higher of insured amount or cash value. Once your policy value crosses beyond $100,000, you're not paying for any insurance anymore."* [Video 6]

> *"It's lifelong coverage. Until 100 years old. So in fact, after 65, the insurance is essentially free."* [Video 9]

## The protection-to-retirement evolution

The structural pitch:

> *"ILP is a solution that can evolve with your life stages — in your early 20s, 30s and 40s, that's when you have more liabilities and dependents. PLP can be a protection-focused plan. In your golden years, you can treat this plan as a retirement-focused plan."* [Video 1]

Same plan, two life stages.

## The four numbers you must know cold

| # | Number | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Age 100** | Policy maturity. Lifelong cover [PS p.1] |
| 2 | **18–70 / 0–17** | Adult / child entry age [Video 2, PS] |
| 3 | **S$100/month** | Minimum regular premium. Top-up min S$1,000 [PS p.5] |
| 4 | **2%** | Special bonus on regular premiums from 10th annual premium onwards [PS p.2] |

If a prospect asks any of these and you fumble, you've lost the room.

## The brochure's headline framing

> *"Get started from as low as S$7/day."* (illustrated as 30-yr-old male non-smoker, S$100k coverage, with TDA + LCC + Relapse, Pro Balanced) [Brochure p.1]

This is the price-anchor headline — but verify in iPOS at quote time. The illustrative profile is narrow.

## Target client profile

- **20s-40s** building protection that converts into retirement income
- **30s-50s** owning a permanent floor for legacy / estate / business succession
- **Existing GPP holder** wanting a permanent floor underneath the term-pay GPP

## Anti-market

The disclosure that builds trust:

> *"This plan isn't right for someone who wants pure investment only, prefers DIY/robo-advisors, is totally risk-averse to non-guaranteed returns, can't afford the S$100/month minimum, or wants short-term access to funds. PLP needs at least a 10-year horizon."* [Video 14]

Saying this out loud separates trustworthy FCs from product-pushers.

## The 5-minute intro script

> *"This is AIA Pro Lifetime Protector — PLP. Three things make it different from any other plan we offer.*
>
> *One — it's two-in-one. Protection plus investment, in one plan, on one premium. The premium splits — part funds the cover, part goes into a Mercer-guided sub-fund.*
>
> *Two — it's the only plan you can pause. After 10 years of paying, you can stop. The policy keeps growing. The cover keeps protecting. To age 100.*
>
> *Three — your insurance gets cheaper as your money grows, not more expensive. On the Max plan, once your policy value crosses your insured amount, insurance is free.*
>
> *Two structural choices to make: Plus or Max — different death benefit calculations. And one of three Mercer-guided portfolios — Adventurous, Balanced, Cautious. Which life stage are we solving for — protection now, or retirement income later?"*

That's 60 seconds. End with the question — they pick the life stage, the close has started.

# Part 2 — The Signature Pitch Concept: Limited-Pay Permanent Asset (Lifeboat-on-the-Ship)

> **The one idea for Part 2:** A ship needs a lifeboat. Investment alone is the ship; PLP welds the lifeboat in. This is the only AIA plan that lets you pay 10 years, then walk away — the policy keeps compounding and keeps protecting to age 100.

By the end of Part 2 you should be able to: deploy the lifeboat-on-the-ship analogy from memory, draw the limited-pay permanent-asset structure on paper, recite the No-Lapse Privilege mechanics, and walk a prospect through the post-Year-10 stop-paying scenario.

---

## The signature analogy

This is the verbatim from Video 14:

> *"100K death coverage is not just a ship, but a ship WITH a lifeboat — two-in-one protection."* [Video 14]

> *"It's not just an investment plan, but it gives you protection as well. So it's not just a ship, but a lifeboat. A ship has to contain a lifeboat. A ship without a lifeboat is not secure."* [Video 14]

This lands for high-net-worth prospects who think of investments as ships and worry about catastrophic events that sink them. The lifeboat = the death/CI cover that pays out regardless of investment performance.

## The limited-pay permanent-asset structure

The killer feature for PLP — pay short, own forever:

> *"You only have to pay for insurance for a limited period — maybe just 10 years. From now until age 30, you pay, then you stop. After that, you let the policy run on its own. Your coverage continues even after you stop paying, and you still get a pretty decent return. Put in $24k over 10 years, and you're looking at getting back about $1.8 million."* [Video 6]

> *"Pay for 10 years, then you don't pay anymore. You stop paying, and you just let the policy go. Your coverage actually is continuing even after you stop paying and you still get a pretty decent amount."* [Video 6]

The discipline:
- Pay 10 years → coverage continues to age 100 → cash value compounds → in retirement, partial withdraw or full surrender on Max plan converts the asset into income

## The 10-year No-Lapse Privilege (the safety net)

The mechanic that backs the limited-pay claim:

> *"Within the first 10 policy years, the policy will not lapse even if policy value is insufficient to pay all charges, provided all regular premiums are paid and no withdrawals made."* [PS p.7, Brochure footnote 8]

Even in a market crash during years 1-10, the policy survives as long as premiums are paid. After year 10, the prospect chooses: continue paying (compound faster) or stop (No-Lapse expires; policy continues to be supported by accumulated policy value).

## The premium charge schedule (the foundation of the math)

This is the single most important numeric sequence in PLP. Memorise:

| Policy Year | Premium Charge | What gets invested |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **80%** | 20% allocation |
| 2 | **55%** | 45% allocation |
| 3 | **50%** | 50% allocation |
| 4 | **8%** | 92% allocation |
| 5+ | **0%** | **100% allocation** |

[Product Summary p.3]

After 10 years, **102% of premium** flows in (100% + 2% special bonus) [Brochure p.3, PS p.2].

The "laptop" analogy that explains it:

> *"First year there's a very high charge of 80%, so $8,000 of $10,000 will be paid to insurance. Second year 55%, third year 50%, fourth year 8%, and fifth year onwards it's all paid to the plan. Think of it like buying a laptop — upfront there will be a lot of costs, but beyond that the charges drop to zero."* [Video 1]

> *"After 10 years, we give you an extra 2% special bonus. After 6 years it's 100% allocation versus the previous plan which was only 95%."* [Video 1]

This explains why the cash value looks LOW in the first 4 years — and why this is a feature, not a bug. The front-loading is the price of permanent + flexibility.

## The lifelong-coverage discount

For estate-planning prospects, the structural cost-saving vs term:

> *"It's lifelong coverage. Until 100 years old. So in fact, after 65, the insurance is essentially free."* [Video 9]

Pair this with the SFT comparison from Day 4 — extending term coverage from 65 to 95 at $500K cover costs ~$21,000/year. PLP doesn't pay that — it's already built in.

## Sum-assured discounts on Death Benefit Charge

| Insured Amount | Discount |
|---|---|
| ≥ S$120,000 | 5% [PS p.4] |
| ≥ S$250,000 | 8% [PS p.4] |
| First-year benefit-charge discount | 50% [PS p.4] |

## When NOT to pull out the lifeboat analogy

- **Prospect is a hardcore quant / DIY investor** — the analogy will feel slick; switch to spreadsheet math
- **Prospect already understands whole-life mechanics** — skip the analogy, jump to numbers
- **Time-boxed appointment <15 min** — analogy needs 90 seconds; truncate to verbal limited-pay framing

## Quiz
1. **PLP is best described as which type of policy?**
- A) A traditional whole-life par plan with guaranteed bonus
- B) A regular-premium investment-linked policy (ILP) combining lifetime protection with a Mercer-guided wealth engine ✓
- C) A pure investment unit trust with optional term rider
- D) A single-premium endowment with maturity at 65

**Why:** PLP is a SGD-denominated regular-premium ILP that combines lifetime cover to age 100 with a Mercer-guided sub-fund [Brochure p.1, PS p.1].

2. **Which statement about the Plus vs Max distinction is correct?**
- A) The buyer can switch between Plus and Max anytime after Year 5
- B) Plus pays the higher of Insured Amount or Policy Value at death
- C) Max pays Insured Amount plus Policy Value at death
- D) Plus vs Max is locked at issue and cannot be altered after the policy is in force ✓

**Why:** The Plus vs Max election is a structural decision locked at issue and cannot be changed later [Day 1 Part 1].

3. **On the Max plan, what happens to insurance charges once Policy Value crosses the Insured Amount?**
- A) Charges double because of additional sum-at-risk
- B) Charges drop to zero because Sum-at-Risk has gone to zero or negative ✓
- C) Charges convert to a flat $50/month policy fee
- D) Charges continue at the original Year-1 rate

**Why:** When Sum-at-Risk is zero or negative, no Benefit Charge is payable for that month — insurance is essentially free [Video 6, PS p.4].

4. **What is the lifeboat-on-the-ship signature analogy meant to convey?**
- A) That investment alone is sufficient as long as returns exceed 7%
- B) That PLP is purely an investment plan, not insurance
- C) That investment is the ship and PLP welds in the lifeboat — protection that pays regardless of investment performance ✓
- D) That clients should only buy PLP if they sail or work near water

**Why:** Investment alone is the ship; PLP welds in the lifeboat — death/CI cover that pays regardless of investment performance [Video 14].

5. **What is the minimum regular premium for PLP?**
- A) S$50/month
- B) S$100/month ✓
- C) S$200/month
- D) S$500/month

**Why:** The minimum regular premium is S$100/month, with top-up minimum of S$1,000 [PS p.5].

6. **What is the entry age range for adults vs children on PLP?**
- A) Adult 21-65, child 1-18
- B) Adult 18-70, child 0-17 ✓
- C) Adult 16-75, child 0-15
- D) Adult 25-60, child 5-17

**Why:** Adult entry age is 18-70 and child entry age is 0-17 [Video 2, PS].

7. **What is the special bonus on regular premiums and when does it begin?**
- A) 5% from the 5th annual premium onwards
- B) 1% from the 15th annual premium onwards
- C) 2% from the 10th annual premium onwards ✓
- D) 10% from the 20th annual premium onwards

**Why:** PLP awards a 2% special bonus on regular premiums from the 10th annual premium onwards, taking allocation to 102% [PS p.2, Brochure p.3].

8. **A prospect says: "I want pure investment with no insurance — can I just put my money in PLP?" What is the right response?**
- A) Yes, PLP is fine for pure investment because the cover is small
- B) PLP isn't right for pure-investment-only prospects; APA is the right plan ✓
- C) Yes, but only if they choose Pro Adventurous
- D) Yes, but only after a 14-day cooling period

**Why:** The anti-market disclosure: PLP isn't right for pure-investment-only buyers — pivot to APA [Video 14, Day 1 Part 1].

9. **A prospect asks: "If I pay $10K in Year 1, why does my account look so low?" Which is the correct framing?**
- A) AIA charges hidden management fees that are not disclosed
- B) The first-year premium charge is 80%, so most of Year 1's premium funds the cover; allocation rises to 100% by Year 5 — like buying a laptop, upfront cost, then drops to zero ✓
- C) The market dropped because of inflation
- D) The prospect should surrender immediately and try again next year

**Why:** The laptop analogy: Y1 premium charge is 80%, falling to 0% from Year 5 onwards — front-loading is a feature, not a bug [Video 1, PS p.3].

10. **Scenario: A 35-year-old SME owner with a $1M term gap, dependants and a clear 25-year horizon asks if PLP fits. Best response?**
- A) Decline — PLP is only for people aged 50+
- B) Recommend PLP only on the Plus plan for legacy
- C) Qualify protection foundation first (HSGM, accident, base CI), confirm 10+ year horizon and non-guaranteed-returns tolerance, then recommend PLP — likely Max for self-funding insurance ✓
- D) Recommend PLP single-premium top-up of $1M immediately

**Why:** PLP requires a 10+ year horizon, non-guaranteed-returns tolerance, and the protection foundation mapped before layering — Max is the default for most prospects so the policy can self-fund insurance over time [Day 1 Part 1, Video 6].
