# Part 1 — The Numbers: Premiums, Deductibles, Claim Limits, Cancer Care Pro Mechanics

> **The one idea for Part 1:** HSGM's premium has two layers — MediSave-payable (base + AIA top-up, capped by AWL) and cash-only (the rider). Lead the quote with the cash component. The 90-cent cheque story makes the deductible memorable; the worked example on brochure page 9 makes pre-authorisation matter.

By the end of Part 1 you should be able to: explain the MediSave / cash split correctly (no "fully MediSave-payable" claim), draw the deductible / co-insurance / cap structure on a whiteboard, recite the brochure-page-9 Tom worked example from memory, and quote Cancer Care Pro outpatient ceilings without notes.

## The premium structure (MediSave AWL + cash)

HSGM premium has two layers. The FC who quotes "fully MediSave-payable" is wrong:

| Component | Source of payment |
|---|---|
| MediShield Life base | MediSave (auto) |
| AIA top-up (HSGM portion) | MediSave UP TO the Additional Withdrawal Limit (AWL); excess in cash |
| AIA Max VitalHealth Pro rider | **100% cash** |
| Cancer Care Pro / Emergency Care Pro add-ons | **100% cash** |

For young adults, AWL on the AIA top-up is **S$300/year**. Typical AIA top-up Plan A is ~S$329/year — leaving ~S$29/year cash shortfall on the "MediSave portion" alone. [Video 8]

The script that prevents the most common confusion:

> *"There is a maximum withdrawal limit from MediSave. For the AIA hospital plan portion, the limit is S$300 a year. The shortfall — usually around S$29 — has to be paid in cash. So the hospital plan is not 100% MediSave-payable, even before the rider."*

When quoting, lead with the **cash all-in** — that's what hits the prospect's wallet.

## Deductible — the doorway

Ward-tied, not plan-tied. Deductible only applies to inpatient and day-surgery / short-stay benefits — outpatient benefits are not subject to deductible (but are subject to co-insurance).

| Ward (age 81 next birthday or younger) | Deductible |
|---|---|
| C-class | S$1,500 |
| B2 / B2+ | S$2,000 |
| B1-class | S$2,500 |
| A-class | S$3,500 |
| Private hospital (all wards excl. day surgery / short-stay) | S$3,500 |
| Day surgery / short-stay | S$2,000 |

[Brochure p.12]

## Co-insurance — the % you keep paying inside

| Without rider | **10%** of bill above deductible |
| With AIA Max VitalHealth Pro | **5%** capped at **S$6,000/policy year** |

The S$6,000 cap requires **at least one** of:
1. Treatment by an AIA preferred provider, OR
2. Certificate of pre-authorisation, OR
3. A&E referral from a Singapore hospital

If none — the 5% has no cap. [Brochure p.16]

The disclosure script that builds trust:

> *"Small bills, the deductible is irritating. Large bills, the co-insurance is scary. Without a rider, both ends hurt. With the rider, your maximum is S$6,000 a year — capped, no matter how big the bill."*

## The 90-cent cheque story (memorise)

> *"If your bill is S$3,500, can you claim anything? No. If your bill is S$3,501, how much can you claim? S$1, but you still pay 10 cents in co-insurance. AIA gave my client a cheque for 90 cents — the postage cost more than the cheque."*

Sells the deductible AND the rider in one anecdote.

## The S$1M worked example

> *"Imagine your bill is S$100,000. The deductible is S$3,500 — you pay that yourself. The co-insurance is 10% of the remainder, which is S$9,650. Total out of pocket without a rider: about S$13,150."*

Then scale up:

> *"On a S$1 million bill, the 10% co-insurance alone is roughly S$99,650. Without rider, that's S$103,150 out of pocket — for one event."*

The rider converts unbounded medical risk into a known annual maximum.

## The brochure-page-9 Tom worked example (memorise)

The single most useful concrete number to know:

```
Tom — S$200,000 private hospital bill
HSG Max A + AIA Max VitalHealth Pro A

Scenario A: AIA preferred provider AND pre-authorisation
  Deductible:  S$3,500
+ 5% capped:   S$6,000
= TOTAL OUT-OF-POCKET: S$9,500

Scenario B: Non-preferred provider, no pre-auth
  Deductible:        S$3,500
+ 5% straight:       S$9,825  (5% of S$196,500, no cap)
= TOTAL OUT-OF-POCKET: S$13,325

Difference: S$3,825 — just because of how the surgery was set up.
```

[Brochure p.9]

This number does the conviction work for "why pre-auth matters."

## Pro-ration (why MediShield Life alone fails for private)

Under MediShield Life alone, a private-hospital bill is pro-rated to **35%** before MediShield Life pays anything (sized for B2/C). HSGM A removes pro-ration for private bills (factor 100%). [Brochure p.8 + p.14]

| Plan | Pro-ration |
|---|---|
| HSG Max A | **100%** across the board |
| HSG Max B | 70% for inpatient/outpatient/day-surgery in private hospitals |
| HSG Max B Lite | 90% B1 (PR), 80% A-class, 50% private, 65% private outpatient |

This is why a prospect who says "I'll only ever go public" still benefits from at least Plan B Lite — emergencies don't ask which ward you wanted.

# Part 2 — Top 5 Client Questions: Crisp, Scripted Answers

> **The one idea for Part 2:** Five questions cover ~80% of what comes up in a real meeting. Memorise the answer in two clean sentences each — looking like an expert means not flipping through the brochure.

By the end of Part 2 you should be able to deliver each answer in under 30 seconds, with the closing question that hands the conversation back.

---

## Q1 — "Can I use this for an A&E visit if I just need stitches?"

**A:** No. HSGM is an inpatient plan. The trigger is being warded for at least six hours or undergoing surgery. For minor accident-only outpatient cover, that's what a **Personal Accident plan** is for.

> *"Hospital plan is for inpatient. For the everyday bumps and bruises — stitches, sprains, minor accidents — that's where the Personal Accident plan comes in."*

[Source: Video 4]

## Q2 — "What's the difference between this and CI insurance? Don't I already have CI?"

**A:** A CI plan is a **lump-sum payout** — money to you, to replace lost income for the years you cannot work. HSGM is **reimbursement** — money to the hospital, to cover the bill itself.

> *"CI alone gets eaten up by the bill. HSGM alone leaves no income while you recover. The two products solve different problems."*

[Source: Video 4 + Video 10]

## Q3 — "I already have employer/corporate medical. Why do I need this?"

**A:** Three reasons:
1. **Portability** — corporate cover disappears when the job disappears (retrenchment, career break, retirement, job switch)
2. **Pre-existing exclusion** — anything that develops while you're with this employer becomes uninsurable later if you lose the cover
3. **Cap gap** — corporate caps are typically well below S$2M annually

> *"HSGM is portable and lifetime — you keep it whether or not you keep the job. Lock it in now while you're healthy and underwriting is clean."*

[Source: Video 9]

## Q4 — "Is the hospital plan fully payable through MediSave?"

**A:** No, and any FC who tells you yes is wrong:
- MediShield Life base — paid from MediSave (auto)
- AIA top-up — MediSave-payable but only up to **S$300/year** AWL for young adults; ~S$29/year typically in cash
- Rider (AIA Max VitalHealth Pro) — **100% cash, no MediSave**

> *"So expect a small MediSave shortfall plus the full rider premium in cash."*

[Source: Video 8]

## Q5 — "What if I claim — do my premiums go up?"

**A:** **No.** AIA HealthShield Gold Max has **no change in premiums after a claim**. This is on the brochure cover.

> *"Premiums move with age and with periodic re-pricing across the whole portfolio, but they don't load up against the individual because that individual claimed. Some competitors use claims-based pricing where each claim pushes the next year's premium higher — AIA does not."*

[Source: Brochure p.1 + p.2, Video 4, Video 7, Video 19]

## Bonus question (rare but loaded) — "What about pre-existing conditions / waiting periods?"

> *"Pre-existing exclusions apply. Pregnancy complications: 10-month waiting. Congenital abnormalities: 10-month waiting (eligible expenses within first 24 months from child's birth). Living donor organ transplant: 24-month waiting. Anything that's pre-existing or surgery before policy start — not covered."*

[Brochure p.20]

## Quiz
1. **The HSGM premium is best described as:**
- A) Fully MediSave-payable for all components
- B) Fully cash, no MediSave
- C) Two layers — MediShield Life base + AIA top-up (MediSave-payable up to AWL) and the rider (100% cash) ✓
- D) Paid only on claim, never upfront

**Why:** The base + AIA top-up is MediSave-payable up to the Additional Withdrawal Limit; the rider (Pro A/B) is 100% cash. The "fully MediSave-payable" claim is wrong.

2. **The deductible is described as:**
- A) The annual policy claim limit
- B) The doorway — the amount you pay before insurance kicks in (ward-tied, applies to inpatient and day-surgery only) ✓
- C) The co-insurance percentage
- D) The premium loading for smokers

**Why:** Deductible is the doorway, ward-tied not plan-tied, and only applies to inpatient and day-surgery / short-stay benefits — outpatient is not subject to deductible.

3. **With the AIA Max VitalHealth Pro rider, co-insurance is:**
- A) 10% with no cap
- B) 5% with no cap
- C) 5% capped at S$6,000 per policy year (subject to cap conditions) ✓
- D) 0% — the rider eliminates co-insurance entirely

**Why:** The rider takes co-insurance to 5% capped at S$6,000/year, provided one of: AIA preferred provider OR pre-authorisation OR A&E referral [Brochure p.16].

4. **The deductible for an A-class ward (or any private hospital admission excluding day surgery) is:**
- A) S$1,500
- B) S$2,000
- C) S$2,500
- D) S$3,500 ✓

**Why:** A-class and private deductible is S$3,500. C-class is S$1,500, B2 is S$2,000, B1 is S$2,500 [Brochure p.12].

5. **The S$6,000 co-insurance cap requires at least ONE of which three conditions?**
- A) AIA preferred provider, pre-authorisation, or A&E referral ✓
- B) Plan A, full premium paid, no smoker loading
- C) Plan A, rider, Cancer Care Pro
- D) Pre-existing exclusion waiver, MediSave authorisation, signed declaration

**Why:** The cap conditions are AIA preferred provider, certificate of pre-authorisation, or A&E referral from a Singapore hospital. Without any one of these, the 5% has no cap.

6. **In the brochure-page-9 Tom worked example (S$200K private hospital bill, HSG Max A + Pro A), what is Tom's out-of-pocket WITH preferred provider AND pre-authorisation?**
- A) S$3,500
- B) S$6,000
- C) S$9,500 ✓
- D) S$13,325

**Why:** S$3,500 deductible + S$6,000 capped co-insurance = S$9,500 total OOP. Without pre-auth and a non-preferred provider, OOP rises to S$13,325 [Brochure p.9].

7. **For young adults, the AWL on the AIA top-up is approximately:**
- A) S$100/year
- B) S$300/year ✓
- C) S$600/year
- D) S$1,200/year

**Why:** The Additional Withdrawal Limit on the AIA top-up portion is S$300/year for young adults; typical AIA top-up Plan A is ~S$329/year, leaving ~S$29/year cash shortfall on the "MediSave portion" alone.

8. **A prospect asks, "Can I claim HSGM if I just need stitches at A&E?" The correct answer is:**
- A) "Yes — HSGM covers any A&E visit."
- B) "No — HSGM is an inpatient plan, triggered by being warded for at least 6 hours or undergoing surgery. For minor outpatient cover, that's a Personal Accident plan." ✓
- C) "Yes, but only at AIA-preferred providers."
- D) "Only if you have the rider."

**Why:** HSGM is inpatient — minimum 6 hours warded or surgery. Stitches without admission go to Personal Accident, not HSGM.

- A) "Yes — every claim adds 5% to next year's premium."

- D) "Only the rider portion goes up, not the base."

**Why:** No claim-based loading — this is on the brochure cover. Premiums move with age and portfolio re-pricing, but they don't load against the individual because that individual claimed.

10. **Scenario: Your 38-year-old client receives a S$1,000,000 cancer treatment bill. He has HSG Max A but no rider. What is his approximate out-of-pocket?**
- A) ~S$3,500
- B) ~S$6,000

**Why:** S$3,500 deductible + 10% of S$996,500 (~S$99,650) = ~S$103,150 OOP. The rider converts that unbounded exposure into a known S$6,000 annual maximum — that's why the rider is non-negotiable.
