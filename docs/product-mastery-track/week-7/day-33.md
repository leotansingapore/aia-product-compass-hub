> **Today's frame:** Move from problem-painting to problem-pricing. # Part 1 — The Numbers: Healthcare Angle + Color-Coded Policy Review

> **The one idea for Part 1:** Loss aversion (~2x stronger than gain motivation) is the conversion lever for pre-retirees. The healthcare angle ($412K lifetime premium) is loss made specific. The color-coded policy review (Green = Keep, Orange = Optimize, Red = Restructure/Surrender) makes the recommendation visual. Both convert PWV from a product to a portfolio decision.

By the end of Part 1 you should be able to recite the healthcare premium escalation numbers, deploy the asset-vs-expense reframe, classify any existing policy as Green/Orange/Red, and quote the PWV fee architecture cold.

---

## The Healthcare Cost Angle (the loss aversion hook)

This is the single most powerful emotional driver for pre-retirees. Every client already feels the pain of rising hospital premiums — the FC names it as a number.

The opener:

> *"Mr Tan, are you aware that based on your HealthShield Gold Max A-Plan, from age 55 to age 85, you will spend a total of $412,000 just to pay for this hospitalization plan premium?"*

[Video 2, Video 23]

The pain reinforcement:

> *"If you don't do anything today, you are still going to spend this $412,000 no matter what. Just for hospitalization plan."*

> *"And this is based on today's figures. The premium has been adjusted every 2-3 years over the past decade because of healthcare inflation."*

## The asset-vs-expense reframe (the conversion)

> *"Instead of paying this $400,000 to the insurer as an expense, what if we set this money aside today to build an asset for yourself? You'd generate enough income to pay for your hospitalization plan premium AND have excess for your lifestyle AND leave the capital to your family when you pass on."*

> *"Even at 6% dividend yield, we cover all the premiums. Even at 5%, we still cover all the premiums. But what our clients are actually getting is around 7%."*

The math:

```
$412,000 of HSGM premium over 30 years (age 55-85)
vs
$200K-$400K invested in PWV at 6% dividend
=
PWV income that COVERS the HSGM premium AND has excess for lifestyle AND preserves capital for family
```

## The "if you downgrade to public" pre-emption

If the prospect threatens to downgrade hospital cover:

> *"Once you downgrade to public, the cost drops — for yourself. But what about for your wife? Now we're looking at $600,000 for both of you."*

The number doubles. Most prospects rebound.

## The Color-Coded Policy Review (Green / Orange / Red)

After the healthcare angle, walk through the prospect's existing policies (pulled from SingPass at Phase 3). Label each:

### Green = KEEP
> *"Green means Keep — good value, adequate coverage, reasonable premiums."*

Examples:
- HSGM (regardless of plan tier — keep, possibly Optimize at the rider level)
- Recently bought CI plans with strong rider stack
- Term plans at favourable legacy premium rates

### Orange = OPTIMIZE
> *"Orange means Optimize — same plan, but we improve riders, switch funds, or change payment frequency."*

Examples:
- HSGM rider could be downgraded or upgraded
- Annual vs monthly payment (monthly costs ~2-3% more)
- Junior policy that could be upgraded to adult coverage

Premium savings examples [Video 19]:
| Optimisation | Annual savings |
|---|---|
| Rider downgrade A-plan to Value | ~S$1,400/year |
| Annual vs monthly payment | 2-3% |
| Junior to adult policy upgrade | 3x coverage for $30 more |

### Red = RESTRUCTURE / SURRENDER
> *"Red means Restructure or Surrender — poor returns, redundant coverage, or excessive cost."*

Examples (real client cases from Video 19):
- **NTUC whole life** — yielding 1.07% on long-held policy
- **GE Living Assurance** — $48K cash value vs $50K death after 29 years (protection essentially gone)
- **Pro Achiever ILP** — $2,800 growth over 7 years (~1.4% IRR)
- **Prudential** — cost of insurance escalation ($11/$1K at 55 → $67/$1K at 69)

The reframe script:

> *"Your NTUC whole life is yielding 1.07% — your bank fixed deposit gives you 2.5%. By restructuring this and your other two red policies, you'll save $7,000 per year. That's money that could be working for you instead."*

# Part 2 — Top 5 Client Questions: Crisp, Scripted Answers

> **The one idea for Part 2:** Pre-retirees ask the same five questions in 80% of meetings. Memorise scripted answers with specific numbers from the Product Summary — the FC who quotes "3.60% supplementary charge for the first 10 policy years" (not "around 3.5%") is perceived as a technician. That perception shift is what turns a hesitant client into a buyer.

By the end of Part 2 you should be able to deliver each answer in under 60 seconds with the page citation, distinguish curiosity from objection, and run all 5 answers in random order.

---

## Q1 — "What's the dividend rate, and is it guaranteed?"

**A:** We project at 6% to be conservative. The AIA Global Adventurous Income Fund has historically achieved around 7% per annum since inception.

> *"Dividends are not guaranteed — that's regulated and I have to say it — but I'll show you actual quarterly payouts from real clients so you can see what's been happening, not just projections. Here's what a client with $120K invested received last quarter: $2,100. That's real money, not a projection."*

[Sources: Brochure p.5, PS p.13]

The discipline:
- **Always say "not guaranteed"**
- Always project at 6%, never 7% or 8%
- Show real client payouts when possible

## Q2 — "What happens to my money if I die?"

**A:** Capital floor — your family receives the **higher** of two amounts:
- Total premiums you've paid in (less any withdrawals)
- Policy value at the time

> *"So if your account value is $300K when you pass, they get $300K. If the market drops and your account is at $150K, they still get back at least the $200K you put in. That capital floor is a contractual feature of the policy."*

[Source: PS p.1, Section 3.1]

This is the single most reassuring fact about PWV. Always emphasise.

## Q3 — "Can I take my money out early if I need it?"

**A:** Yes, with two layers:

| Layer | Mechanic |
|---|---|
| **Partial withdrawal** | From day 1, but charge factor decreases yearly (Year 1 = 2.333 → Year 8 = 0.250 → Year 11+ = 0) |
| **Full surrender** | After year 7, no surrender charges (70% Y1 → 50% Y5 → 30% Y7 → 0% Y11+) |

> *"After year 7 there are no surrender charges on full surrender either. So the plan is built to be liquid after year 7. If you need access in years 1-7, you have it — you just pay a charge. Most of my clients never trigger this."*

[Sources: PS p.5, Section 5.5; PS p.7, Section 6.4]

## Q4 — "How are the dividends paid out?"

**A:** Quarterly. Two options:

### Option A — Cash payout
- Paid into wallet via PayNow NRIC every quarter
- Use however you want

### Option B — Reinvestment
- Dividends buy more units inside your fund
- Grows your future dividend base

> *"You don't have to decide today; you can switch between the two at any time by sending a service request, and from the next quarter the new option takes effect."*

[Source: PS p.10, Section 8.4]

## Q5 — "What are the actual fees?"

**A:** Three to know:

### 1. Supplementary Charge — 3.60% p.a.
On Regular Premium Policy Value, deducted monthly, **first 10 policy years only**. **Zero from year 11 onwards.** [PS p.4]

### 2. Premium Charge — 3%
On any top-up premiums you make. [PS p.4]

### 3. Benefit Charge — age/gender-rated
Per S$1,000 of Sum-at-Risk per year. Sample at age 55 male: $7.12; female: $5.22. [PS p.14-15]

### Against these fees, you receive bonuses:

| Bonus | Offset |
|---|---|
| Welcome Bonus | Up to 35% over years 1-5 |
| Investment Bonus | 2.5% per year in years 9-12 |
| Performance Bonus | 0.30% per year from year 9 onwards |

> *"The bonuses largely offset the fees, and after year 10 the supplementary charge stops while the bonuses continue."*

## Bonus question — "Why not just keep my money in fixed deposit at 2.85%?"

**A:** Three reasons:

1. **Capital floor** — FD has FDIC up to limits but no insurance death benefit; PWV has the higher-of capital guarantee
2. **Yield** — 6% PWV vs 2.5% FD = $7,000 extra annual income on $200K capital. Over 20 years, ~$300K of lost compounding
3. **Reset risk** — FDs roll at the prevailing rate; SIBOR drops, your income drops. PWV dividend frequency is quarterly and the underlying fund yield has historically been more stable than FD rates.

[Source: Video 30]
