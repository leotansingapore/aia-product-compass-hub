# Day 5 — Compliance + Close: ILP Disclosures + Gentle Close + 12-Item Execution Checklist

> **Today's frame:** Compliance is foundation, not tax. Deliver crisply (not mumbled, not buried). Then gentle close — pre-retirees have time, capital, and skepticism, so high-pressure closes don't work. Always Two Never Three converts decisions into preferences. Complete the full 12-item checklist in one 89-minute session — every follow-up is a future cancellation in disguise.

This is the final module. Part 1 covers the 10 critical compliance points. Part 2 covers gentle close + 12-item execution checklist. Estimated read + drill: ~65 minutes.

# Part 1 — Compliance + Risk Disclosures (10 Critical Points)

> **The one idea for Part 1:** PWV is an ILP. Capital NOT guaranteed during the term. Dividends NOT guaranteed. Past performance NOT indicative. The FC who delivers these crisply earns trust because the client has been waiting to find out the catch — and naming risks first, then reframing, turns compliance into reassurance.

By the end of Part 1 you should be able to deliver all 10 critical compliance points cleanly, recognise when PWV is the wrong product, and pass an RNF sample audit without a coaching note.

## The 3 ILP pillar disclosures (verbatim, every meeting)

### Pillar 1 — Capital not guaranteed

> *"AIA Platinum Wealth Venture 2.0 is an Investment-Linked Plan. Investments in this plan are subject to investment risks including the possible loss of the principal amount invested."*

> *"The performance of the ILP sub-funds is not guaranteed. The value of the units may fall or rise. Past performance is not necessarily indicative of future performance."*

[Sources: PS p.13, Important Notes; Brochure p.10]

### Pillar 2 — Dividends not guaranteed

> *"Dividends are not guaranteed — both the rate and the frequency are determined at AIA's sole discretion. AIA may distribute dividends out of capital, which would reduce your original investment."*

[Sources: PS p.13; Brochure p.9, footnote #]

### Pillar 3 — When you say 6%, you mean a working assumption

> *"When I project at 6%, that is a conservative working assumption — not a promise. Our fund has historically delivered around 7% on average, but historical performance is not a guarantee of future performance."*

The discipline: ALWAYS use 6% in client conversation. Never 7%, never 8%. The brochure case studies show 8% — disregard those when projecting in front of a real client.

## The reframe discipline (compliance into reassurance)

The technique:
1. State the risk crisply
2. Name the structural protection that addresses it
3. Move on

Examples:

| Risk stated | Reassurance reframe |
|---|---|
| "Capital not guaranteed" | "Which is why we project at 6% conservative — actual fund history is ~7% — and the death benefit floor is contractual" |
| "Dividends not guaranteed" | "Which is why we deploy across 5 years for dollar cost averaging, and you can switch between cash payout and reinvestment any time" |
| "Fees can be revised" | "Which is why we underpromise on returns — even at 5% the math works for your retirement income gap" |

Don't dwell on the risk. State, reframe, move.

## The "anti-market" disclosure

> *"This plan isn't right for everyone. The anti-market for PWV is people who:*
>
> *- Need short-term liquidity (PWV requires 7-10 year horizon)*
> *- Want fully guaranteed returns (consider CPF Life or fixed deposits)*
> *- Are uncomfortable with non-guaranteed dividend income*
> *- Don't have at least 5 years of stable premium-paying capacity*
>
> *If any of those describe you, PWV isn't the right plan. Be honest with me."*

If the prospect hesitates on any of these, slow down. The wrong sale is worse than no sale.

## The full RNF-clean closing block

Deliver as one block right before signature:

> *"Before we authenticate, four things to make sure I've covered:*
>
> *One — capital is NOT guaranteed during the term. Dividends are NOT guaranteed. Past performance is NOT indicative.*
>
> *Two — 14-day free-look from receipt of policy documents. During free-look you can cancel; AIA refunds premiums minus market value adjustments and any medical fees.*
>
> *Three — surrender and partial withdrawal charges apply in years 1-10. Premium Holiday Charge applies if you pause regular premium payments.*
>
> *Four — fees and charges aren't guaranteed. AIA reserves the right to vary with at least 1 month's written notice.*
>
> *Any of those concern you?"*

If "no" — RNF audit pass. Move to in-meeting execution.

If "yes" — slow down. Address it.

# Part 2 — Gentle Close + In-Meeting Execution (12-Item Checklist)

> **The one idea for Part 2:** High-pressure closes don't work with pre-retirees. Gentle closes outperform. Always Two Never Three converts decisions into preferences. Build micro-yeses throughout — by the closing question, the client has already said yes 15 times. Complete EVERYTHING in the closing meeting (89 minutes); every follow-up needed for "later paperwork" is a future cancellation in disguise.

By the end of Part 2 you should be able to deliver the gentle close cleanly, run all 12 items of the closing checklist in one session, set the dividend preference at point of sale, and book the post-sale rhythm.

---

## The Always Two Never Three principle

Limit choices to exactly two options at every decision point. Pre-retirees are good at preferences, bad at decisions.

Examples:

> *"Would you prefer to start with $30K or $42K annually?"*

> *"Would you like dividends paid quarterly to your wallet, or reinvested for the first 3 years?"*

> *"Should we set this up under your name, or your spouse's?"*

Three options paralyses. Two creates a choice between two yeses.

## The exit options (reduce commitment anxiety)

For pre-retirees, naming the exits reduces the emotional barrier:

> *"You have 14 days to review and cancel with full refund — no questions asked."*

> *"We can submit the application without any payment first, just to see if you qualify under the underwriting. If anything comes up that you're not comfortable with, you walk away with no commitment."*

> *"Begin with $10-12K, monitor for a year, then scale up — that way you see the dividend behaviour with your own eyes before committing to the full size."*

Three exits stacked = no excuse to delay.

## The micro-yeses framework

By the time the closing question is asked, the prospect has said yes ~15 times throughout the meeting:

| "Yes, $36K annually fits my cashflow" | Phase 6 |
| "Yes, my spouse should be at the next meeting" | Phase 7 |
| ...etc | |

The closing "yes" feels like the natural extension of all the previous yeses.

## In-meeting execution scripts

### SingPass authentication

> *"Let's do the SingPass authentication right now — pulls your particulars in under a minute."*

### PayNow setup for premium payment

> *"For PayNow we need to bump your daily limit before we transfer. Open your bank app, I'll walk you through it."*

> *"For amounts over $100K, we'll stagger across 2-3 days. Some banks need advance notice for large transfers."*

### Beneficiary nominations (paper + electronic + witnesses)

> *"Beneficiary nomination — paper form plus electronic with two witnesses. I have witnesses available right now."*

Have two staff members on standby for witness signatures. This is non-negotiable.

### Dividend preference setup

> *"Let me set up the dividend preference now — reinvest or payout? You can switch later but we set the default today."*

### GIRO for ongoing top-ups

> *"GIRO setup for any future top-ups — we'll do it now while you have your bank details out."*

Run every item. Skip none.

## The benchmark (what good looks like)

A senior FC running PWV cleanly produces:

- **2-3 PWV cases per week** (longer cycle than HSGM/PA)
- **40%+ first-meeting → close conversion** (Meeting 1 to Meeting 2 within 10 days, then close)
- **Average case S$200K-$300K invested over 5 years** (i.e., $40K-$60K/year premium)
- **80%+ close-rate within Meeting 2** (when Meeting 1 was run cleanly)
- **2+ adjacent sells per closed case within 90 days** (HSGM upgrade, GPP/PLP for protection floor, family plans)

That's the target.

## The "case is closed" definition

A PWV case is NOT closed at iPOS submission. It's closed when:

1. ✅ iPOS submitted
2. ✅ FHR signed
3. ✅ Payment received
4. ✅ Beneficiary nominations witnessed and filed
5. ✅ Dividend preference set
6. ✅ GIRO for top-ups configured
7. ✅ Policy contract issued
8. ✅ 14-day free-look window passed without cancellation
9. ✅ First quarterly dividend paid (proof of income stream)
10. ✅ 6-month review meeting completed
11. ✅ Spouse / referral conversation surfaced

If you stop at iPOS and never run #4-11, you closed a fraction of a case. PWV retention compounds with every subsequent dividend payment.
