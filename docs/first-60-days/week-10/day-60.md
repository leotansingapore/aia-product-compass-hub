# Day 60 — Claims Handling + Graduation

> **The one idea for today:** You never truly understand the purpose of this career until you process your first major claim. The day a CI payout reaches a client mid-diagnosis, the day a death benefit lands with a grieving family — that's when the 60 days of grinding, prospecting, and studying suddenly make sense. The career changes you at that moment.

## What you'll walk away with

By the end of today you should be able to:

1. **Handle** both minor and major claims correctly — the process, the tone, the empathy.
2. **Reflect** on your 60 days — what you built, what you learned, what's next.
3. **Commit** to the next 60 days with a specific, grounded plan.

---

## 1. Claims — the part of the work that matters most

Every policy you sell over the course of your career is, fundamentally, a promise. Claims are where promises are kept.

Most of the work — prospecting, fact-finding, proposals, closing — is in the **service of** the moment when a claim comes in.

There are two claim categories:

| Category | Examples | Your role |
|---|---|---|
| **Minor claims** | Outpatient accidents, food poisoning, minor injuries, small hospital stays | Process efficiently, keep the client informed |
| **Major claims** | Critical illness, permanent disability, death | Process with care + empathy; support the client / family emotionally |

## 2. Handling minor claims — the 5-step process

For outpatient accidents, small medical events, and hospital stays classified as minor:

### Step 1 — Client documents the claim
- Client takes clear photos of all receipts.
- Any medical reports are scanned.
- Incident description is noted.

### Step 2 — Upload to the claims portal
- Use the AIA Claims portal.
- Complete the advisor report (how incident happened, area of injury).
- Attach all receipts and medical documents.

### Step 3 — Keep a personal record
- Save copies in your own Drive / CRM.
- Note the claim number and submission date.
- Makes follow-up faster.

### Step 4 — Follow up
- AIA may request additional information.
- Respond within 24 hours.
- Keep client updated ("AIA is processing; they've asked for X; I've sent it").

### Step 5 — Payment
- Cheque or PayNow.
- Confirm receipt with client.
- Ask: "Everything clear? Any questions?"

**Rule:** a well-handled minor claim is worth 10 marketing touchpoints. Clients remember how you responded during small medical events — they refer you to family because of it.

## 3. Handling major claims — a different job

Major claims are not administrative tasks. They are **moments of service.**

The three main categories:

### Critical Illness claims
- Client receives a CI diagnosis.
- Your job: process the lump-sum payout while they're dealing with doctors, treatment, family.

**What matters:**
- **Empathy first.** "I'm so sorry. How are you holding up? How can I help beyond the claim?"
- **Process quickly.** The client needs cash to make decisions (treatment choices, home modifications).
- **Provide clarity.** Explain what they'll receive, when, and how.
- **Be available.** Respond within hours, not days.

### Permanent Disability claims
- Client is unable to work.
- Often follows a major medical event.

**What matters:**
- **Ongoing support**, not a one-time transaction. Monthly disability income claims need ongoing management.
- **Advocacy with the insurer.** If something is unclear, you advocate for the client, not the insurer.
- **Connect them to resources.** Support groups, rehab programs, government schemes.

### Death claims
- The hardest. The most important.
- You're processing a payout for a family that just lost a member.

**What matters:**
- **Attend the funeral / wake.** Not optional. Your presence says more than anything else.
- **Initiate the claim without being asked.** The family has enough to deal with.
- **Handle all paperwork.** Only ask them to sign what's absolutely required.
- **Be there in Week 3, Week 10, Month 6.** Grief doesn't end when the payout clears.

## 4. The emotional weight of a major claim

Your first major claim will change you. Here's what tends to happen:

- You realise the numbers on policies — $500K, $1M — are real money, arriving in real family hands, at real moments.
- You realise the CI rider you almost didn't recommend — that saved a family from bankruptcy.
- You realise the late-night compliance work matters — because the family's payout depends on it.

Most FCs report that processing the first major claim is when they **genuinely commit** to the career. Not for the commission. For the purpose.

**The honest warning:** major claims also wear you down emotionally. You are witnessing hard things happen to good people. Build your own support systems — talk to your mentor, your partner, a therapist if needed. You can't process these alone forever.

The loop never stops. A client you closed in Month 2 re-enters the cycle at "Service" in Month 14. A referral from that client starts again at "Reconnect." Your book of business is just the sum of how many people are moving through this loop at any given time.

### Weekly target cadence (first 180 days)

These are the numbers that produce a sustainable pipeline. Miss them for a week — recover. Miss them for a month — you're rebuilding.

| Activity | Weekly target | Why this number |
|---|---|---|
| **Reconnecting meetings** | 5 | Keeps the top of the funnel warm |
| **CSTs / fact-finds** | 5 | The main skill-rep |
| **Policy summaries + proposals** | 3 | Where closes get manufactured |
| **Referrals asked for** | 20 | You get ~50% names → ~40% meetings |

These four numbers, run every week for 180 days, produce what most call "a real practice."

## 7. Proactive service — the four buckets

Claims are *reactive* service — you're handling an event that already happened. The other, more important half of service is **proactive**: staying present in the client's life so that when something does happen, they call you first, not their bank, not Google, not another FC.

Proactive service splits into four buckets. Run all four, every year, for every client. This is how you build a book that refers, renews, and never churns.

### Bucket 1 — Immediately after a sale
The first 30 days after a close set the tone for the next 30 years.

- Email confirmation of what was purchased
- Red folder handover (policies, contacts, benefits summary)
- Personalised policy summary (what they actually own, in plain English)
- Thank-you gift
- Beneficiary nomination (don't skip this)
- Referral ask (warm, not pushy)

### Bucket 2 — Pre / Post appointment
Every meeting before and after the close.

- Proposal (ahead of the meeting)
- Policy summary (live document, updated each meeting)
- Post-appointment summary (objectives discussed, next action, timeline)

### Bucket 3 — Routine systems
Annual cadences. Cheap to run. Expensive to forget.

- Birthday greeting + small gift
- Mooncake delivery (Mid-Autumn)
- CNY / Deepavali / Hari Raya / Christmas greetings
- End-of-year greeting card
- Annual diary / calendar delivery
- Pending-service memos (renewal notices, premium alerts)

### Bucket 4 — Ad hoc life events
Client's life keeps moving. You show up when it matters.

- Wedding
- New home
- Hospitalisation
- New child
- Bereavement
- Major career move

Every client should touch at least one bucket per quarter. Most will touch two. The ones who go a full year without a real touchpoint are the ones who leave for another FC at renewal.

**The operator's rule:** service is not something you do when you have time. It is the thing that *makes* the time, because it creates the renewals and referrals that mean you don't have to keep climbing from zero.

## 9. The next 60 days — the plan

Day 60 is not a finish line. It's a checkpoint.

### Your next 60-day priorities

1. **Double down on the 2 activities that worked best** in Days 1–60.
2. **Drop the 2 activities that didn't produce.** Be honest.
3. **Fix the 2 skills that are holding you back.** Role-play them weekly.
4. **Add 30 new names to your prospect list.**
5. **Set a specific production target** for Days 61–120. Not "more" — a number.

### The big mental shift for Days 61+

The first 60 days were about **learning.** The next 60 are about **repeating.**

You've been taught the full arc. Now you run the arc, dozens of times, refining each iteration.

**The reps from here are what make you a career FC.**

## 10. The 3-year target

Today is Day 60. Three years from today = Day 1,095.

What does the Year-3 version of you look like?

- 150–300 active clients.
- Annual review rhythm running for a year or two.
- First major claims processed.
- First real renewal-book income arriving regardless of new sales.
- MDRT qualification (or clear path to).
- A few direct referrers who fund 30%+ of your new business.
- A publicly visible brand (social, referrals, recognised in your niche).

**This is achievable.** It's the 1% compounding daily over 1,095 days.

**The math:** Day 60 you → Day 1,095 you is roughly 18× more capable. Not 2×. Not 5×. Eighteen times.

## 11. The final frame

Six 10-word truths to carry into Day 61:

1. **Activities × Skills × Knowledge = results.** All three matter.
2. **Sell slightly less. Service a lot more.** Longevity beats volume.
3. **The client's problem matters more than your sales target.**
4. **Rejection is reps. Reps are the skill. The skill is the career.**
5. **Protect your Q2 time. Everything else fights for scraps.**
6. **Be the advisor your clients refer to their own children.**
