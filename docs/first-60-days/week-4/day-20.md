# Day 20 — Basic Productivity & Time Efficiency

> **The one idea for today:** You don't have a "time" problem. You have a *priority* problem. The difference between a $60K FYC and a $180K FYC is rarely hours worked — it's which hours you protect, and for what.

## What you'll walk away with

By the end of today you should be able to:

1. **Protect** at least one hour per day that is *only* for revenue-generating work.
2. **Batch** similar tasks to reduce context-switching cost.
3. **Track** your actual time usage for one week and confront the gap between stated and real priorities.

---

## 1. The core productivity insight

"It's not the hours you put in your work that counts; it's the work you put in the hours."

A new FC who works 60 hours a week doing admin, studying, and scrolling LinkedIn will get crushed by a new FC who works 35 hours a week doing prospecting, meetings, and focused study.

Output is not linear with input. It's linear with **the right input.**

Most new FCs:
- Spend 20% of their week in revenue-generating activity.
- Spend 50% in preparation, planning, and admin.
- Spend 30% in distraction (social media, messaging, "research").

Top producers invert the first two. The 30% distraction gets compressed into specific, scheduled breaks.

## 2. What counts as revenue-generating activity

These are the only activities that directly or nearly-directly produce commissions:

1. **Prospecting** — outreach, follow-up, referral asks.
2. **Client meetings** — first meeting, fact-find, proposal, close.
3. **Proposal preparation** — illustrations, needs analysis, presentations.
4. **Service touchpoints** — annual reviews, claim help, policy updates.
5. **Content creation / visibility work** (long-term lead gen).

**What doesn't count (even though it feels productive):**
- Product study (necessary, but lagging).
- Admin / filing / expense reports.
- Team chat / slack.
- Training attendance.
- Reorganising your CRM for the third time.
- Researching new frameworks.

None of those are bad. They just aren't the work that pays. Most days, spend ~60% of your productive hours in the top list and only 40% in the bottom list.

## 3. Protect the peak hour

**Find your 60–90 minutes of peak focus.** For most people it's 8:30–10:30am. Some people are evening owls — 9pm–11pm.

Whenever it is, that hour is **off-limits** for anything non-revenue-generating. No meetings, no admin, no email, no team chat.

In that hour, you do **one** of:
- Prospecting calls.
- Preparing for today's client meeting.
- Writing a client proposal.
- Creating public content.

This single discipline separates the top quartile from the rest. Not genius. Not extra effort. Just protected hours.

**How to protect it:**
- Block it in your calendar as "Deep Work" or "Revenue Block."
- Phone on do-not-disturb.
- No Slack, no Telegram open.
- No browser tabs except what the task needs.
- One task, one hour.

## 4. Batching — the context-switching tax

Every time you switch tasks, your brain pays a **~20 minute refocus tax.** Research consistently shows this.

Four task types spread across the day = four refocus taxes = **~80 minutes/day lost** to context-switching.

**The fix — batch.** Group similar tasks, do them in one block.

| Batched block | What goes in it |
|---|---|
| **Morning Revenue Block** | Prospecting calls, proposal writing, deep work |
| **Mid-morning Meeting Block** | 2–3 back-to-back meetings |
| **Late afternoon Admin Block** | Email, Slack, CRM notes, expense claims |
| **End of day Learning Block** | Product study, role-play prep |

Each block is **one task type.** Inside the block, you don't switch.

This alone can recover 5–8 hours per week.

## 5. The "lifetime value" mental trick

When a small task arrives that feels urgent — an email, a client text, a Slack mention — most FCs respond immediately, breaking their current block.

**The question to ask instead:**
> "Will this matter in 6 months?"

If yes, handle it now.
If no, **batch it into the next Admin Block.**

Clients who want you to respond in 30 seconds will get the same result if you respond in 3 hours — inside your batch. The urgency is usually their perception, not a real deadline.

## 6. Track your time for one week

Here's the exercise that changes everyone who does it:

For **one week**, log what you do in 30-minute increments. A simple spreadsheet or note works.

At the end of the week, total the hours by category:

| Category | Hours |
|---|---:|
| Prospecting | ? |
| Client meetings | ? |
| Proposal prep | ? |
| Product / CMFAS study | ? |
| Admin | ? |
| Training attended | ? |
| Messaging / social / phone (non-work) | ? |
| Transit / logistics | ? |

Compare to what you *thought* you were doing.

The gap is always bigger than you expected. That gap is where your Year 1 income is hiding.

## 7. Three procrastination-killer rules

### Two-minute rule
If something takes under 2 minutes and is in front of you, do it immediately. Replies. Calendar blocks. Small CRM updates. Don't let them pile up.

### One-touch rule
When a task arrives in your inbox/calendar/CRM, decide in that moment: **do it, delegate it, defer it to a specific block, or delete it.** Don't look at it twice without action. Clicking on an email 4 times before replying is 3 wasted clicks.

### Ten-minute rule
For any task you're procrastinating on, commit to just 10 minutes. Set a timer. Start. 90% of the time, momentum carries you past 10 minutes. The resistance was at the start line, not in the task itself.

## 8. Done > perfect

"Done is better than perfect." — a core principle that kills most perfectionism.

The perfectly structured proposal delivered 3 days late is worse than the 80% proposal delivered tomorrow. The unfinished LinkedIn post drafted for a week is worse than the rough post published today.

Start before you feel ready. Publish before it's polished. Send before you've re-read it the fifth time.

**Velocity matters more than polish** in Year 1. Polish comes from reps. Reps come from velocity.
