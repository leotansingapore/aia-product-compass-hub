# Day 22 — Productivity Principles: Schedule Your Priorities

> **The one idea for today:** "The key is not to prioritize what's on your schedule — it's to schedule your priorities." (Stephen Covey.) Most new FCs have priorities in theory. Top producers have priorities on the calendar.

## What you'll walk away with

By the end of today you should be able to:

1. **Distinguish** strategic priority-setting (this lesson) from tactical productivity (Day 20).
2. **Plan** your week, not just your day — using a weekly rhythm.
3. **Install** a "done over perfect" default for drafts, proposals, and content.

---

## 1. Yesterday's lesson, today's lesson

Day 20 was tactical: **how to protect your peak hour, batch tasks, and kill procrastination moment-by-moment.**

Today is strategic: **how to decide what actually matters in the next 7 days, 30 days, 90 days** — so that when you sit down in your peak hour, you're working on the *right thing.*

Both layers are needed. Great tactics on wrong priorities is just fast-failing.

## 2. The weekly planning ritual

Most new FCs plan day-by-day. Top producers plan week-by-week, review weekly, and adjust.

**Sunday evening, 30 minutes:**

1. **Review last week.**
 - What activity did you do? (Calls, meetings, content, study.)
 - What did you close? What's in pipeline?
 - Where did you leak time?

2. **Decide next week's 3 priorities.**
 - Not 10. Three.
 - Make them specific and measurable.
 - Example: "15 outreach calls Monday–Wednesday. 2 client proposals written by Thursday. Post 1 LinkedIn article by Friday."

3. **Block the calendar first.**
 - Peak-hour blocks (60–90 min each morning).
 - Meeting blocks.
 - Sunday review slot next week.
 - **Then** fit in everything else.

4. **Protect one evening for life.**
 - Friday dinner, Saturday activity, or Sunday family time. Non-negotiable.
 - Without this, you burn out in Month 3. (Day 18's "shadow side" of 10X.)

30 minutes on Sunday protects 168 hours of the coming week.

## 3. Schedule priorities, don't prioritise your schedule

This is the Covey reframe, and it matters.

**Wrong approach:** look at your calendar Monday morning. See what's there. Decide what to do based on what's already scheduled.

**Right approach:** decide what the most important 3 things are. **Put them on the calendar.** Defend those blocks. Everything else fights for the remaining time.

Most new FCs arrive at Monday with:
- A client meeting someone else put on the calendar.
- Training sessions someone else scheduled.
- No time carved out for prospecting.
- Realising by Wednesday that they've done no prospecting.

The fix: **prospecting goes on the calendar first, before anyone else's requests.** It's the revenue engine. Everything else bends around it.

## 4. Done is better than perfect

Perfectionism is procrastination wearing a lab coat.

**Where new FCs lose weeks to perfectionism:**

- **Proposals** — re-formatting the deck 7 times before sending.
- **Public content** — drafting a LinkedIn post for 5 days before not posting it.
- **Cold call scripts** — rewriting the opener until it's "just right" (it never is).
- **Bio / profile / LinkedIn page** — tweaking headshots and headers for 3 weekends.
- **CRM / tools / systems** — re-organising Notion/Asana workspaces for 4 hours.

None of those activities produce income. They produce the *feeling* of productivity without the substance.

### The 80% rule

For almost everything, **80% polish in 20% of the time beats 100% polish in 100% of the time.** The last 20% of polish consumes 80% of the effort and rarely changes outcomes.

**Operational tests:**

- **Proposal:** is it factually correct, professionally formatted, and on-brand? Send.
- **LinkedIn post:** does it have one clear idea, no typos, a specific CTA? Post.
- **Cold call script:** does it make the first 30 seconds useful? Use it for 20 calls, then iterate.

## 5. Minimum Viable Product (MVP) thinking for FCs

MVP is a software-world concept. It translates directly to our work.

| Traditional (perfectionist) | MVP (velocity-first) |
|---|---|
| Build perfect proposal template. Launch in month 3. | Write a rough proposal. Send 5 of them. Refine based on client reactions. |
| Spend 2 weeks perfecting LinkedIn presence. | Post your first piece this Sunday. Iterate weekly. |
| Memorise entire product suite before first meeting. | Learn top 3 products. Run 5 meetings. Study what came up. |
| Build elaborate prospecting spreadsheet. | Start with Notion notes. Upgrade when the system visibly breaks. |

**The principle:** get to a real user (client, prospect, audience) with something rough. Let real feedback shape the refinement. Don't refine in a vacuum.

## 6. Overcoming perfectionism — the 3-question filter

When you catch yourself polishing, ask:

1. **"Is this improving the client outcome, or soothing my anxiety?"**
 Most polishing is self-soothing.

2. **"Would my mentor say this is ready to ship?"**
 If yes, ship. If you don't know, ask quickly.

3. **"What's the cost of delaying by a day?"**
 Usually zero. Sometimes the cost is a lost client.

If perfection isn't serving the client, it's serving your ego. Ship anyway.

## 7. The resistance insight

> "The problem is not doing the work but just starting the work. Resistance provides the beating heart of procrastination."

Most days, the hardest part is **the first 10 minutes.** Once you're into a task, flow carries you.

So the tactical move: **lower the activation energy.**

- Don't schedule "write proposal." Schedule "open the proposal doc and write for 10 minutes."
- Don't schedule "make calls." Schedule "dial the first number on the list."
- Don't schedule "post on LinkedIn." Schedule "write the first sentence."

Start stupid small. Momentum handles the rest.

## Quick quiz

1. **"Schedule your priorities" vs "prioritise your schedule" — what's the difference?**
 - A) Just semantics; same meaning
 - B) The first puts priority items on the calendar *first*; the second reacts to whatever's already there ✓
 - C) Scheduling is for top-down planners; prioritising is for bottom-up workers
 - D) The first is strategic; the second is tactical — both are fine

 **Why:** The Covey reframe is that most new FCs arrive Monday and work from whatever is already on the calendar — client meetings others booked, training others scheduled — with no prospecting time carved out, realising by Wednesday the pipeline is empty. Scheduling your priorities means deciding the 3 most important outcomes first, putting them in the calendar, and defending those blocks against everything else. The two approaches are not equivalent (A), and D is wrong because "both are fine" misses the point that one approach systematically produces the wrong results.

2. **MVP thinking applied to an FC's career means:**
 - A) Start with the cheapest products
 - B) Ship rough things fast, iterate from real feedback ✓
 - C) Minimum viable performance on all tasks
 - D) A staged launch plan

 **Why:** MVP (Minimum Viable Product) thinking means getting something real in front of a real user — client, prospect, audience — as quickly as possible, then refining based on their actual responses rather than imagined perfection. The lesson illustrates this with proposals sent rough vs. perfected in isolation, and LinkedIn posts published this Sunday vs. drafted for two weeks. It is not about cheapest products (A), acceptable mediocrity across the board (C), or a formal staged rollout plan (D).

3. **The hardest part of most productive work is:**
 - A) Finishing well
 - B) Deciding what to work on
 - C) The first 10 minutes of starting ✓
 - D) Concentration during the middle

 **Why:** The lesson cites the "resistance" insight directly — the problem is not doing the work but starting it, and once you are inside a task, flow usually carries you forward. This is why the tactical advice is to lower activation energy by scheduling something stupidly small ("dial the first number" rather than "make calls"). Finishing (A) and mid-task concentration (D) are not identified as the primary resistance point. Deciding what to work on (B) is addressed in the weekly planning ritual, not named as the daily friction point.

4. **An FC's Sunday plan for next week reads: "Get better at prospecting. Improve LinkedIn. Be more consistent with follow-ups." Applying today's ritual, the main problem with this list is:**
 - A) There are too few items — three is light for a full week
 - B) The items are not specific or measurable, so they can't be calendar-blocked or verified at week's end ✓
 - C) Personal development goals shouldn't be mixed with work goals
 - D) The list should be ranked by urgency before being acted on

 **Why:** The ritual requires priorities to be both **specific and measurable** — the lesson's own example reads "15 outreach calls Mon–Wed, 2 proposals written by Thursday, 1 LinkedIn article posted by Friday." Vague verbs like "get better" or "be more consistent" can't be scheduled into a calendar block, can't be defended against intrusions, and produce no clear yes/no at week's end — the FC finishes the week feeling busy but unable to prove anything moved. Three is the correct number, not too few (A). Mixing personal and work priorities is fine (C) — what matters is concreteness, not category. Ranking by urgency (D) is the wrong selection criterion entirely; urgency confuses what's loudest with what's most important.

5. **An FC rewrites their cold-call opener for the fourth time without having tested it on a real prospect. According to the MVP principle, the better action is:**
 - A) Refine one more time until it feels perfect
 - B) Have a mentor review it before using
 - C) Use the current version for 20 calls and iterate based on real responses ✓
 - D) Draft two versions and A/B test them in theory

 **Why:** MVP demands real-world feedback from real users — 20 actual calls will reveal far more about what works than any number of solo rewrites or theoretical comparisons. Refining again (A) is perfectionism compounding; the script will never feel perfect until it has been tested. A mentor review (B) is still vacuum-refinement rather than live feedback. Theoretical A/B testing (D) is contradicted by the principle that real user reactions, not imagined ones, should drive iteration.

6. **The weekly planning ritual described includes protecting one evening per week for personal life. The primary reason given is:**
 - A) Work-life balance is a company policy
 - B) Without protected personal time, burnout hits around Month 3 ✓
 - C) Evenings are low-productivity anyway
 - D) It keeps relationships strong for referral purposes

 **Why:** The lesson explicitly references Day 18's "shadow side" of 10X — grinding without recovery leads to burnout around Month 3, which ends the career before the compounding starts. One protected evening is a structural defence against that. Work-life balance is not framed as a policy (A). Low evening productivity (C) is not the argument made. And while relationships matter for referrals, that is not the reason given here (D) — the reason is sustainability of effort over 12+ months.
