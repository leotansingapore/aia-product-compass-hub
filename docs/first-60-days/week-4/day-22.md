# Day 22 — Productivity Principles: Schedule Your Priorities

> **The one idea for today:** "The key is not to prioritize what's on your schedule — it's to schedule your priorities." (Stephen Covey.) Most new FCs have priorities in theory. Top producers have priorities on the calendar.

## What you'll walk away with

By the end of today you should be able to:

1. **Distinguish** strategic priority-setting (this lesson) from tactical productivity (Day 20).
2. **Plan** your week, not just your day — using a weekly rhythm.
3. **Install** a "done over perfect" default for drafts, proposals, and content.

---

## 1. Yesterday's lesson, today's lesson

Day 20 was tactical: **how to protect your peak hour, batch tasks, and kill procrastination moment-by-moment.**

Today is strategic: **how to decide what actually matters in the next 7 days, 30 days, 90 days** — so that when you sit down in your peak hour, you're working on the *right thing.*

Both layers are needed. Great tactics on wrong priorities is just fast-failing.

## 2. The weekly planning ritual

Most new FCs plan day-by-day. Top producers plan week-by-week, review weekly, and adjust.

**Sunday evening, 30 minutes:**

1. **Review last week.**
 - What activity did you do? (Calls, meetings, content, study.)
 - What did you close? What's in pipeline?
 - Where did you leak time?

2. **Decide next week's 3 priorities.**
 - Not 10. Three.
 - Make them specific and measurable.
 - Example: "15 outreach calls Monday–Wednesday. 2 client proposals written by Thursday. Post 1 LinkedIn article by Friday."

3. **Block the calendar first.**
 - Peak-hour blocks (60–90 min each morning).
 - Meeting blocks.
 - Sunday review slot next week.
 - **Then** fit in everything else.

4. **Protect one evening for life.**
 - Friday dinner, Saturday activity, or Sunday family time. Non-negotiable.
 - Without this, you burn out in Month 3. (Day 18's "shadow side" of 10X.)

30 minutes on Sunday protects 168 hours of the coming week.

## 3. Schedule priorities, don't prioritise your schedule

This is the Covey reframe, and it matters.

**Wrong approach:** look at your calendar Monday morning. See what's there. Decide what to do based on what's already scheduled.

**Right approach:** decide what the most important 3 things are. **Put them on the calendar.** Defend those blocks. Everything else fights for the remaining time.

Most new FCs arrive at Monday with:
- A client meeting someone else put on the calendar.
- Training sessions someone else scheduled.
- No time carved out for prospecting.
- Realising by Wednesday that they've done no prospecting.

The fix: **prospecting goes on the calendar first, before anyone else's requests.** It's the revenue engine. Everything else bends around it.

## 4. Done is better than perfect

Perfectionism is procrastination wearing a lab coat.

**Where new FCs lose weeks to perfectionism:**

- **Proposals** — re-formatting the deck 7 times before sending.
- **Public content** — drafting a LinkedIn post for 5 days before not posting it.
- **Cold call scripts** — rewriting the opener until it's "just right" (it never is).
- **Bio / profile / LinkedIn page** — tweaking headshots and headers for 3 weekends.
- **CRM / tools / systems** — re-organising Notion/Asana workspaces for 4 hours.

None of those activities produce income. They produce the *feeling* of productivity without the substance.

### The 80% rule

For almost everything, **80% polish in 20% of the time beats 100% polish in 100% of the time.** The last 20% of polish consumes 80% of the effort and rarely changes outcomes.

**Operational tests:**

- **Proposal:** is it factually correct, professionally formatted, and on-brand? Send.
- **LinkedIn post:** does it have one clear idea, no typos, a specific CTA? Post.
- **Cold call script:** does it make the first 30 seconds useful? Use it for 20 calls, then iterate.

## 5. Minimum Viable Product (MVP) thinking for FCs

MVP is a software-world concept. It translates directly to our work.

| Traditional (perfectionist) | MVP (velocity-first) |
|---|---|
| Build perfect proposal template. Launch in month 3. | Write a rough proposal. Send 5 of them. Refine based on client reactions. |
| Spend 2 weeks perfecting LinkedIn presence. | Post your first piece this Sunday. Iterate weekly. |
| Memorise entire product suite before first meeting. | Learn top 3 products. Run 5 meetings. Study what came up. |
| Build elaborate prospecting spreadsheet. | Start with Notion notes. Upgrade when the system visibly breaks. |

**The principle:** get to a real user (client, prospect, audience) with something rough. Let real feedback shape the refinement. Don't refine in a vacuum.

## 6. Overcoming perfectionism — the 3-question filter

When you catch yourself polishing, ask:

1. **"Is this improving the client outcome, or soothing my anxiety?"**
 Most polishing is self-soothing.

2. **"Would my mentor say this is ready to ship?"**
 If yes, ship. If you don't know, ask quickly.

3. **"What's the cost of delaying by a day?"**
 Usually zero. Sometimes the cost is a lost client.

If perfection isn't serving the client, it's serving your ego. Ship anyway.

## 7. The resistance insight

> "The problem is not doing the work but just starting the work. Resistance provides the beating heart of procrastination."

Most days, the hardest part is **the first 10 minutes.** Once you're into a task, flow carries you.

So the tactical move: **lower the activation energy.**

- Don't schedule "write proposal." Schedule "open the proposal doc and write for 10 minutes."
- Don't schedule "make calls." Schedule "dial the first number on the list."
- Don't schedule "post on LinkedIn." Schedule "write the first sentence."

Start stupid small. Momentum handles the rest.
