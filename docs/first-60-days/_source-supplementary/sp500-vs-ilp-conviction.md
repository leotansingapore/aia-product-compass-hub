# Why You Should Not Just Invest in S&P 500 — FC Conviction Reference

**How to use this doc:** Not a script. This is your *internal conviction* reference. Every FC in the field has heard "why not just S&P 500 / robo-advisor / Endowus / VWRA?" from a prospect. This page collects the eight counter-points and the data behind each one so you stop hesitating mid-answer. Internalise the numbers, then deliver them casually when the moment comes.

> **Caveat up front:** the S&P 500 is a fine investment. The argument here is not *S&P 500 is bad*. The argument is *S&P 500 alone, held directly by a Singaporean through a robo-advisor, carries a stack of costs and risks that a structured AIA ILP largely removes*. Concentration, currency, tax, estate, execution. Each one is a small tax on wealth. Together they compound into a big one.

## 1. Dividend Withholding Tax — the 30% you didn't know you paid

US-domiciled ETFs (VOO, SPY, VTI etc.) → **30% withholding tax** on dividends, deducted at source before the cash reaches your brokerage.

Irish-domiciled equivalents (CSPX, VWRA, VUAA) → **15% withholding tax** (tax-treaty rate). Still not zero.

AIA ILP Elite funds → hedging and fund structure mean **no client-level dividend withholding tax**. Every dollar of distribution goes into the policy.

### The compounded cost

On a $2.8M retirement portfolio yielding ~6.3% in dividends = ~$14,900/month ≈ **~$3.6M total dividends over 20 retirement years**.

- US-domiciled S&P 500 (30% DWT): ~**$1.08M lost to withholding**.
- Irish-domiciled (15% DWT): ~**$540K lost** — half as bad, still real.
- AIA ILP structure (0% at client level): **full $3.6M reaches the policy**.

**The one-liner:** *"The S&P 500 itself might be a great index. But the way most Singaporeans buy it — through a US-domiciled ETF on a robo app — means the US government takes 30 cents of every dividend dollar before it ever reaches you. Over 20 years of retirement income, that's a million dollars going to the IRS instead of to you."*

## 2. Currency Risk — USD/SGD is not a constant

The S&P 500 is priced in USD. A Singaporean's retirement is spent in SGD. Every conversion is a new exposure.

### The data

- **5-year USD/SGD return: −3.90%** (Feb 2020 → Feb 2025, per the provided chart).
- Range: 1.28046 (low) to 1.45686 (high) over that window.
- **DBS 2025 forecast:** Singapore GDP to double by 2040, and SGD to reach **parity with USD** by 2040 — meaning SGD strengthens roughly 35% against USD over 15 years.

If that forecast is even half right, a USD-denominated S&P portfolio loses ~17% in purchasing power on conversion, *in addition* to whatever market return it produces.

### The macro forces underneath

Three structural pressures on USD (none a prediction, all a cluster of concerns to name for the client):

1. **COVID-era monetary expansion.** Unprecedented Fed balance-sheet growth → classic supply-and-demand pressure on currency value.
2. **US national debt trajectory.** Interest payments consuming an increasing share of the federal budget → erodes confidence in the currency as a long-term store of value.
3. **De-dollarisation momentum.** BRICS settlement alternatives, growing cross-border crypto adoption, multi-polar reserve-asset diversification by central banks — all reduce the structural demand that props the dollar up.

### The one-liner

*"Your retirement is in SGD. Your lifestyle is in SGD. Every time you pull from a USD portfolio, you're rolling the dice on the exchange rate. DBS is forecasting SGD parity with USD by 2040 — that's a 35% headwind on a USD portfolio alone, before we even talk about market returns."*

The AIA ILP structure is SGD-denominated with currency hedging in the fund architecture — the FX lottery is removed.

## 3. US Estate Tax — the 40% the family finds out about too late

**The rule most Singaporeans don't know:** US-situs assets held by non-US persons are subject to US estate tax on death.

### The numbers

- **Exemption:** $60,000 for non-US persons. (Contrast: $13M+ for US citizens. Not a typo.)
- **Top marginal rate:** 40% on the portion above $1M.
- Progressive schedule below:

| Taxable amount (value above exemption) | Rate |
|---|---|
| $0 to $10,000 | 18% |
| $10,001 to $20,000 | 20% |
| $20,001 to $40,000 | 22% |
| $40,001 to $60,000 | 24% |
| $60,001 to $80,000 | 26% |
| $80,001 to $100,000 | 28% |
| $100,001 to $150,000 | 30% |
| $150,001 to $250,000 | 32% |
| $250,001 to $500,000 | 34% |
| $500,001 to $750,000 | 37% |
| $750,001 to $1,000,000 | 39% |
| **Over $1,000,000** | **40%** |

*(Source: IRS, via Bloomberg / Jimmy Sexton estate-tax schedule.)*

### What this looks like on a real portfolio

US-situs assets of **$2.8M** held by a Singaporean who passes away:

- Exemption: $60K.
- Taxable: $2,740,000.
- Estate tax owed: approximately **$1.05M** (top marginal 40% on the bulk, lower brackets on the slice above $60K).

Over $1M disappearing before the family sees a cent. Most Singaporeans holding VOO / VTI / SPY directly through a US broker have no idea this applies to them.

### Partial mitigations (not elimination)

- **Irish-domiciled ETFs** (CSPX, VWRA): Ireland is the *fund domicile*, but the underlying holdings are still US equities. Irish-domiciled funds generally avoid US estate tax exposure because the investor owns shares of an Irish corporation, not US-situs securities. This is the half-solution most sophisticated Singaporean DIYers use.
- **AIA ILP with proper nomination:** the policy structure means US estate tax does not apply to the policy value, and proceeds pass through nomination rather than probate.

### The one-liner

*"If you hold US assets directly and you're not a US citizen, your family pays up to 40% estate tax on everything above $60K. On a $2.8M retirement portfolio, that's a million dollars the IRS takes before your kids see a cent. Ireland-domiciled funds fix most of it. An AIA ILP with proper nomination removes it entirely."*

The recency bias is real: the S&P 500 has been the best-performing major index for roughly 15 years. That doesn't mean it *will be*. History has plenty of 10-year stretches where it wasn't.

|---|---|
| **S&P 500** | **227%** |
| US Large Cap Value | 166% |
| PVP Equity | 144% |
| US Small Cap Core | 137% |
| US Small Cap Value | 126% |

| Int'l Small Cap Core | 77% |
| Int'l Large Cap Value | 65% |
| Int'l Large Cap Core | 51% |
| Int'l Emerging Markets | 20% |

S&P 500 was the winner of this cycle. No argument. But every prior cycle had a different winner.

### How to use this data

**Don't argue the last decade with the client.** Argue the *structure*: whichever index leads the next decade, a globally diversified portfolio owns it by default. A concentrated S&P portfolio only owns it if the US happens to lead again — which it sometimes does and sometimes does not.

*"Most investors look at the last 10 years of S&P returns and think that's the default. But what you're seeing is the best-performing cycle. The next cycle might belong to emerging markets, or international value, or something that hasn't even shown up yet. A global portfolio owns whichever one wins — without you having to guess."*

## 7. Execution Risk — does your family know what to sell?

The argument nobody makes but everyone will face.

**Scenario:** you die at 68. Your $2M is sitting in a robo-advisor app (Endowus, StashAway, Syfe, SaxoTraderGo). Your spouse has never logged in. Your kids don't know the password.

- Who decides when to sell?
- Who files the US tax paperwork to claim the estate-tax exemption?
- Who handles the FX conversion from USD back to SGD?
- Who manages drawdown so the surviving spouse doesn't run out?

This is a real planning gap. Robo-advisors solve the *investing* problem and leave the *execution on death* problem entirely to the family. Most families are not equipped.

### What an AIA ILP with proper nomination provides instead

- **Named beneficiaries** — no probate delay. Funds flow to the right people automatically.
- **Secondary insured** — the policy continues on the next life assured, so the investment can keep compounding for the spouse or the children.
- **An advisor of record** — someone the family can call who already knows the plan, already knows the tax structure, and already has a relationship with the fund.
- **Estate and will coordination** — the FC can loop in an estate planner / lawyer before death, so the handover is already rehearsed.

### The one-liner

*"The investing part is the easy part. The hard part is 20 years from now when something happens to you, and your family is looking at a locked app they've never used and asking themselves when to sell. AIA puts an advisor on the phone and a policy with named beneficiaries in place of that. That's the difference."*

## 8. Secondary Insured — the legacy mechanic most clients don't understand

**What it is:** an ILP feature that names a second life assured who automatically becomes the policyholder when the original life assured dies. The plan does not terminate — it continues.

### The mechanics most FCs skip

- The secondary insured is named **at policy inception** (or added later, subject to underwriting).
- When the original life assured dies, **the policy does not terminate** and **no death benefit is paid at the first death** — the plan continues on the secondary insured's life.
- All accumulated units, dividends, and bonuses continue. Fund selection continues. The policy outlives the first person.
- Premiums may continue or be pre-funded depending on structure.

### Why this matters for conviction

Without secondary insured, the math on an ILP looks like "40 years of compounding". **With** secondary insured, the math becomes "80-100 years of compounding", because the plan runs through two generations.

**On the ProAchiever bonus example:** $960/year perpetual bonus held from year 20 × ~80 more years (across parent + child) = ~$77K of bonuses alone, on top of the core investment growth. This is why bonuses are not a 60-year story — they're a 100-year story.

### The one-liner

*"On a DIY portfolio, when you die, the portfolio is liquidated and passed to your heirs as cash. On this plan, when you die, the plan doesn't end — it rolls to your child as the secondary insured, and the compounding keeps going. That's not a trick — it's how the structure was designed. The policy outlives you."*
