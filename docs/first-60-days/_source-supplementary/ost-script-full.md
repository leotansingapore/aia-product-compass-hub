## Page 1 — Risk Management & The Pyramid

### Opening — what financial planning is

> "NAME, my role as a Financial Planner is to help my clients with financial planning, and what does financial planning entail? It entails two things: first of all, it's to **protect**, secondly, it's to **grow**. What are we protecting and growing? **(1) your existing wealth and (2) your future potential wealth.**"

You can't just do protection without growing wealth (cash subjected to inflation). You can't just pool everything into savings/investments without insurance (one mishap brings it all to zero or negative).

### The four financial instruments

There are 4 most relevant to this prospect right now:

1. **Insurance** (covered first below)
2. **Savings**
3. **Investments**
4. **Wealth Management** — usually not relevant yet for the prospect; comes later

> "Wealth Management might not be very relevant to look at, hence for you it will be mainly 1, 2 and 3."

### The savings vs investments difference

> "What do you think is the main difference between savings and investments?"

(Pause. Wait for them. Acknowledge.)

> "The most fundamental difference is that **savings have lower risks but lower returns**, while **investments carry higher risk but potentially higher returns**. Most people have a combination of both throughout their lives."

### Singapore inflation

> "For the purpose of this discussion, can we agree that inflation in Singapore is about 2-4%? Let's take an average — say 3%. So inflation will eat into your returns if you leave it in the bank as liquid cash, right?"

### The pyramid (3 levels)

> "My job as a financial planner is to help my clients build their **financial pyramid**. When we build a pyramid, it has 3 levels — Risk Management, Wealth Accumulation, Wealth Management. For your pyramid to be most stable, where should you build it from? (Pause) Yes, the base. Do you expect to build this overnight? No right? Hence, my job is to work with you to help you build this pyramid wider and taller over the years."

### Risk Management — the risk hierarchy

There are several kinds of risk we face in our lives:

| Risk size | Examples | Cost | Solution |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Small** | Cough, flu, fever, headache | $30–$100 (GP / telemedicine) | Pay on your own |
| **Medium** | Job loss / between jobs | A few thousand / few months income | **3–6 months expenses as liquid cash** (emergency fund) |
| **Major (5+1)** | Premature death, disability, critical illness, accident, hospitalisation, **+ retirement** | Tens to hundreds of thousands | **Insurance for 1–5; planned wealth accumulation for 6** |

### The "important AND urgent vs important NOT urgent" trap

> "Numbers 1–5 — do you think they are important and urgent? If they are important and urgent, they have to be handled ASAP, agree? What about number 6 (retirement) — is it important and urgent? If it's not urgent, what do you think most people will do? (Pause) That's right — they procrastinate. Do you see a lot of Singaporeans in their 40s and 50s that simply do not have enough to retire? It's too late to start. The reason they're struggling is because they didn't start while they were in their 20s. Which is exactly what we want to avoid here."

### Three risk management techniques

> "If there are certain risks in life, there must be certain techniques to mitigate them."

| Technique | What it means | Realistic? |
|---|---|---|
| **Avoid** | Avoid the risk (don't cross roads, don't take planes) | **Unrealistic** for most major risks |
| **Retain (self-insure)** | Use your own savings to fund the loss | Not realistic — a CI requires $200K+, will eat your retirement, parents shouldn't have to pay |
| **Transfer** | Pay premiums to transfer risk to an insurer | ✅ **What most people do for major risks** |

> "Insurance is nothing but a **risk transfer tool**. There are many types of insurance in the market, but there are three basic ones that everyone requires: **(1) Life Insurance, (2) Hospitalisation Plan, (3) Personal Accident Plan**. Have you heard of them before?"

### Life Insurance — the middle of the base level

> "It is the **most important policy in your entire portfolio**. The moment you set aside say **$2-3K a year for life insurance, it covers you about 100x that amount**, against risk Number 1 and Number 3. It will cover you a little for Number 2, disability, but may not be sufficient. So mostly for premature death and major illnesses."

> "Let's say you hold the plan for a long time and nothing happens to you, you are still healthy at 60+ years old. What do you think happens to the plan? Will it be gone or will it be worth a sum of money?"

> "In the future, if all is still well, your policy will still be worth quite a lot of money. There will be returns to it that will be enough to cover for inflation. It can help you **partially with your retirement**, or you can leave the money behind for subsequent generations. Hence, **it is the only policy in the world that I know protects a person from contracting a CI and dying too young, AS WELL AS living too long.** That is the dual purpose of a life insurance plan."

### Hospital Plan vs Personal Accident — the "expenses vs loss of income" framing

This is the core of the page-1 sale.

> "When you're doing your risk management portfolio, there are two things you should be concerned about: **Expenses and Loss of Income**."

| Plan | Covers |
|---|---|
| **Hospitalisation plan** | **Expenses** — hospital bills (50K bill → covered, 500K bill → covered) |
| **Life / CI plan** | **Loss of income** — pays YOU when you can't work |

> "Loss of income is what people are blindsided by."

### Worked example — CI hits a 45K-income earner

> "NAME as a [JOB], what is your income? (Pause, ANSWER) Multiply by 12 months — that's $X, and with the 13th month bonus that'll be around $Y."

> "Let's take a CI that may cause $50K or even $100K of hospitalisation bills. NAME, do you know for a person suffering from major CI in Singapore, how long will they be out of work for? (Pause)
> 
> If it's cancer — they may be treated and back to work in **1–1.5 years**. If it's a major stroke — they may be out for **5–10 years**. **On average in Singapore it's 3.5 years.** Round it down to 3 years. With your annual income at $X, 3 years would be $3X worth of income lost."

> "So would a 50–100K hospital bill be more painful, or a $135K loss of income be more painful? Obviously the loss of income, right? But people only see the expenses because expenses are upfront. They don't see that they lost their income over a period of time. Imagine the person tightens the belt, they will still be able to pay for the medical expenses. **It is the lack of income that kills people financially.**"

### Personal Accident — the gap-filler

The PA plan covers what hospital + life plans don't:

| Scenario | Hospital | Life | PA |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hospitalised for CI / accident | ✅ Pays the bills | Pays you (if CI/death/TPD) | (varies) |
| **Minor sprain — X-ray + MRI + physio (3-4K, no hospital admission)** | ❌ Zero | ❌ Zero | **✅ Covers** |
| **Lose 1 limb / 1 eye** | (depends) | ❌ Only TPD = 2 limbs/2 eyes | **✅ Covers** |

> "What if I, as a Financial Planner, get into an accident and lose one of my arms? How much would I be able to claim from my life insurance? **The answer is zero.** But if that happens, I would probably lose a lot of income in my lifetime — I'll do things slower, people may not trust me, some clients may drop me. Where do you think I will be claiming a few hundred K from? **The accident plan, right.**"

### Page 1 wrap

> "In summary, NAME, these 3 plans help cover all the risks you may face in your life. Is that clear? In fact, I am done with risk management. Are there any questions?"

## Page 1.5 — Wealth Accumulation

### Two ways to grow wealth

| Approach | Description |
|---|---|
| **Savings** | Lower risk, lower return |
| **Investments** | Higher risk, higher return |

> "Some people think a third way is putting money in Singapore Pools, RWS, or MBS. They think that's their form of investments. Beginner's luck very often exists in gambling — you'll make some money in the beginning, but in the long term the odds are not in your favour."

### Short-term savings — the "won't beat inflation" reality

| Where | Typical return |
|---|---|
| Bank savings | ~0.05% |
| High-yield account (UOB One, OCBC 360) | ~1+% |
| Fixed deposit (3–5 yr) | 1–2% |
| **Inflation** | **~3%** |

> "All of these still don't beat inflation. So you have to channel some to long-term savings."

### Long-term savings — endowment vs annuity

| Type | What it does | Typical setup |
|---|---|---|
| **Endowment** (savings plan) | Set aside a few hundred/month, choose 10/20/25-year tenure. After maturity, get lump sum back. | $300–500/month, lump sum at 10–25 years |
| **Annuity** (retirement income) | Same monthly contribution, choose retirement age (55/60/65). Plan pays you monthly income for a number of years. | Mostly done in late 30s+, often as 6-figure lump sum |

> "Generally they will give you at best around **3–3.5% per annum** of return over the long run."

### Investments — active vs passive

For people who invest, only **5–10% will actually do it themselves.** 90–95% outsource to professionals.

To invest **actively** on your own, you need 4 things:

1. **Time**
2. **Knowledge**
3. **Expertise**
4. **Capital**

> "Without any of these 4, what are you doing? You're not investing — you're gambling. And you and I know that will lead to negative returns in the long run."

For **passive investing**, you only need **capital**. The other 3 are outsourced to professionals (fund managers).

| Approach | When | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Lump sum investing** | Risky — $20K can grow to $25–30K but can also drop to $10–15K | Rarely recommended when starting |
| **Regular investing ($300–500/month)** | Compounds over time, lower risk because no single entry point | **Most recommended for new earners** |

### The anti-clockwise progression

> "Most people go in an anti-clockwise direction:
> 1. Money in the bank → realise it's not the best way to grow
> 2. Buy a savings/endowment plan → after a while feel it's too slow
> 3. Move to passive investments → most people stop here
> 4. Active investments → only after 20–30 years of working, with time and capital available"

> "But right now, that's not what you'll be looking at, am I right?"

## Page 2 — How Much to Cover

These are not cast in stone — they're useful as a gauge for fresh graduates.

### Coverage uses GROSS income (not take-home)

> "Coverage is based on **gross income** — before deducting CPF. Is CPF still your own money? Yes. Hence it makes sense to also cover for it. But for **budgeting**, we use take-home pay."

### The three coverage benchmarks

| Risk | Benchmark | Worked example ($45K/year earner) |
|---|---|---|
| **Premature death (Risk 1)** | **10× annual income** | Don't be 100K (too little). Aim 400–500K. **Full lifetime value: $45K × 40 yrs = $1.8M** (the ideal) |
| **Critical Illness (Risk 3)** | **$100K + 3–5 years of annual income** | $100K (buffer) + $45K × 3 = **$235K**. 200–250K OK. 100K too low. 500K excessive. |
| **Hospitalisation (Risk 5)** | Based on hospital/ward preference | Lock in early — can't upgrade later if a medical condition emerges |

### Why the $100K CI buffer

> "Whether you are rich or poor, you will always need a buffer of $100K. Imagine a person down with a CI — will there be a need for **home care and nursing** that may not be covered by the hospital plan? Can you expect a person with a stroke or cancer to always take the bus and MRT? They may have to buy a second-hand car or take grab/taxi often. So that will cost a lot of money."

> "Will there be a possible **loss of income to a family member**? A family member may quit their job, or change to a part-time job to take care of the person who is down. Hence, it's good to have $100K as a buffer."

### Why "1.8M lifetime value" matters

> "If I ask you to cover yourself for $1.8 million today, does it sound very daunting? It sounds too big right? But logically, can we agree this is the **minimum value of what your life is actually worth**? Of course we cannot put a value to a human life — but if we have to, this is the minimum in today's dollar."

For a start, may not cover the full $1.8M. **10× income** (~$450K) is the realistic target.

## Page 2.5 — Budget: The 1/3 Rule

> "How you budget depends on your cash flow management. We look at your **take home pay** to be fair. NAME, you mentioned your gross income is $X correct? So your take-home will be $Y."

> "NAME, have you heard of the **1/3 rule**? Something my team and I came up with — it has helped a lot of our clients."

### The rule itself

| Bucket | What | When |
|---|---|---|
| **Short-term needs** | Daily expenses (food, transport, parents, bills) | Spending now |
| **Medium-term needs** | House, car, holiday, wedding | 2–3 years out |
| **Long-term needs** | Insurance + wealth accumulation | Decades out (retirement) |

### The two mistakes most people make

> "**Mistake 1: Most people forgo the long term.** Because if I don't set aside money for retirement today, how does it affect me 10 years down the road? It wouldn't! In fact, I would have more money to spend today."

> "**Mistake 2: Asians (especially Singaporeans) hold too much cash.** Usually after I know how much my clients are holding as cash, I'll ask what it's for. They'll say a certain proportion is for upcoming big-ticket items (wedding, housing, emergency funds) — those are fine. But the remaining? They'll say it's for long-term retirement. For money you plan to set aside for long-term, do you think you should be holding it as cash? **Definitely not** — it should be channelled to other instruments earning higher returns."

### Worked example — $3.5K gross / $2.8K take-home

| Category | Item | Amount |
|---|---|---|
| **Short-term** | Parents (10% take-home) | $300 |
| | Food ($10/day × 20 work days = $200, weekends/dates $200) | $400 |
| | Transport (incl. ~$50 grab) | $150 |
| | Entertainment (gym, hobbies, drinks) | $200 |
| | Phone bill | $50 |
| | **Short-term subtotal** | **$1,100** |
| **Cash remaining** | | **$1,700** |
| **Medium-term** | House / car / 2–3 holidays/yr (~$2K × 2 trips = $4K/yr) | Funded by **bonuses**, not monthly cashflow |
| **Long-term — risk mgmt** | Aim for ~10% of take-home (10–15% range) | **~$300** |
| **Long-term — wealth accum** | Aim for ~20% of take-home | **~$500/month** |
| **Excess cash** | After all of above | ~$900/month → ~$11K/year |

> "Your $20K cash you're holding on to → that's your medium-term + emergency. Bonuses cover holidays."

### The "retirement is the most expensive purchase" anchor

> "NAME, what's the most expensive thing you'll need to fund in your lifetime? (Pause) It's a phase of your life — it starts with R. Yes, **retirement**. Retirement is going to cost more than your property, agree? And who's going to pay for it? Yourself."

> "If it's the most expensive thing in your life, when would be the best time to start? **The best time to start was from your first paycheck. The second best time is now.**"

> "If you set aside just 5% of your income — will you ever be able to retire? No. 10%? Possible if you can earn very high returns. **20%? Sounds about right. 25%? Bit of a stretch.** **20% of take-home at 4–6% return per year is the magic number for comfortable retirement.** As your income increases, maintain at 20%."

### The final budget commitment

> "For risk management, can we do around $300, ok? In the event I cannot keep within $300 and need a bit more, how much can I stretch it to? Okay can. I promise to keep it within this range."

> "Secondly, in terms of wealth accumulation — how much are you willing to set aside each month? After RM, you are left with $1,400. A portion goes towards increasing your cash, a portion goes towards long-term savings/investments. So how much can you set aside? OK, $500/month. So if I were to work with $500, you'll be left with $900/month — about $11,000 excess cash each year. Is that enough for you?"

> "The risk management part you leave to me. The wealth accumulation part — there are 3 options. Could you let me know which is best for you? If not, choose 2 options."

| Option | Wealth allocation |
|---|---|
| **1** | Full $500/month into endowment plan |
| **2** | Full $500/month into investment plan |
| **3** | Split — 50/50 between endowment and investment |

> "Personally, is there 1 you'd prefer over the rest? So in the middle? Okay, I will do 50/50 for you."

### Closing the meeting

> "NAME, this is all I have to do for you today. Do you have any questions? What I will do now is to go back and do up a proposal based on what we discussed. I will then go through the proposal with you — let's set a time **about 2 weeks from now** (set next appointment)."

> "Oh yes, can you help me to write down your full name as in NRIC, as well as your date of birth, in this blank space here? (Top of last section.)"

### The decision-roadblock check

> "Let's say if I come back to you with a proposal that meets all your needs and within the budget we've set aside, would there be any potential roadblocks with you going ahead with implementing the portfolio? So you are able to decide everything on your own right? Okay great! That's what I needed to understand."

(Then: ask for referrals if time permits, and/or end the appointment.)

## Note to advisors — 10 delivery rules

1. **Take a picture of the last section, hand them the paper.** Then ask them before the next session to recap and bring this paper along.
2. **Point accurately** — especially to where exactly you want them to pay attention to, and the answers to your questions.
3. **Eye contact >80%.**
4. **How you say it is more important than what you say.**
5. **3 steps to effectively master a script: memorise → internalise → personalise.** Don't skip any step or it will backfire.
6. **Should be a 2-way conversation** as much as possible.
7. **Ask questions whenever possible.**
8. **Master it 100% first**, then you'll know where to elaborate and where to cut short in an actual setting.
9. **Whole thing should not take more than 45 minutes**, unless there are major objections to handle.
10. **Talk a little faster and louder, don't be monotonous.** Being upbeat and high-energy creates a sense of urgency and confidence for the prospect.
