# Assignment 7 — 100 Whys: The Real Reason You're Here

> **What you're producing:** A list of 100 written reasons why you are doing this work. Then the one sentence that compresses the real ones into your why. The exercise sounds simple. It is not.

## Why this matters

Day 5 told you that purpose is the load-bearing wall — the thing that keeps you in the chair when discipline runs out and financial pressure cuts both ways. The problem is that almost no new FC actually *knows* their why on Day 5. They know a slogan. The slogan won't survive Month 4.

This assignment exists because **the real why is buried under the obvious ones**. You have to write past the surface — past *"financial freedom,"* past *"I want flexibility,"* past *"my parents would be proud"* — to find the reasons that are actually load-bearing. The only known way to do that is to keep writing until your obvious reasons run out, and then keep writing some more.

By why #20 you'll feel done. You're not. The first 20 are what you tell people at parties. By why #50 you'll feel stuck. That stuck feeling is the point — it's where the conscious answers stop and the honest ones start. By why #80 the page changes character. The reasons get specific. They get personal. They get harder to write down. **Those are the ones that will still be standing in Year 5.**

## How to run the exercise

### 1. Set the conditions

This is not a fill-in-the-blank task. It needs **one uninterrupted sitting**, ideally 60 to 90 minutes. Phone face down. No notifications. No music with words. A door that closes.

A pen and paper version works best — typing makes the whys shorter and more polished, which is the opposite of what you want. If you must type, type fast and ugly.

### 2. Number the page 1 to 100

Before you write anything, number every line. Seeing all 100 slots staring back at you is part of the work. It tells your brain *we are not finishing this in five reasons.*

### 3. Write

One why per line. Rules:

- **Don't censor.** If a why feels embarrassing, write it. If it feels selfish, write it. If it contradicts an earlier one, write it. Editing is for next week — not now.
- **Repeat-with-variation is allowed.** *"To prove I can do hard things"* and *"To prove to my dad specifically that I can do hard things"* are two different whys. Specificity is the point.
- **No skipping numbers.** If you're stuck at #38, sit with the silence. The why that comes after the silence is usually the one worth keeping.
- **Speed beats polish.** Three-word whys are fine. *"To not be broke at 60."* counts.

### 4. Push past the wall

Most people hit a wall around #25 to #35. The wall is the entire point of the assignment. Three things to do at the wall:

- **Look back at the last 10.** Anything you'd write again? Don't repeat — go *deeper* on the same theme.
- **Switch frame.** If you've been writing about money, switch to people. If you've been writing about people, switch to identity. If you've been writing about today, switch to who you'll be at 65.
- **Ask the harder version.** Not *"why this career?"* but *"what would I have to give up if I quit this and went back to a salaried job?"* — the inverse question often unblocks 20 more lines.

### 5. Distil

When all 100 are on the page, read them top to bottom. Two highlighters (or two pen colours):

- One colour for the whys that **actually move you** when you read them now.
- A second colour for the whys that look like the right answer but feel hollow.

Then write **one sentence** that captures the highlighted ones. This sentence becomes your draft why. It will change in Year 5. It still has to exist now.

## What good looks like

A passing 100 Whys looks like this:

- The first 20 are short, obvious, and a bit performative. *"Financial freedom." "More time with my kids."*
- The middle 40 get specific and uneven. Some are sharp, some are clumsy. *"I don't want my mum to ever feel guilty about her medical bills." "I want to outwork the version of me from my old job."*
- The last 40 read like someone telling the truth to themselves. They name people, fears, regrets, and futures. They are uncomfortable to share — which is why they are real.

If your last 40 sound exactly like your first 20, you stopped writing before the work started. Reset and try again.

## A note on the one-sentence why

The distilled sentence should be:

- **In your voice** — not corporate, not aspirational marketing.
- **About a change in the world** — not a personal status outcome. *"To close the financial literacy gap one household at a time"* > *"To become a top advisor."*
- **True even on a bad week** — if it stops being true the moment commissions dip, it's not a why.

This sentence is the **Why** layer of the Why → How → What structure from Day 5. Your *How* and *What* are easier to write once this sentence is honest.

## Submission

Fill in the form. Paste your full list of 100 whys (one per line). Write your distilled sentence. Answer the two reflection questions — they're as important as the list itself, because they show whether the exercise actually moved you.

## A reminder for the next 60 days

Keep the page (or its photo) somewhere you'll see it. Phone wallpaper. Laptop background. Notebook front cover. The work of this career is grinding enough that even a great why will fade if you never look at it again. The whys that survive are the ones you re-read.

Most FCs run this exercise once. The ones who run it again at month 6 and at year 1 are the ones whose career is still standing at year 5.
